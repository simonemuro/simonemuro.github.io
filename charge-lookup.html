Case No. 01 Charge lookup QuickBooks support

The page showed how much. Customers were calling to ask why.

Customers could find how much they were charged. Nowhere in the product, the articles, or the assistant could they find why. The only path to an explanation was a phone call. I redesigned the conversation so the question got understood and the help stayed in the channel.

Surface
The QuickBooks support assistant, chat and voice, subscription billing
Role
Conversational AI Manager, Conversational AI Experiences team · 2025 to 2026
Context
About 800,000 billing and subscription contacts a year; one of the top twenty contact drivers, inside an organization goal of reducing two million contacts
Results
Voice self-help acceptance 25% to 39%; chat self-help resolution up 12 points; chat containment about 61%

01Background

A two-million-contact goal, narrowed to one question.

I was a Conversational AI Manager on the Conversational AI Experiences team, inside the digital arm of the customer success organization. The larger organization had a goal of reducing two million contacts.

I worked cross functionally with the organization’s analysts to identify the top contact drivers. Billing and subscription was one of the top twenty. We kept going, breaking down the topics under that umbrella, using analytics and generative AI to hone in on the top questions customers asked about billing and subscriptions. Charge lookup was one of the high volume topics.

This case is about what happened when I typed those questions in myself.

02The problem

I walked the journey myself.

I typed the questions into the chat experience, the voice experience, the in-product search, and Google, to see what help customers actually got.

The self-help path went search bar, then articles, then instructions to open the Billing and Subscription page under Settings. You had to be the account administrator to see it at all. There you saw a breakdown of what you were subscribed to and how much each part cost. How much, but not why. A subscription that was forty nine dollars for the last three months is seventy this month, and the page shows the current amount with no explanation for the increase.

The articles also linked to an account lookup tool that lived outside the product and outside the help experience: enter the amount charged to your card and the date, get a breakdown of the charges. Still no why.

So customers who wanted an explanation eventually had to call support. Here is what the assistant itself gave them, recreated.

03Why it mattered

Calls that ended in downgrades and cancellations.

The supportability teams, the people taking the phone calls, confirmed it: customers called because they wanted an explanation. Some of those calls turned into downgrades or cancellations.

And billing and subscription calls transferred the most internally, tier two to tier three, because customers asked for discount extensions or refunds that only a higher tier could approve. The most expensive kind of call, on one of the highest volume topics: about 800,000 billing and subscription support contacts a year.

04Exploring solutions

Transcripts, and how the experts actually did it.

I interviewed the supportability teams and pulled real transcripts: phone conversations with experts, and the live support chat channels, not the AI chat. Partly to validate why customers called. Mostly to learn how the experts actually handled it: what solutions they found, what questions they asked, what the outcomes were.

Two patterns came straight out of the transcripts. Customers were often caught off guard by a promotional period or discount ending, the subscription snapping back to regular price. And payroll charges confused people: a base subscription plus six dollars for each employee added, so adding employees made the amount go up.

The insight, stated plainly: the digital experiences were not personalized, and articles and instructions were not solving the question. To help, the conversational experience had to go beyond self-help articles, toward personalized information and explainability about charges.

05Design decisions

Understand the question, keep them in the journey.

Three decisions define the redesign. Each one is visible in the shipped conversation in part seven.

  1. 5.1

    Disambiguate the question, not just the topic.

    A price increase, a double charge or a perceived double charge, a charge they do not understand, and simply how much am I paying are different questions, and they need different responses. The assistant had to figure out which one it was hearing before it answered.

  2. 5.2

    Keep the customer in the help journey.

    Stop linking self-help articles. The lookup tool already existed; the redesign offered it inside the assistant window instead of sending the customer outside to do it alone.

  3. 5.3

    Go hybrid: NLU plus a language model.

    The platform was NLU based, and NLU intents struggle with nuance: one billing intent cannot tell a lookup from an explanation from a how-much question. So the NLU processes the initial question, and a language model classifies the exact type and puts the customer in the right bucket.

06The build

A phased PRD, because APIs take time.

Full personalization needed the assistant connected to billing APIs: billing and payment history, amounts, promotional periods, discounts and their dates. Data a system could read and reason about.

From experience I knew API onboarding is a lengthy process. Identify the owners, find out whether the APIs even exist, work out how to onboard them into the chat system. So I wrote a PRD, defined the MVP, and planned the rollout in phases, MVP then v1, v2, v3, improving the existing experience with the minimum required capabilities while the APIs onboarded toward the target state.

The content strategy came together with the conversation designers: both the voice assistant and chat offer the charge lookup tool with engaging language. The voice assistant switched from offering a self-help article to offering the tool, sent by text or email.

But we knew that to provide a real charge explanation and a personalized experience, we needed to onboard APIs and bring in the specific subscription charges for that account. That is when I hit a roadblock. At that time, teams at Intuit were building Intuit Intelligence, a unified central conversational experience that would live outside the help panel, center stage in the product, as the new chat experience customers would interact with. I did not get buy-in from teams to connect and build APIs into the platform I was building in, because that experience was moving to Intuit Intelligence.

We banked on the gains we already produced, and moved on to collaborating with the Intuit Intelligence teams, building agents to onboard the billing experience there.

The target state above later became a working build, as an agent skill for Intuit Intelligence. That is the next case: BillingSkill.

08Learnings

What I learned, and what I keep doing.

Walk in the customer’s shoes first. Typing the real questions into chat, voice, in-product search, and Google gave me the problem faster and more honestly than any dashboard. The whole journey showed how much; nowhere showed why.

The experts’ transcripts were design material, not just validation. The best answers the assistant could give were already being spoken on the phone. The transcripts showed what questions the experts asked and what solutions worked, and I designed the responses closer to what the expert provided.

Phase the product around the API reality. API onboarding is lengthy, so the PRD shipped the minimum required capabilities immediately and let each phase absorb the data as it landed. The MVP moved the numbers while the harder work was still onboarding.

And know when to stop investing in a platform. When Intuit Intelligence made the old platform’s ending visible, the right move was not to fight for buy-in. We banked on the gains we already produced and went to build on the successor.
