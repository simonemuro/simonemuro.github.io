Response Quality at Scale Evaluation Systems Leadership

Turning "the AI feels off" into a number you can fix.

I drove pre-launch quality for Intuit Intelligence, QuickBooks' agentic assistant, partnering with data science and a team of designers: the golden datasets that define a good answer, the LLM-as-judge-plus-human-labeling program that scores real conversations, and the readouts that turn failures back into prompts, ground truth, and content fixes. It's continuous refinement of an LLM product's response quality, run as an operating system.

My role
Quality methodology lead · named pre-launch driver · team lead
Surface
Intuit Intelligence, agentic AI in QuickBooks
What I did
Golden datasets, ground truth, rubrics, LLM-judge plus human labeling, readouts
Scope
Several agent skills across the platform
The problem

You can't scale an assistant you can only judge by vibe.

As agentic AI scaled across QuickBooks, quality was being measured ad hoc. Teams leaned on LLM-as-judge (a model grading other models against golden datasets), which is useful but not sufficient on its own, especially for product and financial workflows where a wrong answer has real consequences.

The stakes were concrete. In executive testing sessions, leaders found the assistant "arrogant": it explained its own steps instead of just answering, over-narrated its thinking, and stopped short of taking action. Those aren't bugs in a single response; they're systemic response-quality problems. You can only catch and fix them with a rigorous, repeatable evaluation program, not spot checks.

My role

I drove the pre-launch quality function, then ran the whole thing when it lost its lead.

  • Named the pre-launch quality driver in the team's official scope of work: building, expanding, and managing the golden datasets that define ground truth for the AI.
  • Standardized golden-dataset intake across skills with a common schema, and authored the source-of-truth answers, expected actions, and diagnostic paths.
  • Designed the audit framework that validates the validators: hide the experts' original ground truth, have designers independently keep-or-cut it, and reconcile the disagreements.
  • Set the evaluation criteria (correctness, completeness, relevance, routing accuracy, and conversation-quality scoring) and the multi-turn user-simulator instructions behind them.
  • Delivered the readouts that turned scores into prioritized fixes for prompts, ground truth, tools, or support content, then re-tested.
  • Stepped in to lead the full function when the peer who owned it went on sudden medical leave, keeping evaluation running across every skill without a pause.

"Proactively stepping up to fill the leadership vacuum in Quality."

What my manager said when I stepped in to cover the function
The quality loop

An operating system, not a dashboard.

The function I built runs as a continuous loop. Each lap turns the abstract question "is the assistant good enough?" into concrete signal: a frozen ground-truth dataset, an LLM judge that grades against it, human labelers that judge the judges, and an audit that routes failures back into prompts, ground truth, tools, or support content.

The key decision

Don't let LLMs be the only judge of LLMs.

The defining methodology call was to refuse a pure auto-eval shortcut. For financial workflows, I insisted on a loop that pairs LLM-as-judge with human labeling against frozen, versioned ground truth, and treats the experts' ground truth as something to audit, not assume.

The tradeoff is real: it's slower and more expensive than letting a model grade itself. But when the answer is about someone's payroll or taxes, "a model said it was fine" isn't a standard you can ship on. Human-in-the-loop is what makes the score trustworthy enough to gate a release.

  • Define the capability and the real customer scenarios it must handle.
  • Build a representative golden dataset; author source-of-truth answers and expected actions.
  • Judge offline with LLM-as-judge; sample production with trained human labelers.
  • Audit the failures and the disagreements between model and humans.
  • Fix and re-test: route issues to prompts, ground truth, tools, or content, then watch for drift.
The outcome

Quality became a lever, not a report.

The program drove measurable quality-score lifts across the skills it covered, from +8 to +13 percentage points, and, more importantly, changed what evaluation was for. It stopped being a backward-looking report and became the mechanism that decided whether agent behavior was good enough to ship, and what to fix when it wasn't.

What I'd carry forward

The rubric is the easy part. Calibration is the job.

The hardest, most valuable work wasn't writing the rubric. It was keeping models and human labelers calibrated to the same bar over time, and translating "the assistant feels arrogant" into specific, scoreable dimensions a team could actually move. I'd invest even earlier in that shared language, because evaluation is only as good as the agreement underneath it.

The transferable principle

Driving the behavior of AI models (through training practices, the content they learn from, and rigorous response-quality evaluation) is the muscle any team needs to keep an AI product helpful as it scales. This is that work, run as a function.
