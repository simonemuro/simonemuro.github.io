Case study 03 · AI quality at scale · Leadership · 2025–26

Making "good enough to ship" measurable for an agentic platform

As Intuit Intelligence scaled across QuickBooks, one judge model grading answers stopped being enough. Agents were taking actions in product and financial workflows, and "the answer sounded right" is not a quality bar for someone's books. I built core pieces of the discipline that made quality measurable — the judge-validation method, the blind ground-truth audit, the pre-population pipeline — and when the manager who ran it day to day left on sudden leave, I took over the whole program.

Role
Built and led the quality evaluation discipline; design manager
Context
Intuit Intelligence — the agentic AI platform across QuickBooks
Methods
Golden datasets · LLM-as-judge · human labeling · user simulators
Outcome
+8 to +13 pp quality lifts; quality became a release gate

Why one judge isn't a quality system

LLM-as-judge is cheap, fast, and necessary. It is not sufficient. A judge model can grade whether an answer reads well; it's far weaker at knowing whether the agent took the right action in a payroll run or a billing workflow, and it drifts when the product underneath it changes. So the discipline I built treats the judge as one instrument in a loop, not the verdict: golden datasets carry ground truth answers and expected actions; offline judging is checked against human labeling on production samples; failure audits feed fixes back into prompts, tools, and content; and regression and drift monitoring make sure last month's quality is still this month's.

The loop, end to end

01Define capability scenarios

What should this agent be able to do, stated as testable scenarios — not vibes.

02Build golden datasets

Curated test cases per capability, drawn from real customer questions.

03Author ground truth

The correct answer and the expected actions — what the agent should say and do.

04Run offline LLM-as-judge

Grade agent output against ground truth at scale, before anything reaches customers.

05Sample production into human labeling

Real conversations, labeled by humans against a rubric — the check on the judge.

06Audit failures and disagreements

Where the agent failed, and where judge and human disagree — both are findings.

07Fix at the source

Route fixes back to prompts, ground truth, tools, or content — whichever actually broke.

08Re-test and watch drift

Regression runs plus drift monitoring, so quality is a state you hold, not a report you wrote once.

One more instrument deserves a mention: multi-turn user simulators — a GenAI bot pretending to be a customer, conversing with the agent across turns under instructions like "if the agent does X, then say Y." Single-turn grading misses the failures that only show up three turns deep; the simulators surface them.

Two weeks' notice

In March 2026 the peer manager who owned day-to-day quality went on sudden leave with two weeks' notice — while quality was the org's top priority. I attended every handoff session, took over the pipeline, and kept delivery moving without a gap. One thing I added under pressure rather than despite it: a KEEP/DELETE audit framework in which designers audited expert-provided ground truth with the original labels hidden, so nobody could anchor on the expert's answer. If the ground truth is wrong, everything downstream of it is confidently wrong.

Then I automated the bottleneck

Authoring golden datasets by hand was the slowest step in the loop, so I built an automated pre-population pipeline: LLM-powered, it pre-fills the evaluation data before a designer ever opens the row. Authoring became human-in-the-loop review — designers verify and correct instead of writing from blank. Turnaround dropped from about three weeks to about one, across 3,000+ rows, without adding headcount.

Rigor that paid for itself

Before scaling the judges, I validated them against a 200-task human-labeled set, with the agreement bar fixed before the test rather than after. The judges cleared it (the internal numbers stay internal, but the decision rule didn't move), and on the strength of that evidence I cut a planned 2,000-row contract with an external labeling vendor. The judges were well-tuned; the spend wasn't needed. That's what an evaluation program should do: tell you not just where quality is, but where your money isn't required.

Help +8 pp · Payroll +9.8 pp · Insights +13 pp

Quality-score lifts as of April 2026 — and the structural win underneath them: quality became a release gate, not a report. Concretely: rows failing correctness or scoring below the conversation-quality threshold were flagged and audited, and a skill didn't ramp until its golden-set results cleared the launch bar.

What this proves

Quality systems leadership has three layers, and I've done all three on the same program: define the methodology (the loop, the rubrics, the audit frameworks), build the tooling that scales it (the pre-population pipeline, the judge validation), and run it through org turbulence (a two-week leadership handoff during the org's top priority, with delivery never stopping). The metrics matter, but the durable outcome is the second one in the callout: shipping now requires evidence.
