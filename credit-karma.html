Case 06 Credit Karma · prompt engineering rotation 2024 · LLM era

The law wrote half the prompt.

A rotation at Credit Karma. The feature reads a member’s credit card wallet and recommends what to add, in plain language, one surface per member. The model’s structured output is the screen. And the hardest material in the build was not the model: it was the law.

The receipt

The legal line was exact: the model reasons over approval-likelihood data and may never state it, in any form. Not a percentage, not a range, not great chances. A flat ban would have been the easy prompt to write. The harder one lets the signal do its work in silence, across every member profile, every time. Compliance here was a design material, not a limiter.

02The constraint

What could not be built.

The binding constraints were legal, and they decided the shape of the feature, not its edges.

reason over

Approval-likelihood data. It decides which cards make sense for this member and which explanation fits.

never state

The same data, in any form. No percentage, no range, no great chances, no hedge that implies a number.

always pair

Rates. An introductory rate never appears without the regular rate in the same sentence, or the sentence does not ship.

The version that answers the member’s actual question could not be built. That was never a model limitation: the capability sat right there in context. It was the law, so the design absorbed it. The odds shape every recommendation and surface in none of them, and the rate disclosure rides along wherever an introductory rate appears.

Note 02

The rule holds on this page too: no approval odds appear anywhere here, including inside the mock.

03The contract

The output did not feed the page. It was the page.

Most LLM features return prose that engineers parse and place. This one returned structure: a list of render blocks, each naming a UI component the app would build, in order. A text block became the analysis copy. A card-tiles block became the row of real offer tiles. The output schema was the layout.

I designed that contract: which blocks existed, the sections they formed, and the rule that mattered most. The model selects cards by reference and never writes their rates, fees, or terms. Those hydrate from real card data downstream, then pass a guardrail layer before anything renders. A model improvising an APR is a lawsuit, not a hallucination.

04The evaluation

Ten profiles, three revisions, one strike.

Tuning was not on vibes. Ten member profiles spanning real credit situations: a thin file with no cards, a high-utilization revolver, a premium member with a long history. Three prompt revisions. Every cell graded on a four-step rubric, and the rubric was one-strike: a single invented benefit, a stray number, or an approval-odds slip made the cell a strike, no matter how well the rest read.

From the first draft to the second revision the strikes drain out. The prompts lived in git, moved through reviewed pull requests, and were readied for release through product, legal, and safety review, not a merge button.

05The handoff

What was mine, and what came next.

A cross-functional build, credited precisely: a research team studied the members, designers owned the UI components the output rendered into, and engineers built the system that resolved the structure into screens and validated it on the way out. Mine: the prompt, the render contract, and the evaluation that decided when it was ready.

These were one-shot explainers. I scoped what came next: a conversational version that could take follow-up questions, and a sibling surface that explains a single recommendation instead of the whole wallet. A model that reasons over what it cannot say, and renders what it does: that constraint became a system I wanted to keep building.
