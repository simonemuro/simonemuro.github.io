No. 06 · Credit Karma · Aug – late 2024

Where the model can't say what it knows.

Regulated finance is the most constrained prompt environment I've worked in. In August 2024 I rotated into Credit Karma as a prompt engineer and picked up production prompts already in motion: "See Why" and "Analyze My Wallet."

WhenAugust to late 2024

ShippedVersioned in git · merged PRs

I joined work already in motion.

Credit Karma recommends credit cards to its members. Two GenAI features explained those recommendations in production: "See Why," which tells a member why a specific card fits them, and "Analyze My Wallet," which reads their whole card portfolio and suggests how to improve it. I authored and iterated the prompts behind both.

The brief sounds simple: explain someone's money to them, personally and plainly. The environment is anything but. Every sentence the model produces lands inside a regulated financial product, and some of the truest sentences available are off limits.

Reason, but don't say.

The sharpest constraint was legal. The model reasons over approval-odds data, because the odds shape which explanation makes sense for a member. And it is legally forbidden from stating them. Not "you're likely to be approved." Not a hint, a hedge, or a percentage.

That's a harder behavior to write than a flat prohibition. The prompt has to let a signal shape the output while keeping it out of the words.

The same feature needed two different prompts.

A prompt is written for a model, not just for a task. "See Why" ran on two, and they wanted structurally different prompts to produce equivalent behavior. GPT-3.5-turbo skipped steps unless it was walked through its own reasoning, so its prompt scaffolded the thinking. GPT-4o wanted the opposite: clean numbered instructions and an explicit response format. The scaffolding that rescued one model was dead weight on the other.

The guardrail, though, travels with the feature. It appears in both architectures, because the law doesn't care which model is answering.

Graded, not vibes.

Every revision was graded against a three-tier rubric, with eval criteria defined alongside the PM and quality engineering. The rubric is strict because the failure modes are expensive: a hallucinated card benefit or an invented number isn't a typo here, it's someone's financial decision.

The prompts shipped the way code ships.

Prompts lived in git, moved through pull requests, and went to production when a PR merged. Before any revision reached members, it ran through the platform's prompt tooling against persona-spanning synthetic test profiles: invented members spanning the credit bands, so a prompt tuned on one member's happy path couldn't quietly regress for everyone else's.

Less than it knows, on purpose.

That's the discipline regulated finance teaches faster than anywhere else. The model holds a signal it never speaks. The output follows a structure that leaves no room to improvise. And the explanation still has to read like it was written for one specific member, in plain language, about their actual wallet.

When the model can't say everything it knows, every word that remains is designed.
