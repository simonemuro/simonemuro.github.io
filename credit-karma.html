Case No. 07 Card recommendations Credit Karma

Helping Credit Karma members see why a card is recommended for them.

Credit Karma shows members credit card offers matched to their credit profile. In 2024 I did a prompt engineering rotation there, writing the prompts behind a feature that explains, in the member’s own terms, why a recommended card fits their wallet. The model reads the member’s credit data, the card’s real terms, and one signal it may never repeat: the member’s approval odds. This case is that boundary, and the craft of shipping model-written language inside credit law.

01Background

A rotation into regulated finance.

Credit Karma is part of Intuit, and in 2024 I rotated over from my conversational AI work in QuickBooks to a prompt engineering seat there. The surface: members browsing card offers see a line under a recommended card inviting them to see why it is their top suggested offer. Tapping it opens a panel where a language model explains the recommendation, written from that member’s credit profile and the card’s actual terms.

The feature existed in its first placement when I arrived, and it was growing into the pages where members actually shop for cards. My job was the language system behind it: the prompts, the synthetic member profiles they were tested against, and the grading that decided when a revision was good enough to ship. The grading criteria were defined with the product manager and quality engineering; the prompt writing and the revision work were mine. This was individual contributor work, all my own hands, inside a full team: product owned the feature and its experiments, designers owned the panel, engineers owned the pipeline that carried the model’s output to the screen, and legal reviewed what the model was allowed to say.

One thing to keep honest about the era: this is 2024 work, before agents. One model call, one structured answer, no tools, no memory. The judgment lived entirely in the prompt and in the evaluation around it.

02The problem

Fluent, accurate, and about nobody.

Ask a capable model why a card is recommended and it produces something like the panel below: fluent, accurate to the card, and generic. It reads like the card’s own marketing page. It could be about anyone, which means it is about no one, and a member deciding whether to apply for credit deserves better than an advertisement restated politely.

An early-draft output, recreated. Card invented. On the rubric this grades acceptable at best: nothing in it comes from the member, and the fee is missing entirely.

And underneath the writing problem sat the harder one. The member’s real question on this panel is the one everybody would ask: am I actually going to get approved for this? The model holds the answer. Approval-likelihood data sits in its context, it is the most useful fact in the room, and credit law says it may never appear in the response. Credit Karma is legally bound not to misrepresent approval odds, so the safe surface area for that signal is zero.

The most useful fact in the room was the one the model could never say.

03Why it mattered

A wrong sentence here is not a typo.

These panels sit exactly where members decide whether to apply for credit. An application is a hard pull on a credit report; a card is a multi-year financial relationship. A generated sentence that overstates a benefit, invents a fee, or hints at approval chances does real harm to a real person’s finances, and it exposes the company legally. In most products a hallucination is a bug. In this one it is a liability with a member attached.

That is also why the work was worth a rotation. Anyone can prompt a model to sound personal. Getting a model to be personal, truthful, and silent about the one thing it knows best, on every profile, on every card, is a design problem worth learning from the inside.

04Exploring solutions

Three ways to handle the odds, and a test bed you can trust.

For the approval-odds signal there were three honest options. Strip the data out of the context entirely, so the model cannot leak what it cannot see; the explanations get safer and worse, because the signal genuinely shapes which cards make sense. Refuse the topic, so any question near approval gets a canned deflection; safe, and the member learns nothing. Or let the model reason over the data and forbid it from ever surfacing it: not a percentage, not a range, not great chances, not a hedge that implies a number. I went with the third. It is the hardest one to hold, and it is the only one where the member gets the full benefit of the signal without ever being shown it.

Testing that boundary needed member data, and real member data was untouchable. So the prompts were tested against synthetic member profiles I could publish revisions against: nine of them, spanning credit bands and wallet situations, from a near-prime member with four category cards and higher utilization to a strong-credit member whose wallet already matched the recommendation. Every revision ran against all nine.

One more constraint shaped the writing. Production ran on two different models, and they did not take instructions the same way: the older one needed its reasoning spelled out step by step before it wrote a word, the newer one took direct instructions. So the prompt was not one artifact. It was a variant per model, kept in lockstep, with the same rules and the same boundary in both.

05Design decisions

Four rules the language had to keep.

Four decisions define the explanation. Each one is visible in the shipped shape in part seven.

  1. 5.1

    Reason over the odds. Never state them.

    Legal set the rule; the design question was what to do with it. The prompt holds the model to the strict version: use the approval-likelihood data in your reasoning, and never state, infer, or reference it in the response. The signal does its work in silence, and it is the last instruction the model reads before it answers.

  2. 5.2

    Explain head to head with the wallet.

    The rewrite that mattered most in the revision workbook: stop describing the card and start comparing it with the cards the member already carries. What their current cards earn, what this one adds, who it is actually for. That is the difference between a recommendation and an advertisement.

  3. 5.3

    The cost always shows.

    One bullet in every explanation states the annual fee, even when it is zero dollars. A recommendation that only lists upsides is a pitch. Naming the cost every time is what makes the rest of the explanation believable.

  4. 5.4

    No arithmetic in the copy.

    The model states terms from the card record and facts from the member’s profile. It performs no calculations on the member’s money. A model doing math on someone’s balances is a wrong answer waiting to render, so any calculation in the output is an automatic failure on the rubric.

06The build

The prompt’s anatomy, and prompts treated like code.

The prompt is a contract in five parts, and its output is not prose an engineer has to parse. The model answers in a fixed structure, valid JSON only: an overview and two to three detail bullets, and that structure renders straight into the panel the member sees. The overview becomes the paragraph, the details become the bullets. Downstream of the model, a guardrail layer validated the output before anything rendered.

The prompts lived in git and moved through reviewed, merged pull requests, the same way the code around them did. A revision was not done when it read well; it was done when it passed review and held its grades. Release readiness ran through product, legal, and trust and safety review, not a merge button. As the feature spread to the category pages where members browse by goal, the prompt gained the page as context, so a travel page got a travel-shaped explanation of the same card.

The shipped shape, recreated. Member profile and card invented; the pattern is real. Grounded in the member’s own cards, the fee stated in its own bullet, no approval odds stated or implied.

What earned that shape was the grind, not a flash of taste. About a dozen prompt revisions, each graded against all nine synthetic profiles on a three-tier rubric: good, acceptable, unacceptable. Some failures were automatic, no matter how well the rest read: approval odds stated or implied, a hallucinated member or card fact, a missing annual fee, arithmetic in the copy. Early drafts collected automatic failures; revision by revision they drained out, until the release candidates held their grades across every profile. Here is one graded cell of that grid.

The honest scoreboard: the prompts shipped through merged pull requests into a feature that was expanding across the product’s card-shopping pages, and the rotation ended before an outcome number I can put my name on. The measurement stayed with the team, so this case claims the craft, not a conversion lift. What it can show is how a prompt earned release: not because it read well once, but because it held across nine member profiles, revision after revision, with the law inside it.

And the rule holds on this page too: no approval odds appear anywhere on it, including inside the recreations.

08Learnings

What I learned, and what I would do differently.

The tightest constraint produced the best writing. Reason over, never state is a harder line to hold than a flat ban, and holding it forced explanations that were personal without ever gesturing at the forbidden number. Compliance was a design material here, not a limiter.

A rubric with automatic failures beats taste. Reading a draft, I would forgive a graceful sentence that brushed the boundary. The rubric would not, and the rubric was right. You cannot argue with a strike, which is exactly what makes it useful.

Write for the model you have, not the model in your head. The same rules had to be delivered two different ways for two models, and pretending one prompt fits all would have shipped the boundary loosely on one of them.

Two things I would do differently. I would build the graded rubric before the first draft instead of arriving at it through the early revisions, because the failures it caught were findable on day one. And I would have arranged to see the experiment readouts after the rotation ended; craft without its outcome number is a story I have to tell more carefully, as this page shows.
