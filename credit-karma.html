Case No. 07  ·  Card recommendations  ·  Credit Karma

The model knows your approval odds. It's never allowed to say them.

At Credit Karma, "see why" puts a model-written explanation under a recommended card, built from the member's own credit profile. The member's real question is whether they'll be approved. The model holds that answer in its context, credit law forbids it in the response, and the craft was an explanation that reasons over the odds without ever leaking them. Eleven prompt revisions in one workbook, graded against nine synthetic member profiles. The workbook is on this page.

Problem

Members want to know if they'll be approved, and the law forbids stating the odds.

What I did

Built eleven prompt revisions that reason over approval odds and never say them, graded against nine synthetic profiles.

Outcome

Strikes gone by revision two; all nine profiles rated ideal or acceptable; shipped through reviewed, merged pull requests.

Role

Prompt engineer, a rotation from my Conversation AI role. The prompts, profiles, and grading runs; criteria set with product and QE.

01 · The problem

Fluent, accurate, and about nobody.

Credit Karma is part of Intuit, and in 2024 I rotated from my QuickBooks Conversation AI work into a prompt engineering seat there. The feature existed in its first placement when I arrived, headed for the pages where members actually shop for cards. Ask a capable model why a card is recommended, though, and you get something like this: fluent, accurate to the card, and generic. It reads like the card's own marketing page.

"Why this card?" · an early-draft outputRecreated · card invented

The Horizon Cash Rewards card is a great choice, offering strong cash back rewards and flexible benefits for everyday spending.

  • Earn unlimited cash back on every purchase you make.
  • Enjoy a welcome bonus after qualifying purchases in your first months.
  • Benefit from flexible redemption options and trusted service.
ContinueSee details

Written by AI from your credit profile and the card's terms.

Nothing in it comes from the member, and the fee is missing entirely.

Underneath the writing problem sat the harder one. The member's real question on this panel is the one everybody would ask: am I actually going to get approved? Approval-likelihood data sits in the model's context. Credit Karma is legally bound not to misrepresent approval odds, so the safe surface area for that signal in the response is zero. In most products a hallucination is a bug. In this one it's a liability with a member attached.

02 · The boundary

Reason over the odds. Never say them.

The move that made the feature possible: let the model keep the approval-likelihood data in its reasoning, where it makes the explanation smarter about fit, and wall it out of the response completely. Here's that wall, as shipped. It sits at the bottom of the prompt, after every other instruction, and it appears word for word in all eleven revisions.

the boundary instruction · verbatimin all eleven revisions
Here is an additional and extremely critical
instruction:

Always exclude any mention of approval odds in your
final response, and exclude any guarantee about your
certainty level when it comes to approval odds.
Credit Karma is legally bound to not misrepresent
approval odds, so while you may use this information
in your reasoning, it is not to ever be shared
directly in your response. Remember, do not include,
infer or reference any information about approval
odds in your response.

Respond with valid JSON only.
Verbatim from my revision workbook; the one runtime detail removed is which model received it. The rule holds on this page too: no approval odds appear anywhere on it.
03 · The test bed

Nine synthetic members, and automatic strikes.

You can't test prompts like this on real members, so I built nine synthetic ones: a thin file with no cards, a high-utilization revolver, a premium member with a thick wallet, and the profiles between. Revisions were graded against the set. The criteria were defined with the product manager and quality engineering: quality rated ideal, acceptable, or below standard, with automatic strikes for legal and safety misses. A strike failed the run no matter how good the writing was. You can't argue with a strike, which is exactly what makes it useful.

Real workbook structure; the tabs are reproduced exactly, spelling included. Twelve tabs, eleven holding a full prompt version; the final tab was named and left empty. Half the log is one theme: making the model talk about the member's cards, not just the recommended one.

Two of those revisions carried the most weight, and the before-and-after language is worth reading closely.

revision · emphasis on cards I hold

From the card's features to the member's gaps

The initial prompt asked the model to sell what the card does. This revision forces every claim through the member's actual wallet, and the workbook shows the theme being re-fought across later tabs.

highlight unique features of the
recommended card that would benefit me

The rewrite: "Provide a brief overview of the recommended credit card by connecting its benefits to the gaps identified in my financial profile. Ensure to call out the specific cards I currently hold and the gap you identified."

revision · Address QE issue

The model stops doing math

Quality engineering flagged outputs where the model worked numbers on its own. Sooner or later it would get someone's balance math wrong, so the revision took the calculator away.

The added rule: "Always rely on the pre-calculated data points from the member's profile, instead of calculating data points yourself." The same revision gave the prompt its shipped persona: "You are 'Intuit Assist', an AI-powered personal Finance Analyst."

Instruction language quoted from the workbook: the before line from the initial prompt, the rewrites from the named tabs.

05 · The prompt, assembled

What eleven revisions add up to.

The late-revision prompt, reduced to its skeleton. Every part earned its place through a graded run, and the order matters: the member's data comes before the card's, the comparison rules come with the fee requirement attached, and the boundary comes last, closest to the response.

prompt anatomy · late revisionStructure verbatim · data invented
persona: "Intuit Assist", a personal finance analyst
  for Credit Karma members
fallback: if the member's card data is unavailable,
  describe the card in general terms; never invent
  the member's side
data_rules: rely on pre-calculated data points from
  the member's profile; never calculate them yourself
member_data:
  financial_profile   # score, balances, utilization
  current_cards       # full terms of what they hold
  recommended_card    # the offer being explained
assessment: review the member's profile and cards,
  then the recommended card, then compare
response_rules: an overview connecting the card to
  this member's gaps, then three comparison bullets
  naming the member's specific cards. always state
  the annual fee and the amount
schema: {"overview": "string", "details": ["string"]}
boundary: reason over approval odds, never state,
  infer, or reference them  # verbatim in part 02
final_line: respond with valid JSON only

06 · Where it landed

Shipped like code, because it was code.

shipped

Prompts went out through reviewed, merged pull requests, with product, legal, and quality engineering review on the way. A revision wasn't done when it read well; it was done when the run held its grades and the review held up.

two surfaces

The prompt system covered the card offer details page and the find-a-card and category pages, tailoring its explanation to the category the member was shopping.

the boundary

Product owned the feature and its experiments; design owned the panel; engineering owned the pipeline; legal wrote the rule. The prompts, the synthetic profiles, and the grading runs are the work on this page. The rotation ended with the prompts merged and readied for release.

What this settled

This is 2024 work, from the era between chatbots and agents: one model call, one structured answer, no tools, no memory. The judgment lived entirely in the prompt and the evaluation around it, which is exactly why the workbook can carry this case. The best writing in it exists because the most useful fact in the room was off limits, so the explanation had to earn its usefulness somewhere else: the member's own cards, the gaps in their wallet, the fee stated plainly. The agentic version of the same discipline is the billing skill.
