No. 07 · Credit Karma · 2024

The prompt that rendered the page.

On a prompt-engineering rotation at Credit Karma, I owned the prompt behind a GenAI feature that reads a member's credit-card wallet and recommends what to add. The twist that made it a design problem: the model's structured output didn't feed the screen, it was the screen. I designed that contract, under the hardest legal constraints I've worked in.

RolePrompt engineer (rotation)
When2024
SurfaceGenAI wallet analysis & card recommendations
OwnedThe prompt · the output-to-layout contract · the evaluation

The feature, in one screen.

A member opens it and gets their money explained back to them. The model reads their actual wallet, the cards they carry and their credit profile, and writes a plain-language analysis: what's working, what's missing. Then it recommends cards that fill the gaps and says why, comparing each one to what the member already holds. One scrollable surface, generated per member.

The summary at the top is fixed data. Everything below the line is the model talking, and it has to read like it was written for one specific person.

The model didn't fill the page. It returned it.

Most LLM features return text, and engineers parse it and place it on a screen. This one returned structure. The model emitted a list of render blocks, and each block named a UI component the app would build, in the order they appeared. A text block became the analysis copy. A card-tiles block became the row of real offer tiles. The model's output schema was the page's layout.

So the prompt wasn't writing prose, it was emitting an interface. I designed that contract: which blocks existed, the sections they formed and their order, and one rule that mattered more than any other, the model selects which cards to surface, by reference, but never writes their rates, fees, or terms. Those come from real card data. A model improvising an APR is a lawsuit, not a hallucination.

Reason over it. Never say it.

The sharpest constraint was legal. The model reasons over approval-likelihood data, because it shapes which cards make sense to surface, and it is legally forbidden from stating it in any form: not a percentage, not a hint, not "great chances." A separate rule: any introductory rate has to be followed immediately by the regular rate, or the sentence can't ship.

That's harder to write than a flat ban. The prompt has to let a signal steer the output while keeping it out of the words, every time, across every member profile.

Graded across ten members, revision by revision.

I didn't tune on vibes. I built a grid: ten member profiles spanning real credit situations, a thin file with no cards, a high-utilization revolver, a premium user with perfect credit, and on, by three prompt revisions. Every cell got graded on a four-color rubric, and it was one-strike: a single hallucinated benefit, a stray number, or an approval-odds slip turned a cell red, no matter how good the rest read.

From the first draft to the second revision, you can watch the red and orange drain out. The prompts lived in git, moved through pull requests, and were readied for release through product, legal, and safety review, not a merge button.

What I owned, and what was next.

To be precise about a cross-functional build: I authored the prompt and the output-to-layout contract, and I ran the evaluation. A research team studied the members. Designers owned the UI components my output rendered into. Engineers built the system that resolved the model's structure into screens and validated it on the way out. My piece was making a model's structured output behave like a designed, lawful surface, reliably, every time.

These were one-shot explainers. I scoped where they'd go next: a follow-up-capable conversational version, and a sibling surface that explains a single recommendation rather than the whole wallet. The constraint, a model that has to reason over what it can't say and render what it does, became a system I wanted to keep building.
