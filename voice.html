Case No. 06 Voice QuickBooks phone support

Designing spoken answers for QuickBooks customers who call support.

On the phone, help has to work when heard once: no screen, no links, no second read. I designed the assistant’s side of those calls, from clarifying-question repairs to the GenAI voice snippet that proved the assistant understood before offering help.

01Background

The phone line, and where my hands were.

I was a conversation designer on the conversational AI team that owned the support assistant. On the phone line, the assistant picks up first: it hears the caller in their own words, works out the problem, and either helps or brings in an expert. Everything it says is spoken by a synthesized voice and heard exactly once. Help on this surface traveled as self-help, a link sent by text or email, because instructions belong on a screen.

Two threads of work run through this case, and my hands were in different places on each. The clarifying question redesigns, payroll taxes in 2022 and login and password reset in 2024, were my own flows: I wrote the questions, the branches, and the fallbacks myself. The voice snippet design and prototypes were mine too: I owned the experience requirements, the content bar, and the prompt rules that shape what the caller hears. Later, I partnered with the voice platform team on the build, including triggering, disclaimers, telephony, and analytics. Where the line between my design ownership and implementation partnership matters below, I say which is which.

02The problem

A link, sight unseen.

Customers calling for product support often felt the assistant did not understand their problem and lacked confidence in the answer it gave. The assistant could not respond with anything specific or relevant before making its offer, so accepting the self-help link felt like wasting time that a person would not waste. Here is the shape of that call, recreated.

The offer names the topic and proves nothing. The caller is asked to trust a link they cannot see, from a voice that has not shown it understood the problem. Most said no.

03Why it mattered

Every refused offer became an expert call.

On this line there is no quiet exit. A caller who refuses the self-help offer does not close a tab; they wait for an expert, and the call costs what expert calls cost. The dials the work is scored on say the same thing: acceptance, the share of offered help that callers take, and containment, the share of calls that end with no expert on the line.

Before the snippet work started, the project wrote its baselines down: of the callers offered self-help, about 30 percent accepted it, and about 20 percent of those who received it resolved without reaching an expert. That was the starting position, not a result. The clarifying question work had already shown the dials could move; the question was whether a generated answer, spoken aloud, could move them further.

04Exploring solutions

The clarifying question had already proved itself.

The first thread of this work was repair design: the behavior for the moment the assistant half understands. Four different problems can share the sentence “I am locked out of my account,” and reading the wrong fix aloud costs the whole call. The pattern I designed spends one turn on a shaped clarifying question, then acts on the answer. On payroll tax calls in 2022, that redesign moved containment from 12.5 to 18.3 percent and acceptance from 23 to 32.

When language models arrived, I tested whether they could carry the same judgment. In a proof of concept on the disambiguation prompt, the existing approach disambiguated correctly 53.3 percent of the time; the treatment prompt I wrote reached 76.7. In 2023 I wrote and presented three generative AI use cases for the phone line: cancellation saves, auto disambiguation, and the answer summary, which became the voice snippet. Hearing why a customer cancels became its own case, No. 05. This case is the snippet.

The insight the snippet is built on, stated plainly: the problem was not that the answer existed somewhere. It was that the offer carried no proof of understanding. Put a short piece of the answer in the caller’s ear, and accepting help no longer means trusting a link sight unseen.

05Design decisions

The behavior spec.

I wrote the snippet’s behavior as a spec before any prompt: what the assistant says, when it asks, and the exits that are never allowed to fail. The deliberate choice at the center of it: the snippet is roughly three short sentences, a teaser of the full answer, and it will likely not fully answer the caller’s question. That is on purpose. Its job is to prove the assistant understood and to make the offer worth accepting; the full steps belong on a screen, sent by text or email.

Reading a dialog is not hearing one. Three of the behaviors on this page, the shaped question, the owned mishear, and the handoff that keeps its context, reconstructed as short calls you can press play on. Every voice is synthetic and every name and value is mock; the label under each player says exactly what you are hearing.

pattern 01repair · disambiguation

One shaped question, then action.

Four problems share the opening sentence. Listen for the shape of the question: three options, one breath, then action on the answer. No apology, no open prompt.

transcript
  1. caller

    Hi, I am locked out of my account, and I have a client waiting.

  2. agent

    I can help with that. Which sounds closest: you forgot your password, your reset email never arrived, or the account says it is locked?

  3. caller

    The second one. I asked for a reset email twice, and nothing came.

  4. agent

    Thank you. Reset emails sometimes land in spam, so check there first for a message from our team. If it is not there, I can send a fresh link to the mobile number ending in four two one seven. Shall I send it?

  5. caller

    Yes, send it.

  6. agent

    Done. The link is on its way, and it stays active for thirty minutes.

pattern 02error recovery · a mishear

Own the mishear, keep the call.

The recognizer hears bega for vega. Listen for who owns the error: the agent does, re-confirms letter by letter, and moves on. The caller is never told they said it wrong.

transcript

    I need to update the email address on my account.

  2. agent

  3. caller

    It is maria dot vega, at example dot com.

  4. agent

    I want to make sure I have it right. I heard maria dot bega, with a b. Did I get that right?

  5. caller

    No, it is vega. V as in victor.

  6. agent

    Thank you. Vega, with a v. That is m, a, r, i, a, dot, v, e, g, a, at example dot com. Is that correct?

  7. caller

pattern 03escalation · with context

Hand off the context, not just the call.

The agent reaches its boundary and says so. Listen for the spoken summary that travels with the transfer: the person picks up already knowing, and the caller never repeats.

transcript
  1. caller

    The reset link finally worked, but when I sign in, it still says my account is locked.

  2. agent

    Thank you for trying that. A lock that remains after a successful reset needs a specialist, so I am bringing one in now. Here is what I am passing along: your reset went through, the new password is good, and the account still shows a lock. You will not need to repeat any of that. One moment.

  3. expert

    Hi, this is Dana. I can see the reset went through and the lock is still active, so I am clearing it on my side right now. Give it about ten seconds, then try again.

  4. caller

    That did it. I am in. Thank you.

06The build

Two spec sheets, a prompt, and a throttle.

The requirements split cleanly in two, and being precise about whose hands wrote which is the point of this paragraph. Mine were the experience and content requirements: the teaser shape, the disambiguation behavior, the escapes, the fallbacks, and the routing parity in part five, plus the prompt rules below. The platform fundamentals belonged to the partner team: trigger the generated experience for one specific intent only, play a disclaimer to exactly the customers who receive it, carry the conversation to the model and back, and track every generated response.

The prompt is where the content bar becomes enforceable. Steps that work on a screen are noise in the ear, so the rules ban lists, URLs, and numbered instructions outright, and make sequence ride on spoken connectives instead. A bare noun never gets an answer; it gets one clarifying question, with no apology padding the turn.

artifact · the snippet prompt, the rules that shape a spoken answer · redacted excerpt 
Your role is to provide a short, accurate response using a friendly and
conversational tone.

If the question is a standalone product name or object with no clear action
("billing", "invoices", "help with my account"), tell the customer you are
happy to help but need more details. Do not apologize. Do not answer until
you clearly understand the action to be taken.

Once the action is clear:
1. Be brief and conversational. Provide an answer that is a teaser snippet
   of the full answer: very concise, only the most important information.
2. Do not use bulleted or numbered lists in your response.
3. Do not provide any URLs in the response.
4. Do not list step-by-step instructions. Use first, then, next, finally...
   instead of numbers.
5. After providing a teaser snippet, offer to send the more detailed
   solution through text or email.

Keep words simple and specific. Use active verbs, and avoid jargon,
gerunds, and passive sentence constructions.

Then the part of the plan I would show any team shipping a first generative experience on a phone line: how it was released. The release did not go straight to the full line. I recommended the invoicing intent on purpose: tenth by call volume, about 2 percent of it, most questions straightforward, no special expert tools required, and containment sitting at about 24 percent against an assistant average of 29. Low risk, with room to move.

That plan is what carried the snippet from a prototype the platform could not yet support in 2023 all the way to production. I authored and prototyped the experience, then partnered with the platform and analytics teams to build it on the sanctioned path and measure it through launch. The experiment launched on December 10, 2024, reached Bayesian significance, and the experience moved to full production shortly after.

07Results

What shipped, and what it moved.

Here is the call the spec and the prompt produce, recreated: a teaser that proves the assistant understood, then the offer.

What I claim about the snippet, I claim exactly. The hypothesis going in was six points or more of containment. The experiment measured a lift of 3.9 points and reached Bayesian significance, so the effect was real, if smaller than the bet. That is the measured outcome of the experiment, not a separate long-run production readout, and I keep it at that. The platform teams owned the runtime; the prototype, the behavior spec, the prompt, the evaluation, and the release strategy were mine, and I carried the experience through to the shipped version with platform and analytics partners.

The rest of the receipted numbers come from the clarifying question thread of this same voice program. The payroll redesign in 2022 proved the pattern. The login and password reset redesign in 2024 carried it furthest.

08Learnings

What I learned, and what I would do differently.

Write for the ear, not the eye. Steps that work on a screen are noise in the ear. Every rule in the prompt excerpt came from hearing what written help sounds like when a machine reads it aloud.

The offer is the product. Callers were not refusing help; they were refusing an offer that carried no proof of understanding. Three sentences of the actual answer change the offer without changing the answer.

De-risk in the release plan, not in the copy. A low risk intent with headroom, 50 customers first, a risk threshold before any throttle. That plan is what let a generated voice go out on a support line at all, and I would reuse it anywhere.

Two things I would do differently. I would push past article links sooner. The post launch list already pointed there: bespoke spoken answers instead of links, and proactive help, so an invoicing question also surfaces the reminders and automation the caller will want next. And I would carry the results readout the same way I carried the prototype, all the way through, so the measured lift and the full production story arrive together instead of the number trailing the ship by a year.
