No. 02 · Scaling the answer key behind AI evaluation

To grade thousands of conversations, you need an answer key.

An AI support agent talks to small-business owners about their books, and it can act on their accounts. To ship a new version safely, you grade it against an answer key, the golden dataset, across thousands of conversations. People were authoring that key by hand, and it didn't scale. So I automated the drafting, and handed the experts a strong first draft to validate instead of a blank sheet to fill.

RoleAI quality-eval team: owned offline datasets + ground truth; authored the pre-fill generator
Built withGolden datasets · pre-fill generation · LLM-as-judge
When2025–2026
OutcomeGolden-dataset turnaround ~3 weeks → ~1 week, across 3,000+ rows

The bottleneck: authoring the answer key by hand.

To grade an AI agent you need a golden dataset: the right answer for thousands of conversations. But the right answer was not a sentence. As the agent learned to do more than reply, to take actions, render interactive widgets, and draw charts, every row had to spell out all of it: the answer text, the exact actions, the widgets, and the chart data, point by point.

People hand-entered all of it, one row at a time. Charting a single correct answer meant typing every label and every value on every axis. It took about three weeks per dataset, it was error-prone, and it was exactly the wrong use of an expert's judgment.

So I automated the drafting. The generator reads a conversation transcript and pre-fills every part of the row, the answer, the actions, the widgets, and the chart data, so the experts validate instead of hand-enter. Not to replace them, but to spend their judgment where it counted. Here is how it came together.

First, learn what a good answer key actually is.

I audited the hand-authored dataset and found the same errors over and over: answers written into the wrong field, missing simulator instructions, mislabeled turns. Then I studied how the experts actually work, their training, their good-and-bad examples, and the criteria the team had agreed on. I pulled all of it into one context document and gave it to the model as its brief. The experts' judgment became the generator's rules.

Then write the generator, and rewrite it, and rewrite it.

The generator is not a script; it is a long instruction set the model follows. I built it up over about seven versions in six weeks, and almost every version answered something real: a working call redefined what counts as a turn; feedback that it was doing too much led me to strip it back to only the ground truth; a teammate's differently shaped sheet pushed me to make it read any column layout. One rewrite mattered most: I stopped telling it to fill fields and started telling it to think like the most experienced labeler on the team. When it got something wrong, I fixed the principle behind it, not the single example.

Keep the model sharp by never asking too much at once.

A thousand-row sheet in one pass is a trap: the model loses the thread and the context window overflows, so the later rows get worse. A Python pipeline I wrote splits the sheet into small batches, runs them in parallel, stitches the results back together, and runs twelve automated checks before a human sees anything. The script's real job is discipline: keep every call small enough to stay accurate, then prove the reassembly changed nothing it should not.

Draft into the margins; never touch the original.

Every value the generator produces lands in a new recommended column. The original ground truth is byte-for-byte identical, input to output, and a check fails the run if that is ever not true. Experts read the recommendations, fix what needs fixing, and promote the ones they trust. The machine drafts; the expert judges, and the expert can trust that the machine never overwrote their work.

The result: authoring became reviewing.

The team adopted the generator to pre-fill ground truth before experts validated it. What had taken about three weeks by hand took about one, across thousands of rows, and the experts spent their time on judgment instead of typing.

Why the answer key had to be this good.

Ground truth is the only reason the eval can catch the failures that matter, and the two that matter most look nothing alike.

On the AI quality-evaluation team, I owned the offline datasets and the ground truth, authored the generator and the LLM-as-judge simulator instructions, and partnered on the quality rubrics. The eval infrastructure, the judge, and the release gate were the wider team's. A quality program this size is never one person's work.

The generator was mine, and it's the reason evaluation could keep pace with the agents.
