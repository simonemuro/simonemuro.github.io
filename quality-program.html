01
The problem

One row could take three kinds of expert.

Quality was the gate: a skill went live, got blocked, or rolled back on its offline eval score, and every eval compared the skill's answer to ground truth, the ideal answer for a scenario. Writing one row was rarely one person's job. The text answer needed a conversation designer. The actions needed a domain expert. A chart or a widget needed a data scientist. A single row could need all three, which is why ground truth was hard to scale, not just slow: every row was a small act of cross-disciplinary judgment.

What one row can demand
Conversation designer
how many turns before a customer drops, how much information is too much, the entities to collect before acting, what good sounds like
Domain expert
knows the product cold, spots a wrong navigation step, and knows the shortest path to the action
Data scientist
specifies the structured chart or widget a system scores, its type, axis, and series, not the picture a customer sees
02
The insight

The rare work is the correction, not the blank page.

Experts weren't slow because judgment is slow. They were slow because every row started empty, and the hardest, rarest work, the cross-disciplinary judgment, was trapped behind hours of drafting and formatting. So I moved the effort. If a system could produce a first draft to spec, roughly seventy percent of the row, the experts' job would flip from authoring to correcting, and their judgment would land only on the part that needed it.

Before · author every row from empty
  • ×Write the text answer, turn by turn
  • ×Write the actions and their steps
  • ×Specify any chart or widget by hand
  • ×Repeat for every row, every skill
After · validate a draft that's ~70% there
  • ✓Text, actions, and chart specs arrive drafted
  • ✓Experts review and correct, not create
  • ✓Judgment lands only where it's needed
  • ✓The same process runs for any skill
03
The solution

A generator that drafts like the best labeler.

I wrote the generator by distilling how the team's most experienced labeler actually works, the rules for the text, the actions, and the chart and widget specs, into one prompt built on judgment over rules: when a row doesn't fit a pattern, do what the best labeler would do. Running a whole dataset through one model would be slow and lossy, so a script splits the sheet into fifty-row chunks, a parallel agent drafts each chunk, and the results are glued back into one sheet. That draft, about seventy percent complete, went to a team I coordinated for the final review, where experts validate and correct.

The safeguard that mattered at scale

In review, I caught a path that could quietly overwrite a validated answer with a model's draft, no trace left behind. I closed it, deleting the path and adding a check that fails the run if it is ever attempted. Every original answer stays intact, on every code path.

04
The outcome

The gate kept pace with the platform.

With drafts arriving pre-filled, experts validated instead of authored, and offline evals ran across every skill instead of stalling behind manual work. A large batch that had taken about three weeks came down to about one. On the three skills measured through this work, quality rose 8 to 13 points.
