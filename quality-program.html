Case 03 · The Quality Program agentic era · 2025 to 2026

To grade thousands of conversations, you need an answer key.

An AI support agent talks to small-business owners about their books, and it can act on their accounts. Before a new version ships, it is graded against the golden dataset: thousands of conversations with the right answer spelled out in full. People authored that key by hand. I automated the drafting.

02The answer key

One row of ground truth is not a sentence.

As the agent learned to do more than reply, to take actions, render widgets, and draw charts, the right answer grew to match. Every row of the key had to spell all of it out: the answer text, the exact actions, the widgets, and the chart data, point by point.

People hand-entered every field, one row at a time. Charting a single correct answer meant typing every label and every value on every axis. A dataset took about three weeks, the errors hid in the typing, and it was exactly the wrong use of an expert’s judgment.

So I automated the drafting. The generator reads a conversation transcript and pre-fills the whole row, so the experts validate instead of hand-enter. Not to replace their judgment: to spend it where it counts.

03The build

Teach the machine to draft like the team’s best labeler.

First, learn what good is.

I audited the hand-authored dataset and found the same errors repeating: answers written into the wrong field, missing simulator instructions, mislabeled turns. Then I studied how the experts actually work: their training, their good and bad examples, the criteria the team had agreed on. All of it went into one context document the model receives as its brief. The experts’ judgment became the generator’s rules.

Rewrite the principles, not the examples.

The generator is not a script; it is a long instruction set the model follows. I built it up over about seven versions in six weeks, and nearly every version answered something real: a working session redefined what counts as a turn; feedback that it was doing too much stripped it back to only the ground truth; a teammate’s differently shaped sheet made it read any column layout.

One rewrite mattered most. I stopped telling it to fill fields and started telling it to reason like the most experienced labeler on the team. When it got something wrong, I fixed the principle behind the miss, not the single example.

Keep every call small enough to stay sharp.

A thousand-row sheet in one pass is a trap: the context window overflows and the later rows quietly degrade. A Python pipeline I wrote splits the sheet into small batches, drafts them in parallel, stitches the results back together, and runs 12 codified QA checks before a person sees anything. The pipeline’s real job is discipline: keep each call inside a reliable window, then prove the reassembly changed nothing it should not have.

Draft into the margins. Never touch the original.

Every value the generator produces lands in a new recommended column. The original ground truth leaves the pipeline byte for byte as it entered, and a check fails the run if that is ever untrue. Experts read the drafts, fix what needs fixing, and promote what they trust. The machine drafts; the expert judges; and the expert can rely on the machine never overwriting their work.

The bug that proved the rule.

The guarantee got its test in review. Before the pipeline shipped, a reviewer caught a fallback path that could silently overwrite existing ground truth: exactly the failure the design promised away. I removed the fallback within hours and replaced it with a safe promote step that only ever writes forward, with all 12 QA checks passing. The lesson stayed with the program: a fallback path is a silent failure path in disguise.

04The proof

What moved, and who measured it.

The team adopted the generator to pre-fill ground truth before experts validate it. An answer key that had taken about three weeks to author by hand took about one week, across 3,000+ rows, and offline evaluation kept pace with the agents.

On the far side of the key, the scores the program exists to move, moved. Conversation quality rose 8 points on Help, 9.8 on Payroll, and 13 on Business Insights in a year. The honest scope: the program’s own judge did the measuring, I owned the datasets and the ground truth it grades against, and the lifts belong to every team that shipped against them.

The other catch the key exists for.

The decision at the top of this page is the rare catch: a right answer that did a wrong thing. The everyday catch is quieter. It just sounds right. Ground truth is what lets the judge fail a fluent answer that a person skimming would wave through.

05The role

Whose work this was.

On the AI quality-evaluation team, I owned the offline datasets and the ground truth, authored the generator and the LLM-as-judge simulator instructions, and partnered with stakeholders on the quality rubrics.

What I did not build: the eval infrastructure, the judge, and the release gate. Those were the wider team’s, and a quality program this size is never one person’s work.

The generator was mine. It is the reason evaluation kept pace with the agents.
