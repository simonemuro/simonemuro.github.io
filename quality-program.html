Case No. 03  ·  Ground truth  ·  Intuit Intelligence · quality team

The answer key was manual. I made it a system.

An agentic platform's quality program scores every skill against ground truth: the answer each skill should have given. I owned that ground truth. As the platform grew, authoring it by hand stopped scaling, so I codified the work into a production system, generators and a pre-fill pipeline shipped as platform code, so experts validate answers instead of writing every one from scratch. That is what lets the program measure quality across every skill.

Problem

The quality program needs a checked answer for every skill. Authoring them by hand couldn't scale.

My scope

The offline ground truth: the answer key, its standard, and the system that produces it.

What I built

A codified, repeatable production process: generators and a pre-fill pipeline, shipped as platform code.

Outcome

The program measures quality across every skill; quality rose +8 to 13 points across three.

01
What I owned

The answer key every skill is scored against.

Ground truth is the answer key: for each scenario, the answer a skill should give and the actions it should take. I owned it. I created the answers, verified them with the experts, and kept them current as the platform grew. It has to be exact, because a skill graded on how its answer reads can tell a customer their taxes were filed on a day that never happened, and sound completely right doing it. I also expanded what “quality” measured, from accuracy, completeness, and relevance out to personalization, the right tool calls, and the right actions, and I trained the designers who authored against the standard.

02
The build

Manual authoring didn't scale. So I codified it.

As the platform grew past a handful of skills, hand-authoring the answer key broke down. Experts spent their hours filling fields instead of making judgment calls, and every new skill started from a blank sheet. I turned it into a production system: the answers get pre-filled to spec, and the expert's job becomes review and correct. I codified the standard into generators and a pre-fill pipeline, shipped as reviewed platform code, so the same process runs for any skill, not just the one in front of me. Their judgment goes where it belongs, to how each answer should be graded.

I didn't get the rules right in one pass. I refined them again and again, testing each version on a sample before it touched the real set. That iteration is what turned a one-off script into something the whole program could rely on.

03
Keeping it honest

A production system needs guardrails.

Scale is only worth it if the output stays trustworthy. I re-checked the ground truth in waves, a fresh keep-or-cut call on each row with the original label hidden, so no one anchored on the first answer. I built the guardrail that keeps a validated answer from ever being overwritten by a model's draft: a reviewer caught the risk, I closed the path, and added a check that fails the run if it's ever tried. And because the judges that scored every skill ran against my ground truth, I gave the eval team the human labels that helped them validate those judges before they were trusted.
