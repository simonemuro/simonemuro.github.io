01Background

The program, and my piece of it.

The earlier cases on this site build agent experiences. This case is the other side of that work: how the team knows an agent is good enough to ship. Before a new version goes out, a user simulator drives multi-turn conversations against it, and an LLM judge scores every conversation against ground truth on a weighted rubric. The result is a conversation quality score, and that score informs the release.

A quality program this size is never one person’s work. Data science and engineering built the eval infrastructure, the judge, and the release gate. The quality rubrics were defined with stakeholders across several teams, and the whole thing runs on cross-team calibration: working calls where designers, data scientists, and engineers argue about what counts as a good answer until the criteria say the same thing to everyone.

My piece was the ground truth. I owned the offline evaluation datasets, the answer key the judge grades against, and I authored the generator that pre-fills it and the LLM-as-judge simulator instructions. The generator prompt and the Python pipeline around it are my hands: I wrote them myself and shipped the pipeline through the same code review as the engineers. I am a designer, not an engineer, and that is the point of this case. The judge, the scoring engine, and the infrastructure are the wider team’s; where the line between my hands and the team matters in this story, I say which is which.

02The problem

A perfect answer is not a passing grade.

Grading what an agent says is the easy half. The agents can also act, and the hardest failure to catch looks flawless. Here is one journey from the eval set, reconstructed on mock data, and it is the reason the key grades more than words.

Every word of that reply is correct, and the judge passes it on every dimension that scores the answer. The same journey holds a write: the agent moved where a company’s payroll lands, and nothing checked whether it was allowed to. So the ground truth for a journey spells out more than what the agent should say. It spells out what the agent may do: the actions the journey allows, and the verification each action requires. Authorization never averages into a score. One unverified write fails the row outright, and the release stays blocked, perfect answer and all.

Ground truth of that shape is expensive, and that is the problem this case is about. People were authoring every row by hand, and the agents were growing faster than the key.

03Why it mattered

Three weeks a dataset, and the errors hid in the typing.

One row of the key is not a sentence. As the agents learned to take actions, render widgets, and draw charts, the right answer grew to match, and a person hand-entered every field, one row at a time. The chart alone is a label and a value for every point on every axis.

A dataset took about three weeks to author, and that pace capped how much could be graded. Worse, the errors hid in the typing. A wrong ground truth is the most dangerous kind of error in an evaluation program, because it trains the judge to score incorrect behavior as correct, and the failure surfaces far downstream where it is hard to trace. And all of it was exactly the wrong use of an expert’s judgment: the people who knew what a good answer looks like were spending their weeks on data entry.

04Exploring solutions

Learn what good ground truth is before automating any of it.

I started with an audit of the hand-authored dataset, thousands of rows, looking for the repeating errors. They were systematic: answers written into the wrong field, missing simulator instructions on multi-turn journeys, mislabeled turns, format violations. Then I studied how the experts actually work: their training materials, their good-versus-bad example rows, and the criteria the team had agreed in calibration. I folded all of it into one brief a model could receive.

The shape I chose was a generator: a long instruction set for a language model run in agent mode, not a runnable script. An instruction set can carry judgment in a way a script cannot, and it can be iterated the way judgment is iterated: against real failures. Every version was tested on a small sample and spot-checked by the experts before any full run. It went through about seven versions in six weeks, each answering a real event: a working call that redefined what counts as a turn, feedback that it was doing too much, a teammate’s differently structured sheet.

05Design decisions

Draft like the best labeler, decide like none.

Three decisions define the generator. Each one exists because of a specific way this could have gone wrong.

  1. 5.1

    The machine drafts; the expert decides.

    Every generated value lands in a new recommended column. The original ground truth leaves the pipeline byte for byte as it entered, a check fails the run if that is ever untrue, and the expert promotes only the recommendations they trust. The experts’ trust in the tool rests on that guarantee.

  2. 5.2

    Reason like a senior labeler, not a form-filler.

    The rewrite that mattered most stopped telling the model to fill fields and started telling it to reason like the most experienced labeler on the team. When it got something wrong, I fixed the principle behind the miss, not the single example.

  3. 5.3

    Keep every call small enough to stay accurate.

    A sheet of more than a thousand rows is far too large for one model pass; quality degrades long before the context window overflows. The pipeline splits the sheet into small batches, drafts them in parallel, reassembles the sheet, and then has to prove the reassembly corrupted nothing.

The way I directed the model is the same discipline the identity asks of it. My note during one iteration, near verbatim: “Do not hyper-focus on fixing this one example. Update the generator’s principles so they extrapolate, as if training a superlative expert to follow principles, not specific rules for specific cases. Then commit it for versioning.”

06The build

A pipeline, twelve checks, and the bug review caught.

Around the generator sits a Python pipeline I wrote, in four phases: extract the rows, draft them in parallel batches, reassemble the sheet, and run 12 codified QA checks before a person sees anything. One of those checks is the guarantee from decision 5.1: it proves the original columns are byte for byte unchanged, every run. I shipped the pipeline through the same pull-request review the engineers use, which is where this story earns its keep.

Because that guarantee got its test in review, before the pipeline ever touched real data.

The lesson stayed with the program: a fallback path is a silent failure path in disguise. I had written the guarantee into the checks and then written a path around it without noticing. The review caught what I could not see from inside my own code, and the fix took hours precisely because the checks already existed to prove it.

07Results

What moved, and who measured it.

The team adopted the generator to pre-fill ground truth before the experts validate it. An answer key that had taken about three weeks to author by hand took about one week, across 3,000+ rows, and offline evaluation kept pace with the agents. On the far side of the key, the scores the program exists to move, moved.

The honest scope on those lifts: the program’s own judge did the measuring, I owned the datasets and the ground truth it grades against, and the quality gains belong to every team that shipped improvements against them. The turnaround number is the one that is mine.

The journey at the top of this page is the rare catch: a right answer that did a wrong thing. The everyday catch is quieter. A fluent, friendly answer that invents a payroll tax date and an amount, when the real answer depends on the filing schedule. It just sounds right. Ground truth is what lets the judge fail the row a person skimming would wave through.

08Learnings

What I learned, and what I would do differently.

Automate the drafting, never the judgment. The recommended-column design is the whole program in one decision: the machine does the first pass, the expert has the final say, and the tool earns trust by proving it cannot touch their work.

Direct a model with principles, not patches. Fixing the single failing example produces a prompt full of special cases. Fixing the principle behind the miss produces a generator that gets the next unseen case right too. That is also how I direct people, and it turned out to be the same skill.

Let people who did not write the code read the code. The silent-overwrite fallback was invisible to me and obvious to a reviewer. Shipping through the engineers’ review as a designer cost me some pride the first week and saved the program’s core guarantee.

One thing I would do differently: start from the exact sheet the experts already use. The seventh version of the generator matched their real column layout; the first version should have. Meeting the experts inside their own artifact, from day one, is cheaper than asking them to meet the tool.
