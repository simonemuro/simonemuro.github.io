Case 04 · The Quality Program agentic era · 2025 to 2026

To grade thousands of conversations, you need an answer key.

An AI support agent talks to small-business owners about their books, and it can act on their accounts. Before a new version ships, it is graded against the golden dataset: thousands of conversations with the right answer spelled out in full. People authored that key by hand. I automated the drafting.

Outcome
Measured quality up 8, 9.8 and 13 points across three agent skills
Throughput
Answer keys in about one week, down from about three, across 3,000+ rows
Role
Quality-eval team · owned the datasets and ground truth · authored the generator

01The decision

A perfect answer is not a passing grade.

The answer key exists to catch failures, and the hardest one to catch looked flawless. One journey from the eval set, reconstructed on mock data. The eval could score the words, flag the row for a person, or let the version ship and watch. Her call: stop the release.

The receipt

The key already spelled out what the agent should say. This row is why it also spells out what the agent may do: the actions each journey is allowed, and the verification each action requires. That check never averages into the score. One unverified write fails the row outright and blocks the release. The scorecard here is the receipt: three passes, one hard fail, and only the hard fail decided.

02The build

Teach the machine to draft like the team’s best labeler.

One row of ground truth is not a sentence. As the agent learned to take actions, render widgets, and draw charts, the right answer grew to match, and people hand-entered every field, one row at a time: the chart alone is a label and a value for every point on every axis. A dataset took about three weeks, the errors hid in the typing, and it was exactly the wrong use of an expert’s judgment.

one row of the key answer · what it should say actions · what it should do widgets · what it should render chart · every label and value

So I automated the drafting. I audited the hand-authored dataset for its repeating errors, studied how the experts actually work, and folded their training, their examples, and the team’s agreed criteria into one brief the model receives. The generator reads a conversation transcript and pre-fills the whole row; the experts validate instead of hand-enter.

One rewrite mattered most. I stopped telling it to fill fields and started telling it to reason like the most experienced labeler on the team. When it got something wrong, I fixed the principle behind the miss, not the single example.

Around the generator sits discipline. A Python pipeline splits a 1,000+ row sheet into small batches, drafts them in parallel, reassembles the sheet, and runs 12 codified QA checks before a person sees anything, so no call overflows a reliable context window. And every generated value lands in a new recommended column: the original ground truth leaves the pipeline byte for byte as it entered, a check fails the run if that is ever untrue, and the expert has the final say.

That guarantee got its test in review. Before the pipeline shipped, a reviewer caught a fallback path that could silently overwrite existing ground truth: exactly the failure the design promised away. I removed it within hours and replaced it with a safe promote step that only ever writes forward, all 12 checks passing. The lesson stayed with the program: a fallback path is a silent failure path in disguise.

03The proof

What moved, and who measured it.

The team adopted the generator to pre-fill ground truth before experts validate it. An answer key that had taken about three weeks to author by hand took about one week, across 3,000+ rows, and offline evaluation kept pace with the agents.

On the far side of the key, the scores the program exists to move, moved. Conversation quality rose 8 points on Help, 9.8 on Payroll, and 13 on Business Insights in a year. The honest scope: the program’s own judge did the measuring, I owned the datasets and the ground truth it grades against, and the lifts belong to every team that shipped against them.

The decision at the top of this page is the rare catch: a right answer that did a wrong thing. The everyday catch is quieter. It just sounds right: a fluent, friendly answer that invents a payroll tax date and an amount, when the real answer depends on the filing schedule. Ground truth is what lets the judge fail the row a person skimming would wave through.

04The role

Whose work this was.

On the AI quality-evaluation team, I owned the offline datasets and the ground truth, authored the generator and the LLM-as-judge simulator instructions, and partnered with stakeholders on the quality rubrics.

What I did not build: the eval infrastructure, the judge, and the release gate. Those were the wider team’s, and a quality program this size is never one person’s work.

The generator was mine. It is the reason evaluation kept pace with the agents.
