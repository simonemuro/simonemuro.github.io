No. 02 · The eval that decides whether an agent ships

To grade thousands of conversations, you need an answer key.

To know whether an AI support agent is good enough to ship, you need a graded answer for thousands of conversations. At a large fintech, humans were hand-authoring that answer key. It didn't scale, and it couldn't catch its own mistakes. I built the practice that fixed both: the generators that draft the answer key, the simulator standard, and the human-labeler program, feeding the team's LLM-as-judge and a release gate.

Hand-authoring the answer key didn't scale, and it couldn't catch itself.

An AI support agent talks to small-business owners about their books. To know whether a new version is safe to ship, you compare what it says to what the right answer was, conversation after conversation, across thousands of them. That right answer is the golden dataset: the answer key.

People were writing that key by hand, one row at a time. It was slow, it capped how much I could grade, and a hand-written key has no second pair of eyes. If a human got an entry wrong, every judgment built on it was wrong too, and nothing flagged it.

How a release gets graded, end to end.

The pipeline is one line. A golden dataset and a user-simulator drive the agent through real conversations. The agent answers. An LLM-as-judge scores each answer against the ground truth on a weighted rubric. The scores roll up to one quality number, and that number decides: ship, or hold.

This is what the gate is for: catching a confident wrong answer.

A customer asks a simple question. The agent answers with total confidence and invents the specifics, a date and an amount that were never in the data. To a person skimming, it reads fine. The judge doesn't skim. It compares the answer to the ground truth, the no-hallucination and correctness dimensions fail, the quality score drops below the bar, and the release is blocked.

My differentiator: I built the machine that drafts the answer key.

Here's the part I'm a designer, not an engineer, for. I wrote a pipeline that drafts the answer key instead of hand-writing it. It pulls each case, has AI draft the four ground-truth fields per row in parallel batches, assembles the result, and runs it through 12 codified QA checks that fail the build like a test suite. Originals are never overwritten. People then validate the drafts instead of authoring from scratch. It shipped through the same review process as the engineers' code.

The judge isn't one opinion. It's a weighted scorecard.

The judge scores every answer on multiple dimensions, and they don't count equally. Being right is weighted far above everything else. Not inventing things, being complete, and staying on topic each matter, but less. Compliance and tone matter too, and they're the lightest. Those weights combine into one quality score, and that score is the ship-or-hold bar.

The other half: real traffic, labeled by trained humans.

The offline gate is only half the picture. Real customers ask things no answer key anticipated, so I ran the online side too: a small, stratified sample of real conversations, scored by trained humans. I wrote the instructions and the training, onboarded and monitored the labelers, and coordinated internal labeling alongside two labeling vendors. Failures flow back into the golden set on a roughly weekly cadence, so the answer key keeps learning from production.

The bug that taught the lesson.

My pipeline had a fallback path that, in one case, would silently overwrite existing ground truth instead of failing. No error, no flag, just a quietly corrupted answer key poisoning every judgment downstream. A reviewer caught it. I removed the fallback, shipped a safe auto-promote in its place in a few hours, and every one of the 12 checks passed. The fix was easy. The lesson stuck.

What I built and led

  • The golden-dataset practice: the answer key the judge grades against
  • The generators that draft ground truth, with 12 QA checks and a no-overwrite guarantee
  • The user-simulator standard the judge tests the agent against
  • The human-labeler program: training, onboarding, monitoring, the weekly refresh

What I'm careful not to claim

  • The eval infrastructure, the runtime and data pipeline, was data science and engineering
  • The weighted metric framework itself was a cross-functional artifact; I authored the labeler training and the generation system, not the metric's invention
  • The vendor labelers I coordinated, I did not manage them

A designer who builds. I set the bar and built the system that holds it. The work came down to one thing: making "good enough to ship" a thing a machine could decide, on real conversations, before a single one reached a customer.
