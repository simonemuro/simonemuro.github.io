Case No. 03  ·  Ground truth  ·  Intuit Intelligence · quality team

The answer sounded right.

A fluent, confident answer invented a date about a customer's payroll taxes. Catching that takes one thing: a checked answer to compare against. I owned the ground truth that an entire agentic platform measured its quality against, and built the generators that let it scale across every skill.

Problem

Fluent answers that are confidently wrong, and still read as right.

My scope

The offline ground truth, human-validated, that measured conversation quality across every skill.

The build

Generators that pre-fill ground truth to spec, so experts validate and correct instead of authoring from blank rows.

Outcome

Conversation quality rose +8 to 13 points across three skills, on the ground truth I built.

01
The catch

Sounds right. Fails the bar.

Grade an answer on how it reads, and a customer gets told their taxes were filed on a day that never happened. Three gates pass. The one that matters catches the invented date, and it can only catch it because there is a checked answer to compare against: the ground truth.

02
What I owned

The answer key everything was scored against.

Ground truth is the north-star answer each model is graded against: the scenario, the answer it should give, and the actions it should take. I created it, validated it human-in-the-loop, verified it with the experts, kept it from going stale, and added rows as the platform grew. I also expanded what “quality” even measured, from accuracy, completeness, and relevance out to personalization, the right tool calls, and the right actions.

One ground-truth rowMock data
The scenario
A customer asks whether their 941 federal deposit was filed, and when.
The answer it should give
State the filing and its status exactly as the record holds. Do not assert a date the record doesn't carry; offer where the dated confirmation lives.
→Expected actions · look up the filing record before answering
→Graded on · correctness · completeness · relevance · personalization · tool calls · actions

What “good” meant grew over time. Early rows scored accuracy, completeness, and relevance. As skills started acting on a customer's account, the ground truth had to grade personalization, whether the right tool was called, and whether the right action was taken.

03
The build

I made the slowest step a review, not a rewrite.

As the program grew, hand-authoring didn't scale. Experts burned their time on error-prone data entry, filling chart, widget, and graph evaluation formats by hand, instead of on judgment. So I codified it. I gathered the ground-truth guidelines, the expert-training docs, the meeting notes, the Slack threads, and the team's decisions about how each output should be graded, and turned them into a generator prompt that pre-fills every row to spec. Blank-page authoring became review-and-correct, and the experts got their judgment back.

The bug that mattered

One path could silently overwrite a human's validated ground truth with model output, no audit trail, quietly poisoning every judge built on it. I caught it in review, deleted the path so originals are never touched, added a check that fails the run if it's ever violated, and shipped the fix in about four hours.

It runs every day, on every skill.

I also automated the script that feeds the simulator. When the pipeline runs, the bot follows my script to trigger a flow and play the customer, and an LLM-as-judge rates the answer against the ground truth. Every skill, every release.

05
The rigor

I tested the judge, and re-audited the truth.

Two things can rot underneath a quality number: a judge you never checked, and a ground truth you stopped questioning. I validated the LLM judges against 200 human-labeled tasks before trusting them. Once they cleared the bar, the planned 2,000-row vendor labeling wasn't justified, so it didn't happen. And I re-audited the ground truth blind, a fresh KEEP-or-DELETE call on every row with the original label hidden, so no one could anchor on the first answer.
