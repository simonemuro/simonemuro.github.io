Building the quality loop for agentic AI

I led and expanded a quality-evaluation practice that connected golden datasets, source-of-truth answers, LLM-as-judge workflows, human labeling, production samples, and product readouts into a model-improvement loop.

Role

Quality methodology lead, model UX writer, evaluation framework owner, team lead.

Methods

Golden datasets, human labeling, LLM-as-judge, rubrics, production samples, failure analysis.

Outcomes

Quality score lifts across Help (+8 pp), Payroll (+9.8 pp), and Insights (+13 pp).

Context

As agentic AI scaled across support skills, teams needed a reliable way to define and measure response quality. LLM-as-judge was useful, but not enough for product, financial, and account-sensitive workflows.

The organization needed a shared system for ground truth, human review, quality gates, and a way to move failures back into prompts, tools, support content, and evaluation datasets.

Quality principle Evaluation is not reporting. It is how the model gets better.

Approach

I helped turn response quality from a subjective conversation into a repeatable operating loop that product, design, data science, and domain experts could use together.

01

Define scenarios

Translate agent capabilities and customer scenarios into representative evaluation rows.

02

Write ground truth

Capture expected answers, actions, constraints, data requirements, and handoff triggers.

03

Judge and label

Combine LLM-as-judge with human review where nuance, correctness, or risk requires judgment.

04

Fix behavior

Route failures back into prompts, tools, ground truth, product requirements, or support content.

Key decisions

Separate correctness from conversation quality

A response can be factually correct and still be too generic, too verbose, poorly sequenced, or missing a required next step.

Use human labeling where judgment matters

Human review helped catch nuance the judge could miss: tone, unsupported assumptions, missing context, and whether a customer could actually act on the answer.

Treat disagreement as signal

Failures and judge-human disagreements became prompts for better rubrics, clearer ground truth, and sharper model behavior.

Failure taxonomy

Correct but unhelpful The answer is true but too generic to solve the user’s actual problem.
Ungrounded The response implies customer-specific facts the system has not verified.
Missing action The user needs the system to ask, act, retrieve, route, or hand off, not only explain.
Poor recovery The AI keeps answering when it should clarify, refuse, or escalate with context.
