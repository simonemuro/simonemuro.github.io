Model UX · AI Prototyping · Subscription Support

Building account-aware subscription support from the instruction layer up

I built an end-to-end AI support proof of concept that moved billing help from generic guidance to grounded account-aware behavior: classify the intent, retrieve safe account context, explain the charge, and offer the right next step when cancellation intent appears.

Role

Solo POC builder, model UX writer, prompt/skill designer, conversation designer.

Scope

2026 prototype sprint for subscription billing, charge explanation, cancellation intent, and handoff.

Proof

Working prototype, 16-scenario golden dataset, skill instructions, tool-call plan, and demo flow.

Context

Customers asking about billing or subscription charges were getting generic help that pushed them away from the support experience. That is risky in any subscription business: the customer is confused, worried about money, and often one poor answer away from cancellation.

The problem was not only the visible response. The AI needed a better operating model for when to use account context, when to ask for clarification, when to avoid inventing details, and when to route to a human or product flow.

Behavior goal Answer, explain, and route without hallucinating account facts.

Approach

I treated the prototype as an agent-behavior problem. The experience needed a decision path before it needed polished copy.

01

Classify the billing question

Separate charge explanation, plan detail, cancellation intent, unknown charge, and unsupported account requests.

02

Retrieve safe context

Use account data only when grounded; ask or hand off when the system does not have the facts.

03

Explain the charge

Say what changed, why it changed, and what the customer can do next in plain, non-defensive language.

04

Confirm before acting

When cancellation intent appears, confirm the customer’s goal and provide a safe path instead of rushing the action.

Key decisions

Design the model’s permissions, not just its tone

I wrote instructions for what the skill was allowed to answer, when it needed account data, and when it should say it could not verify a charge.

Use the golden dataset as a design artifact

The 16-scenario dataset forced the behavior to be testable: happy path, missing data, vague questions, unsupported asks, and cancellation intent.

Make cancellation respectful

The flow did not trap the customer. It acknowledged intent, explained available options, and created a clearer bridge to the next step.

Example behavior pattern

Customer intent “Why was my subscription charge higher this month?”
System check Look for plan change, discount expiration, billing cycle change, or missing account data.
Response rule Explain only grounded facts; avoid guessing; offer plan review or expert handoff when needed.
Quality bar Specific, concise, non-defensive, and clear about what the customer can do next.

Result

The POC showed that a designer could move beyond wireframes into the agent layer: skill instructions, scenarios, tool-use logic, response strategy, and a demoable customer experience.

What this proves

I can design the instructions and evidence that shape AI behavior, then build enough of the experience for teams to critique and improve it early.
