Case No. 02 billing skill Agentic support platform

Turning billing anxiety into an account-aware agent prototype.

I knew the problem from building in QuickBooks Digital Assistant: customers were asking about charges they did not recognize, refunds, cancellations, and pricing, and the assistant could mostly send them to settings. When a company-wide challenge asked teams to increase build velocity and deliver customer value faster, I used that real problem space to build a billing skill: a working proof of concept that let the agent read billing context, explain what changed, and route safely when the moment became cancellation or refund risk.

01Background

Why billing was worth solving.

The billing problem did not start at the hackathon. I had already worked on the Digital Assistant path for Intuit Fees: a high-volume support area where customers asked why they were charged, why the amount changed, how to get a refund, or how to cancel.

The business case was not abstract. Billing, cancel, and refund questions represented more than one million annual support contacts in the SMB support data I used. They were also emotionally loaded: customers felt fear, frustration, and loss of control when they saw an unfamiliar charge and could not get a clear explanation.

The first DA MVP could improve classification and point customers to billing history, but it could not answer the deeper question itself. The target state required account-aware agent behavior: read the customer’s plan, add-ons, next billing date, and charge history, then explain the why in plain language.

02The problem

The assistant was answering around the problem.

Before-state testing showed the gap clearly. When a customer asked about an unfamiliar charge, the assistant could answer with broad billing guidance or plausible generic reasons instead of the customer’s actual plan, add-ons, and recent billing history. In one case, it misunderstood the billing question and searched reports instead.

Those answers were not malicious. They were under-informed. Without billing data, the assistant could only give broad steps, link to settings, or speculate. For a money question, that creates the exact experience customers came to support to escape: slow, generic, and not grounded in their account.

The design challenge was therefore not just conversation polish. It was agent capability: give the model a safe way to look up account facts, then constrain what it could do with them.

03Why it mattered

The hackathon was a way to prove the target state.

The DA MVP had a pragmatic strategy: classify the customer’s billing question better, respond with empathy, and deep-link to billing history while richer data access remained future work. That was the right near-term move, but it still left the customer doing the detective work.

The billing skill was the hackathon answer to the next question: what would it look like if the assistant could actually know the customer? Not in a vague personalization sense, but in a product-safe way: plan, add-ons, billing date, recent charge history, and admin boundaries.

The internal challenge was about build velocity: the classic path from idea to requirements, design, build, test, review, and release had too much translation work, coordination churn, and low-value execution. The billing skill was the right kind of proof because I could turn a known roadmap problem into something people could run, inspect, and evaluate in days.

The value of the POC was not that it shipped. It proved the shape of the experience, the tool contract, the handoff boundary, and the eval surface needed to make account-aware billing support real.

04Exploring solutions

What I built in the hackathon.

I built a local three-part stack: a billing lookup server, a production-format remote skill definition, and a Streamlit test UI that let me run the agent against a controlled test account. The skill returned account-specific billing facts and used a confirmation widget when the customer wanted to cancel.

The cancel widget mattered because cancellation is not only an information request. It is a decision moment. The assistant should not trap the customer, but it should create a measurable save opportunity: connect to a billing specialist, or show self-serve steps if the customer wants to proceed alone.

  1. ToolRead plan name, status, next billing date, add-ons, and recent billing facts.
  2. SkillHandle unrecognized-charge, pricing, duplicate-charge, cancel, refund, and escalation intents.
  3. WidgetOffer specialist handoff or self-serve steps for cancel intent.
  4. EvalFail when the agent guesses, skips the lookup, loops the widget, or crosses policy boundaries.

06The build

Skill, server, eval gate.

The skill is a spec before it is code. Behind it sits the billing lookup tool. I learned the protocol, stood up a local server, registered the tools, and debugged the agent until calls landed end to end.

The real work was not making the demo look convincing. It was catching the moments where the model sounded helpful but crossed the product boundary.

Model behavior bug

Formula leak

The local run exposed raw pricing logic in the customer-facing answer. Instead of saying the payroll add-on was 75 dollars, the response showed calculation syntax in the table details.

Payroll Core · 75 dollars · 45base + 6 x 5 employees
Time Premium · 50 dollars · 20base + 10 x 3 users

Design correction: customer-facing answers needed plain-language totals, not raw pricing logic or calculation syntax.

Boundary bug

Unsafe downgrade advice

A price-pressure test turned into cost-reduction advice. The agent suggested a downgrade even though plan changes can remove features, break workflows, affect add-ons, and create business risk.

Steps to lower your subscription cost
1. Review add-ons and user counts
2. Consider downgrading your plan
3. Remove unused add-ons

Design correction: informational billing questions should explain the bill. Cost-saving or plan-change paths belong to an explicit customer request or a specialist conversation.

Prototype evidence

I tested the build, not the deck.

I used Cursor and the model as build partners, then treated every generated behavior as something to verify. I ran the local harness in Streamlit, pointed it at the agent stack, and tested the hard cases: unfamiliar charge, changed amount, cancel intent, no-thanks fallback, and saves-expert handoff. The raw screenshots include private run metadata, so this is reconstructed with mock IDs.

  • HarnessStreamlit chat surface with endpoint selection, clear chat, and conversation export.
  • ScenariosUnrecognized charge, changed amount, cancel subscription, cancel account, show me the steps.
  • SignalsRun time, tool trace, answer grounding, escalation boundary, and whether the response stayed inside the right intent.

The eval came with the skill, not after it. The dataset covered billing admin and non-admin cases, cancel flows, escalation paths, tool failures, and edge conditions. It gave me a way to judge whether the agent used account facts, avoided guesses, and routed safely.

08Learnings

What I learned, and what I would do differently.

Domain knowledge was the shortcut. The hackathon only worked because the problem was already familiar from DA research, transcripts, before-state testing, and the earlier billing MVP.

An AI pair speeds up the typing, not the judgment. The hard part was still deciding what the agent should know, when it should act, when it should stop, and how to prove whether it behaved correctly.

The widget was a product decision, not a decoration. Cancel intent needed an explicit choice point so the customer could proceed on their own or connect with a specialist without the agent overstepping.

If I continued the work, I would harden the permission checks and admin boundaries inside the tool layer, then run the eval against a broader set of billing, refund, cancel, and non-admin scenarios before any production pilot.
