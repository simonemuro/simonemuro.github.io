Case No. 01 Subscription billing Digital Assistant → Intuit Intelligence

The charge customers didn’t recognize.

I took two runs at one of QuickBooks support’s biggest problems: the assistant’s answer to “what is this charge?” First a production redesign that moved the numbers. Then a working agent skill with a 108-case eval. The problem behind both is the same. A charge from Intuit lands on a bank statement, the customer doesn’t recognize it, opens the assistant, and gets told to go check a settings page. When Intuit Intelligence replaced the Digital Assistant, I built the billing skill that reads the account and explains the charge itself: the skill definition, the lookup tool, the test harness, and the eval that judged whether it behaved.

Surface
About 800K annual billing and subscription support contacts; one of the top twenty drivers of a company-wide contact-reduction goal
My role
Conversation AI lead. Research and conversation design on the production redesign; on the skill, the whole build: behavior spec, tool, harness, and eval
When
Digital Assistant redesign shipped to production; billing skill built during a five-day internal hackathon, May 2026
Results
Production redesign: voice self-help acceptance 25% to 39%; chat self-help resolution up 12 points; chat containment near 61%. Hackathon skill: working prototype, 108-case eval, leadership buy-in to implement

01The problem

A money question, answered with directions.

Billing questions aren’t like other support questions. The customer can see money leaving their account and can’t see why, so they arrive worried and a little suspicious. What they got back was navigation: go to Settings, open Billing and Subscription, read your account history.

The answer wasn’t wrong. It just handed the investigation back to the person who came for help. And when self-help failed, these contacts were expensive ones: billing calls moved between expert tiers more than almost any other topic, because discount extensions and refunds needed a higher tier than the person who picked up.

02The redesign that shipped

Learn from the experts, then stop sending people away.

I started where the good answers already lived: expert calls. I analyzed more than 200 expert transcripts and 500 customer utterances to learn what experts checked, what they explained, and what next step they offered. Experts didn’t point at settings. They named the charge, explained what changed, and offered something concrete.

The synthesis became the design brief for the Digital Assistant redesign.

Recreated from my research synthesis; internal system names removed.

The product decision followed from the research. One billing topic isn’t one customer question: a price increase, a duplicate charge, a charge they don’t understand, and “how much am I paying” each deserve different treatment. The full answer needed billing APIs the assistant didn’t have yet, so I scoped a phased plan: classify the actual question first, stop linking articles, and offer the existing charge lookup tool inside the conversation, with language that made it worth accepting.

03New platform, same gap

Intuit Intelligence arrived. The billing answer didn’t.

In 2026, Intuit Intelligence replaced the Digital Assistant as the support surface. It was a stronger platform in almost every way, and it still answered billing questions generically, because it had the same missing piece: no access to the customer’s account.

the tell“Depends on your plan and any add-ons you use.” The system knows the plan. The answer doesn’t use it.

missing dataPlan, add-ons, team size, promo state, next billing date: none of it appears.

same endingThe customer gets sent to a pricing page and a settings link to do the investigation themselves.

Conversation polish couldn’t close this gap. The assistant needed a safe way to read billing facts, and rules for what it could do with them. When a five-day internal hackathon asked what we could prove quickly, I brought this problem and built the answer: a skill, a tool, and the eval that checks them.

04Five days, a working skill

Skill, tool, harness. Built and running.

I built the billing skill solo during the hackathon week: a skill definition in the production format, a read-only billing lookup tool that returns plan, add-ons, team members, recent charges, and promo state, and a local chat harness to run the agent end to end. I learned MCP, the protocol connecting the tool to the agent, from scratch that week.

The live billing API needed permissions that sat in another team’s queue, so the tool ran in three modes: LIVE for the real APIs, stubbed until permissions cleared; EVAL for scenario injection; DEV for mock data. That let me test the behavior end to end while the integration waited.

Here it is running. Same question type the Digital Assistant used to deflect, now answered from account data, with the math done for the customer.

Two behaviors surfaced in testing that looked helpful and weren’t.

caught in testing · 01

The formula leak

Asked about an add-on’s cost, the model answered with its own arithmetic showing. Correct numbers, wrong register: nobody’s bill should read like source code.

Payroll Core $75 45base + 6 x 5 employees Time Premium $50 20base + 10 x 3 users

The rule that fixed it: never show raw math, formulas, or calculation syntax. Write the result in plain English, then explain what changed.

caught in testing · 02

Unasked-for downgrade advice

A price-pressure test turned into cost-cutting coaching: the model suggested downgrading the plan and trimming users, unprompted. Plan changes can remove features and break workflows, and the customer hadn’t asked.

Steps to lower your subscription cost 1. Review add-ons and user counts 2. Consider downgrading your plan

The rule that fixed it: explain the bill; don’t volunteer plan changes. Downgrades get discussed when the customer asks, with the tradeoffs on the table.

05The cancel moment

“I want to cancel” isn’t an information request.

Cancellation was the one intent where the skill had to make a judgment call, not just an explanation. I’d spent years on this exact moment: my earlier cancellation-saves work taught me that the reason behind a cancel decides everything downstream. So the skill assessed the customer’s stated reason before responding.

A life circumstance, closing the business or retiring, means the decision is final. The skill confirms the plan and billing date, gives cancellation steps filtered to what’s actually on the account, and closes with an offer of help through the process. It doesn’t recite the full cost breakdown, and it doesn’t open with “I’m sorry to hear that.” Retirement isn’t bad news, and a scripted condolence reads as exactly that: scripted.

Price pressure or dissatisfaction means the situation might be fixable. There the skill surfaces the full account picture, makes one clear offer to connect with a billing specialist in its first response, and keeps self-serve steps available in the same breath. The customer always has both doors. What it never does is volunteer a downgrade to save money: that’s a business decision with real tradeoffs, and it belongs to the customer and a human.

06The eval

Every behavior above is a row you can check.

I wrote the eval alongside the skill. Each case pairs a customer message with expectations written as checkable statements: what the agent must do, and what it must not. The cases came from the same place as the research: real customer language, including 55 production conversations rewritten onto the mock account. The set grew to 108 cases across 13 intent categories, and I ran an overlap analysis against the seven existing skills so the new one wouldn’t collide with behavior already in production.

Running it earned its keep quickly. Beyond the two prompt fixes above, testing against the cases caught the agent calling tools in the wrong order and mishandling the moment a customer declines the specialist and still wants the steps. Both became rules, then rows, so they can’t quietly come back.

The cases are written in the production golden-dataset format, the same format skill teams contribute for release gating: LLM judges score each row across six dimensions, correctness weighted heaviest, and a run needs at least 80% to pass, with correctness, completeness, and compliance thresholds that can block a skill on their own. I ran the offline eval against the agent with the billing skill loaded. It passed.

07Where it landed

Buy-in, 80% built, then I left.

hackathon

Submitted and judged at Intuit’s internal 3X PM/XD Hackathon, May 2026: working prototype, cancel-intercept widget, and the 108-case eval.

leadership review

Presented the prototype and the process to my group manager, the director, and product managers, and got buy-in to implement the skill with product development.

state of the build

About 80% of the way to shippable, by the remaining-work list. The skill definition, the lookup tool, and the golden dataset were built. What remained: wiring the server into the production stack and clearing API permissions.

the ending

I left Intuit before implementation started. The skill stayed where I handed it: a working prototype, an eval to hold it accountable, and a leadership decision to build it.

What I’d do differently: start the API-permissions request on day one instead of when the tool needed it, harden the admin and non-admin checks inside the tool layer rather than the prompt, and widen the eval’s refund and non-admin coverage before any production pilot.

What the week settled for me: a conversation designer with the production repo, a coding agent, and real customer knowledge can carry a skill from problem to evaluated behavior. Deciding what the agent should do was still the slow, valuable part. The typing was never the bottleneck.
