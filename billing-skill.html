Case No. 01 BillingSkill QuickBooks support

From a page of links to a real answer.

Customers asking about a charge got a list of help articles. Most gave up and called. I designed the answer they should have gotten: what the charge is, why the amount changed, and what to do next, in one reply.

Surface
The QuickBooks support assistant, subscription billing
Role
Conversation designer for the surface; later built the agent skill solo, AI-paired
Scale
About 800,000 billing support contacts a year
Results
Self-help resolution up 12 points; containment about 61%

01Background

Where this work sat, and what I owned.

QuickBooks is accounting software for small businesses. Inside it sits a support assistant, the chat people open when something looks wrong. Billing is one of the top reasons they open it: a charge is higher than usual, a subscription renewed, an amount on the statement does not match what they expected.

The people asking are small business owners and the office managers who keep their books. They are busy, and they are asking about their own money.

My role, stated plainly: I owned the conversation design for billing support in the assistant. I decided what the answers should say, made the boundary calls, and later built a working agent skill for this surface myself, end to end, with an AI pair for the code.

The assistant recognized the topic and handed back a reading list. The customer had to open each article, find their own situation inside it, and do the math alone. Most did not. They called.

The part that made this hard to accept: the account already held the answer. The plan, the add-on, the date it changed. The assistant never looked.

And this is not a small surface. Subscription billing drives about 800,000 support contacts a year.

03Why it mattered

The cost of a page of links.

A deflected chat becomes a phone call, and a phone call costs many times what a chat answer costs. At this volume, every point of deflection is real money.

The quieter cost is trust. A customer who cannot understand a charge starts to believe the product is careless with their money. Many billing complaints are not questions at all. They are cancellation decisions already in progress, wearing a billing question.

So the problem was never writing better articles. It was answering the question.

04Exploring solutions

What we considered, and the wall we hit.

There were three ways to go. Write better help articles: cheap, but the customer still does the work. Build a guided flow for every billing question: better, but rigid, and each new question means new screens. Or let the assistant read the account and answer the specific question. I wanted the third one, so I scoped it with evidence: more than 500 labeled customer utterances, grouped and ranked, and one target chosen for volume and data dependency. Subscription charges.

Then the wall. Mid-build, the live billing API answered with a permission block, and access sat weeks deep in someone else’s queue. I did not wait. I designed the lookup tool with three modes: live for the real API, eval for scenario injection, dev for mock data. Evaluation stopped depending on access. The live mode stayed stubbed behind a documented blocker, and nothing else stopped.

05Design decisions

The conversation design itself.

Four decisions define the after experience. Each one is visible in the recreated conversation in part seven.

  1. 5.1

    Answer first, then the detail.

    The first sentence names the charge and why it changed. The breakdown rows come after, for anyone who wants to check the math. Nobody has to scroll to learn the answer.

  2. 5.2

    The reasoning collapses to one line.

    The lookups the agent ran sit behind a quiet Work done line. A customer can open it and see what was checked. Nobody is made to read it.

  3. 5.3

    Hand off when the exit shows.

    When a billing question is really a cancellation decision, the skill reads the account, gives the real steps, and offers a person. A specialist can save an account. A chatbot arguing cannot.

  4. 5.4

    There are things the agent must never do.

    It never cancels for you. The handoff card closes with two choices, talk to a specialist or keep my plan, and neither one is cancel. Payment capture stays out of the channel entirely. And every amount the agent states must trace to account data. No guessed numbers.

06The build

Skill, server, eval gate.

The skill is a spec before it is code: tone, when to use, graceful handling, and an admin check, so admins get charge detail and everyone else gets a safe redirect. Behind the spec sits the billing lookup tool. In production the platform reaches its tools over MCP, so I learned the protocol from scratch, stood up a local MCP server, registered it, and watched the agent discover the tools and call them end to end.

It broke once in a way worth telling. The skill loaded clean. The server ran. And not one tool call arrived.

The eval came with the skill, not after it. Every row in the golden dataset carries a scenario, the mock account data the tool returns, and gating criteria an LLM judge scores. The set started at 33 rows, scoped to subscription charges. Then 55 real production conversations reset the map: cancel intent, price complaints, downgrades. The set grew to 108 cases across 13 intent categories, and the skill grew from an answer engine into an intent router.

The eval is the gate. A change that fails a row does not ship.

08Learnings

What I learned, and what I would do differently.

Production conversations beat my assumptions. My first 33 eval rows tested the skill I had planned. The 55 real conversations showed the skill customers needed, and it was a different one. Next time I start with the transcripts.

An AI pair speeds up the typing, not the judgment. The config bug was invisible from inside the pair; I found it by stopping and reading the code. Knowing what to verify yourself is the actual skill.

Never let evaluation depend on access. The three-mode tool design came out of a blocked API, and it is the part of the build I would keep exactly as it is.

Two things I would do differently. I would request API access on day one, not when the build hit the wall. And I would push the admin check down into the tool itself, so the boundary lives in code instead of instructions.
