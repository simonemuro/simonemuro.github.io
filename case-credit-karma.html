Credit Karma: prompt systems under hard legal constraints

From August to late 2024 I did a prompt-engineering rotation at Credit Karma (an Intuit company), working on production GenAI features in the most constrained writing environment I've encountered: where the model is legally forbidden from saying things it knows.

Role
Prompt engineer (rotation, Aug–late 2024)
Surface
"See Why" card explanations · "Analyze My Wallet"
What I did
Prompt authoring & iteration, synthetic test profiles, rubric-graded evaluation
Workflow
Prompts versioned in git, shipped through merged PRs

The setting

Two production GenAI features: "See Why," which explains why a specific credit card is recommended to a member, and "Analyze My Wallet," which reads a member's whole card portfolio and suggests how to improve it. I authored and iterated prompts for both, joining work in motion, not inventing it from zero. The job was to make a model's explanation of someone's money personalized, plainly written, and legally airtight, all at once.

To regression-test that personalization actually held, I built persona-spanning synthetic test profiles (invented members from sub-prime through prime) so every prompt revision could be checked across credit bands, not just against the happy-path member it was tuned on.

Craft beat one: the same feature needed two different prompt architectures

The least obvious lesson of the rotation: a prompt is written for a model, not just for a task. The same feature ran on two models, and they needed structurally different prompts to produce equivalent behavior.

gpt-3.5-turboHeavy chain-of-thought scaffolding: "devise a plan… solve step by step." The model had to be walked through its own reasoning or it skipped steps and drifted off the member's data.
gpt-4oRestructured to clean numbered instructions + an explicit response format. The scaffolding that rescued 3.5 was dead weight here: clarity and format did the work.
the pointSame feature, same constraints, two prompt architectures. Porting a prompt between models is a redesign, not a copy-paste.

Craft beat two: reason, but don't say

The sharpest constraint was legal. The model could reason over approval-odds data (it shaped which explanations made sense) but it was legally forbidden from stating it. No "you're likely to be approved," ever, even when the data said so. Writing that behavior is harder than a flat prohibition: the prompt has to let the signal inform the output while keeping it out of the words. That reason-but-don't-say pattern is, to me, the essence of regulated-domain prompting.

Craft beat three: hard output constraints as hallucination prevention

The other guardrails were structural, and that's what made them reliable. Strict JSON output. No arithmetic allowed: the model never computes a number, because a model doing math about someone's money is a hallucination waiting to be screenshotted. And a mandatory annual-fee disclosure, even when the fee is $0. The constraint that's easy to forget is the one the rubric made impossible to forget.

Every output was graded against a three-tier rubric:

goodReferences the member's actual data · correct card category · genuine lifestyle fit · states the annual fee (even when $0) · no hallucinations, no math, no approval-odds mentions.
acceptableCompliant and accurate, but generic. It misses the personalization that makes the explanation worth reading.
unacceptableAny hallucinated detail, any arithmetic, any approval-odds statement, or a missing annual-fee disclosure. One strike fails the output.

What a passing explanation looks like

"See Why" · synthetic example: invented card, invented member data
Why is the Meridian Everyday Cash card recommended for me?
You spend most on groceries and gas, and your current card earns a flat 1% on both. Meridian Everyday Cash earns 3% in exactly those two categories, so it fits how you actually spend. It has a $0 annual fee. grounded in member data · category fit · fee stated even at $0 · no odds, no math

Entirely synthetic: the card, the member, and the copy are invented to illustrate the rubric's bar. Production prompts and member data are confidential.

Prompts shipped like code, tested like a product

The workflow was engineering, not vibes: prompts versioned in git and shipped through merged PRs, tested through the platform's prompt playground and internal fishfood before anything reached members. For Analyze My Wallet, a 10-participant unmoderated user study drove the P0 changes: move the recommended cards earlier in the response, and shorten "tell me more" into scannable bullets. Members wanted the answer first and the essay never.

What this proves

I won't dress this one up with outcome metrics, because none exist for my pieces. The rotation ended before post-launch numbers attached to anything I'd authored, and I don't claim numbers I can't show. What the work proves is craft under constraint: model-specific prompt architecture, behavior rules that thread a legal needle, structure as hallucination prevention, and an engineering-grade workflow around all of it. Regulated finance is where behavior design earns its keep: when the model genuinely can't say everything it knows, every word that remains has to be designed.
