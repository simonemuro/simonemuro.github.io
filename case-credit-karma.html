Prompt Engineering LLM Response Quality Regulated Finance

Teaching an LLM to explain money — without overstepping the law.

As Credit Karma's prompt engineer, I authored and continuously refined two shipped LLM experiences: "See Why", which explains why a specific credit card is recommended, and "Analyze My Wallet," which reads a member's whole financial profile and suggests how to improve it. Both had to be personalized, persuasive, plainly written — and legally airtight.

My role
Prompt Engineer (IC)
Surface
Card marketplace + embedded financial assistant
What I did
Prompt authoring & versioning, structured output, tool-grounding, QE evaluation
Brand & year
Credit Karma · 2024
The problem

A recommendation a member can't understand isn't a recommendation.

Credit Karma members were being shown credit-card recommendations they couldn't make sense of. Members couldn't easily see the strengths and weaknesses of the wallet they already had, and when a new card was suggested, the "why" was generic — the same benefits language anyone would see, disconnected from their actual finances.

The fix had to be a genuinely personalized, generative explanation. But this is a regulated commerce surface, and that turned a writing problem into a model-behavior problem with three hard edges:

  • Legal limits on claims. Credit Karma is legally bound not to misrepresent approval odds. The model could reason over them, but could never state or guarantee them.
  • No invented offers. The model had to recommend only cards present in approved offer data — never a card it "knew about" from training.
  • Real personalization, plainly said. Answers had to reflect the member's true profile — score, utilization, the specific cards in their wallet — and explain APR, rewards categories, and fees in language a non-expert could act on.
My role

I owned the prompts end to end — and the discipline around them.

  • Authored both system prompts from the product's response requirements: the persona (a personal-finance analyst focused on card optimization), the step-by-step reasoning, and a strict JSON output schema (overview · details[] · summary).
  • Versioned every change in my own Git repo, tracking each prompt revision alongside its test inputs and the model's outputs, so every change was attributable rather than remembered.
  • Worked baseline-first: I ran the unedited prompt against a member profile before changing anything, so I could isolate the effect of each revision instead of trusting a hunch.
  • Stress-tested edge cases — members with no cards, no matched offer data, thin files, and prime members who shouldn't see secured-card offers — to find where the model hedged badly or broke.
  • Tuned tool-invocation reliability so the model consistently called the offer tool instead of answering from memory.
  • Took the prompts through Quality-Engineering evaluation against a multi-dimensional rubric and raised the production PRs that shipped them.
The key decision

I made the model say less, on purpose.

The sharpest decision was about grounding. In testing, I found that whenever offer data was missing, the model would cheerfully recommend cards anyway — pulling them from its own training knowledge, with details it had no way to verify. On a regulated commerce surface, a confident, unverifiable recommendation is worse than no recommendation at all.

So I constrained the prompt and the tool-invocation behavior to a hard rule: recommend only cards present in the approved offer data, and say nothing when that data is absent. I deliberately traded breadth and apparent helpfulness for verifiable accuracy and compliance.

The same instinct drove a later revision. Legal reviewed the comparison language the model used when it weighed a recommended card against the member's current cards. I rewrote it so the model can still make a comparison persuasive — but only with claims it can substantiate from the data in front of it, and never anything that reads as a guarantee.

// output contract — sanitized
{
  "overview": "plain-language read of the member's situation, grounded in their data",
  "details": ["2–3 specific, comparative reasons this card fits their gaps"],
  "summary": "one 'if… then…' line on what to do next"
}

// hard guardrail
// approval odds may inform reasoning — never stated, never guaranteed.
// recommend only cards present in approved offer data. no data → no card.
Before — generic

"The Discover it® Cash Back card is a great choice — it offers strong cash-back rewards and no annual fee, making it a versatile addition to your wallet."

After — grounded & comparative

"Unlike your Citi Double Cash® (a flat 2%), Discover it® earns 5% in rotating categories you activate. And while both your current cards charge a 3% foreign-transaction fee, this one charges none — useful if you travel."

Note: examples are reconstructed with representative card details to protect confidential prompts and member data. The structure — grounded, comparative, tied to the member's existing cards — mirrors the shipped behavior.

The outcome

Shipped, evaluated, and validated with real members.

10/10user-test participants reacted positively; several said it would save them time

Both experiences shipped to production. They cleared a multi-dimensional Quality-Engineering review — scored pass/fail across safety, accuracy, structure, and quality — and validated in moderated user testing, where members found the wallet analysis easy to understand and a faster starting point than reading third-party card-review sites. Along the way I left behind a versioned prompt repository and a prompt-development lifecycle the team carried forward.

Honest scope: these are the launch-readiness outcomes I owned as prompt engineer. Post-launch engagement and conversion metrics sat with the product team — happy to discuss what I know in an interview.

What I'd do differently

Members didn't want a better paragraph. They wanted a conversation.

Both experiences were single-shot: each answered one question well and then stopped. In testing, the most consistent request was for a follow-up — "what about this card instead?", "why not the one with the bigger bonus?" If I built it again, I'd design for multi-turn from the start, because the embedded answer was really the opening line of a conversation members wanted to keep having.

The baseline-first habit is the thing I kept. Testing the unchanged prompt before every revision felt slow, but it's the only reason I could ever say a change actually improved the response rather than just changed it. That discipline is now the foundation of how I run evaluation at scale.

The transferable principle

On a regulated commerce surface, the prompt engineer's job isn't to make the model sound smart — it's to make it trustworthy: grounded in real data, honest about uncertainty, and clear enough that a customer can act. That's the job every time an AI explains a product recommendation or a charge to a customer.
