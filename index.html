What I build

The four artifacts every serious AI team needs

01

Prompts & agent instructions

System guidance, tool-use conditions, structured output, refusal boundaries, and tone rules that make model behavior explicit enough to test.

02

Golden datasets & ground truth

Source-of-truth answers, diagnostic decision trees, and judge-checkable gating criteria that give a team a shared definition of good.

03

Evaluation & quality loops

Rubrics, LLM-as-judge, human labeling, multi-turn simulators, and failure taxonomies that turn response quality into a release gate.

04

Working prototypes

Skills, tools, and demos built in Claude Code, Cursor, Vapi, and Voiceflow — so teams test the experience before they commit the quarter.

About

Not an engineer. Not "just" a designer. The person in between — by choice.

I've spent nine years making AI genuinely useful to the person on the other side of the screen — at Meta, Apple (Siri), Sam's Club, and Intuit across QuickBooks and Credit Karma. I grew from hands-on conversation designer to leading the team and the discipline: what the AI says, when it acts, when it hands off to a human, and how we prove it's good enough to ship.

My bias is action. When LLMs arrived, I didn't wait for a paved path — I prototyped GenAI classification a year before our platform approved it, and the concepts shipped when the platform caught up. When agentic systems arrived, I didn't stop at writing requirements — I learned the repo, wrote the YAML, built the tool, and shipped a working skill in five days. Strategy first, always: map the problem to what the technology can genuinely do — then prove it with a build.

I've led that work end to end and led the people doing it: a team of three designers and design technologists, a promotion championed through, hiring loops run, and a craft standard the team still builds to.
