01Background

An agent was being built. Its judgment was not.

The payroll team was building an agent for high-stakes support moments: tax liabilities, filings that looked wrong, debits that did not match agency payments. These topics were selected by call volume, customer pain, and expert handle time.

My part sat underneath the product surface. I designed the diagnostic behavior: what the agent reads silently, what it may ask, when it must stop, and what context travels to a person.

I authored the generator prompt, the answer templates, and the gates myself, then directed the team using that framework across nine troubleshooting territories. The product roadmap belonged to the shipping team; the ground truth for diagnosis was the thing I owned.

02The problem

The system could sound helpful while avoiding the real question.

The warning sign was not that the assistant failed loudly. It failed politely. In payroll-tax eval runs, the agent could list general guidance, stop at federal data when state obligations existed, add stale-data caveats, or name possible causes when one more tool call would resolve the ambiguity.

Payroll support carries a different kind of pressure. A customer asking about liabilities, filings, or a bank debit is really asking whether money moved correctly and whether compliance is at risk. A plausible generic answer can still leave the customer exposed.

The design problem was sequence. The agent had tools for setup, active jurisdictions, payment groups, filings, debits, liability reports, and payment details. The diagnostic framework had to turn those data paths into the requirements for how the skill should behave.

The before pattern. Recreated from eval notes: guidance that sounds reasonable, but does not prove the agent checked the account.

The old pattern left too much room for a broadly helpful answer. The diagnostic had to force a narrower path: discover active jurisdictions, resolve the relevant payment groups, pull liability and payment data, compare records, and ask only when the missing fact truly lives outside the system.

03Why it mattered

Payroll is where a confident wrong answer hurts someone.

Behind every payroll-tax question sits the same fear: did money move correctly, and am I still compliant? A customer asking about state liabilities or an unexplained bank debit may be days from a filing deadline. A generic answer spends that customer’s calm at the worst possible moment, and it is the same ambiguity that makes these calls long for experts.

The deeper risk was the model itself. Payroll is regulated, full of rules a model can imitate without knowing. Asked about a filing schedule or a tax rule it cannot look up, a model will produce a fluent, confident invention. The dangerous failure in this domain is not a wrong answer that looks wrong. It is a wrong answer that looks right.

So the diagnostic knowledge could not be left to the model, and it could not be left implicit in the heads of experts. It had to be written down, tested, and validated by the people who already carry it.

04Exploring solutions

The experts, and the fact map that changed the design.

The diagnostic paths had to come from humans and the actual skill architecture. I compared support expertise with the Omni Agent payroll-tax skills: setup context, active jurisdictions, payment groups, tax payment details, liability reports, and employer debit records.

The insight that shaped the design came from comparing the expert interview to the agent’s advantage. A person starts by asking for context. The agent can start by reading the setup and transaction record. So I mapped each deciding fact to the tool that should prove it.

fact map · payroll tax liabilitiesread first · ask only if missing

DiagnosisDo not stop federal-only. If the answer is hedged, stale, or lists possibilities, the diagnostic is incomplete.
BoundaryIf action changes a filing, payment, or agency response, hand off with the records already checked.

The design conclusion followed directly: do not mimic a broad support script. A liability question must discover active jurisdictions first. A debit question must narrow the date range and match the withdrawal to payment records. A setup question must read setup status before explaining next steps.

Engineering pushed back on one thing, and it was fair: hand-written root causes for every territory would be a maintenance burden as the agent grew. The answer was to make the framework itself reusable, instructions rather than answers, so each new territory starts from the same diagnostic machinery instead of a blank page. Part six shows that machinery.

05Design decisions

Read first. Ask only when the answer lives elsewhere. Stop before guessing.

Four decisions define the behavior. Each one is visible in the reconstructed trace below.

  1. 5.1

    Read the account before the first word.

    The agent silently reads setup context, active jurisdictions, payment groups, liability reports, payment details, debit records, and audit history, then matches what it finds against the known root causes.

  2. 5.2

    Every question carries a written why.

    A question may be asked only when the answer cannot be inferred from the customer data, available tools, or conversation context. Each one ships with a written justification saying where the fact lives and why the system cannot see it.

  3. 5.3

    Instructions, not answers.

    One person hand-writing golden answers for every territory was fantasy. The framework is templates, data dependencies, and gates that generate and check the answers, so it compounds across territories instead of being spent on one.

  4. 5.4

    Name where this is the wrong move.

    Changes too sensitive for an agent to make alone stop, in branches the decision trees name themselves. The case hands off with everything the agent read, ruled out, and was told. A person picks up a diagnosed case; the customer never starts over.

06The build

A generator built to distrust the model, and a ten-day sprint.

The framework is a system prompt before it is anything else: a generator that drafts a complete diagnostic decision tree for a territory, so an expert corrects a draft instead of authoring from a blank page. It runs a six-step reasoning pass, bans payroll concepts the product does not have, locks every draft to verified tools, and caps the questions the agent may ask, each with its written why. Here is the working prompt itself.

Around the generator sit the rest of the framework’s parts: templates for a complete golden answer, the tool and data dependencies behind each one, the exact format the agent team consumes, and a pass or fail gate on every case: can the agent infer this from the account, or must it be told? Clear the gate or do not ship.

The delivery was a sprint of about ten days. I held the generator prompt, the templates, the gates, and the plan; the delivery team drafted the nine territories with the generator and corrected drafts with the experts. Nothing generated shipped as drafted. The class of correction on the record: drafts that assumed the product could see everything, where the honest instruction is manual. The deepest territory reached version ten before it settled, and a final reviewer signed off the closing accuracy pass across the full set.

08Learnings

What I learned, and what I would do differently.

Institutional knowledge lives in people, and the model will confidently invent whatever it cannot look up. A week and a half with the experts who resolve these calls was worth more than any amount of prompting, and nothing generated was allowed to ship as drafted.

Design the agent to its own strengths, not the expert’s script. The expert interviews because a human starts blind; the agent starts with the account open. Porting the human question sequence would have shipped the old interrogation with a friendlier voice.

Instructions compound where answers do not. The framework outlived the sprint: partner teams picked it up for their own territories, which is what made the hand-written root causes defensible against the maintainability concern.

What I would do differently: bring engineering into the framework on day one. The maintainability pushback arrived mid-flight, and reusability was the answer we found under deadline. It was the right answer, and I would rather have designed it with them from the start than proven it to them at the end.
