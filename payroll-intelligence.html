Case 03 · Payroll Intelligence agentic era · 2026

Teaching a payroll agent what the experts know.

Read first. Ask once. Hand off before guessing.

January 2026. A payroll agent was being built, and the hard part was not what it should say: it was what it should do, and when. A model asked for payroll knowledge it cannot look up will invent it, confidently. So the diagnostic ground truth had to be written down, tested, and validated by the people who already knew it. This page is that framework, and the behavior it produced.

Outcome
Payroll conversation quality up 9.8 points in a year, measured by the program’s judge · one question asked where the old flow interrogated
Role
Designed the agent’s diagnostic behavior; authored the ground-truth framework
Scope
The top contact drivers: high traffic, high complexity, do-it-for-me potential · nine troubleshooting territories
When
2026 · agentic era

01The decision

The behavior turns on one customer sentence.

The agent could answer with a list of maybes, open the expert interview, or fix the run on the spot. The framework had to pick one first move and defend it. Her call: verify first.

The decision ask vs act diagnostic behavior

The decision: the missing employee

My employee is missing from this run.

A customer, inside the payroll agent
The setup

Half a dozen root causes wear this one sentence. A human expert opens with a long interview, because a human starts blind. The agent does not: before its first reply it can read the account. Six facts decide the diagnosis. Where does each one live?

subscription statuson the account
employee list and statuson the account
pay schedule assignmentson the account
audit log of recent changeson the account
what the customer expectedonly the customer
what is on their screenonly the customer

Her call

verify.

Read the account first, silently. Then spend exactly one question, on the one fact only the customer holds.

The receipt

The rule I wrote into the framework: do not mimic the expert’s question sequence. The account already answers most of the interview, so the agent matches what it reads against the known root causes first, then asks only for what it cannot retrieve. In the shipped dataset, every must-ask question carries a written justification for why the system cannot look it up. Only two rows in the setup survive that test: what the customer expected, and what is on their screen.

02The trace

Read first. Ask once.

The same sentence, handled two ways. The interrogation asks the customer for what the system already holds; the shipped behavior reads first and spends one question.

before · the interrogation

What is the employee’s full name? When did you add them? Which pay schedule are they on? Did you already run payroll this period? Four questions asked of the customer, and every answer already sits on the account: the employee list, the audit log, the schedule table, the run history.

after · read first, then ask once

The agent reads before it speaks: subscription, employee record, this run against the next one. It diagnoses, then spends its one question on intent, the only fact it cannot retrieve. The session below is that behavior, step by step.

The framework also names, ahead of time, where read first and ask once is the wrong move. Payroll is regulated, and behind every question sits the same one: will my people get paid? Changes too sensitive for an agent to make alone stop, in branches the decision trees name themselves, and the case hands off with everything the agent read, ruled out, and was told. A person picks up a diagnosed case; the customer never starts over.

Note 01

Three silent reads before the agent’s first word. The customer answered one question, total.

03The framework

Instructions, not answers.

The target was payroll’s top contact drivers: high traffic, high complexity, and the kind of question an agent could resolve end to end, not just answer. One person hand-writing golden answers for all of them was fantasy. So the framework is instructions, because instructions compound where answers do not.

What the instructions hold

Templates for a complete golden answer, the tool and data dependencies behind each one, the exact format the agent team consumes, and a pass or fail gate on every case: can the agent infer this from the account, or must it be told? Clear the gate or do not ship.

Built to distrust the model

Troubleshooting territories get golden decision trees drafted by a system prompt, so an expert corrects a draft instead of authoring from a blank page. The prompt runs a six-step reasoning pass, bans payroll concepts the product does not have, and locks every draft to verified tools: the dangerous failure is not a wrong answer, it is a confident invented one.

The people

The diagnostic paths had to come from humans: about a week and a half of interviews with the people who resolve these calls. Support, engineering, the policy team. Nothing generated shipped as drafted; subject-matter experts validated every answer.

What I did not build

The agent. The payroll agent, its product vision, and its roadmap belong to the team that ships it. What I owned sits underneath: the ground truth for its diagnostic behavior, the generator that drafts it, and the gates that decide when it is good enough.

Leading the sprint · what stayed with me

The framework had one author; the delivery was a team of three I directed, in a sprint of about ten days. I held the generator prompt, the answer templates, the gates, and the plan: which territories, in what order, against the deadline. The team drafted the territories with the generator, correcting drafts instead of authoring from scratch.

What the experts corrected

Nothing shipped as drafted. The class of correction on the record: drafts that assumed the product could see everything, where the honest instruction is manual. The deepest territory reached version ten before it settled, and one of the three signed off the closing accuracy pass across the full set.
