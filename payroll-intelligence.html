Case 02 · Payroll Intelligence agentic era · 2026

Teaching a payroll agent what the experts know.

Read first. Ask once. Hand off before guessing.

January 2026. A payroll agent was being built, and the hard part was not what it should say: it was what it should do, and when. A model asked for payroll knowledge it cannot look up will invent it, confidently. So the diagnostic ground truth had to be written down, tested, and validated by the people who already knew it. This page is that framework, and the behavior it produced.

Outcome
Payroll conversation quality up 9.8 points in a year, measured by the program’s judge · one question asked where the old flow interrogated
Role
Designed the agent’s diagnostic behavior; authored the ground-truth framework
Scope
The top contact drivers: high traffic, high complexity, do-it-for-me potential · nine troubleshooting territories

The receipt

The rule I wrote into the framework: do not mimic the expert’s question sequence. The account already answers most of the interview, so the agent matches what it reads against the known root causes first, then asks only for what it cannot retrieve. In the shipped dataset, every must-ask question carries a written justification for why the system cannot look it up. Only two rows in the setup survive that test: what the customer expected, and what is on their screen.

02The trace

Read first. Ask once.

The same sentence, handled two ways. The interrogation asks the customer for what the system already holds; the shipped behavior reads first and spends one question.

before · the interrogation

What is the employee’s full name? When did you add them? Which pay schedule are they on? Did you already run payroll this period? Four questions asked of the customer, and every answer already sits on the account: the employee list, the audit log, the schedule table, the run history.

after · read first, then ask once

The agent reads before it speaks: subscription, employee record, this run against the next one. It diagnoses, then spends its one question on intent, the only fact it cannot retrieve. The session below is that behavior, step by step.

03The handoff

It also knows when to stop.

Payroll is a regulated domain. A wrong answer is a compliance problem, not just a bad session, and behind every question sits the same one: will my people get paid? Read first and ask once is the right move for most of the territory. The framework also says, ahead of time, where it is not.

The triggers

Changes too sensitive or too regulated for an agent to make alone. Questions where the safe answer is a person. Every decision tree in the framework names these branches in the tree itself, so stopping is designed, not discovered on a live account.

The contract

The agent stops and passes the case with everything it has: what it read, what it ruled out, the one answer the customer gave. A person picks up a diagnosed case, and the customer never starts over.

04The framework

Instructions, not answers.

The target was payroll’s top contact drivers: high traffic, high complexity, and the kind of question an agent could resolve end to end, not just answer. One person hand-writing golden answers for all of them was fantasy. So the framework is instructions, because instructions compound where answers do not.

What the instructions hold

Templates for what a complete golden answer looks like. The tool and data dependencies behind each one. The exact format the agent team consumes. And the gate every answer has to clear before it ships. One artifact: design, engineering, and quality each pull their slice from the same source of truth.

The generator

How-to questions get golden answers: one verified procedure. Troubleshooting questions get golden decision trees, and every branch needs expert-grade coverage. Hand-writing nine territories of them would have taken weeks. So a system prompt drafts each tree in one validated structure, and an expert corrects a draft instead of authoring from a blank page.

Built to distrust the model

The prompt runs a six-step reasoning pass before it writes anything. It bans payroll concepts the product does not have and locks every draft to a fixed set of verified tools, because the dangerous failure here is not a wrong answer, it is a confident invented one. It also carries contrastive examples: “check your settings” fails; the exact settings path, screen by screen, passes.

The gate

Every case carries its own pass or fail gate: a handful of binary checks, each forcing the same question. Can the agent infer this from the account, or must it be told? Clear the gate or do not ship.

The people

The diagnostic paths had to come from humans: about a week and a half of interviews with the people who resolve these calls. Support, engineering, the policy team. Nothing generated shipped as drafted; subject-matter experts validated every answer. I led the sprint that delivered the full set in about ten days, as a blueprint the team keeps regenerating as the product shifts.

What I did not build

The agent. The payroll agent, its product vision, and its roadmap belong to the team that ships it. What I owned sits underneath: the ground truth for its diagnostic behavior, the generator that drafts it, and the gates that decide when it is good enough.

05Leading the sprint

Directed work, on the record.

The framework had one author. The delivery was a team I directed, and the division of labor survives in the sprint’s own artifacts.

What stayed with me

The generator system prompt, the answer templates, the pass or fail gates, and the plan: which territories, in what order, against a deadline measured in days. I set the structure every draft had to land in, and the bar every draft had to clear.

What the team ran

The team of three I led drafted the territories with the generator, correcting drafts instead of authoring from a blank page. Same framework, same validated structure, whoever held the pen.

What the experts corrected

Nothing shipped as drafted. The class of correction on the record: drafts that assumed the product could see everything. The expert pass caught steps that depend on data living outside the product, in systems the agent cannot read, where the honest instruction is manual.

How many rounds it took

The deepest territory, the diagnostic for an automated payroll run that silently fails, reached version ten before it settled. One of the three then owned the closing accuracy pass: scope, root causes, decision points, and every decision tree, verified with the experts and signed off across the full set.
