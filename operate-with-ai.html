No. 07 · How I operate with AI

I direct the systems I used to design.

For ten years I've directed language systems: first telling a model what to recognize, then how to reason, then how to produce structured truth at scale. Now I direct crews of AI agents that build and check their own work. This site is one of them.

Most of what people call "using AI" is one person typing into one chat. That isn't how I work, and it isn't new for me. Directing AI is the fourth rung of a single craft I've practiced for a decade.

The job never changed. The altitude did.

In the NLU years I told models what to recognize, intent by intent. When LLMs arrived, I stopped training and started instructing: telling a model how to reason, where to stop, and what it must never say. Then I built generators that draft structured evaluation data through parallel sub-agents, so a system could produce ground truth at scale instead of a person filling a blank row.

Each step, the unit of work got larger and I moved up a level: from labeling, to prompting, to directing a system that produces and checks its own output. Same craft, rising altitude.

I direct a crew, not a chatbot.

When the work is large, I stand up a crew of agents, each with a written job. A narrative lead owns the story. A voice editor guards how it sounds. An evidence-and-redaction guardian checks every claim and keeps confidential material off the page. A panel of judges reads the work cold, from a skeptic's seat. I brief them, they fan out in parallel, and I read what comes back.

It's the shift every new manager makes. You stop doing the task and start writing the job description, the definition of good, and the line that says when something has gone wrong. Prompting was the old skill. Directing a system to produce and verify work is the new one.

An agent isn't answering your question. It's picking up from where you left off, so the work is in how you set it up.Simone, the principle behind the method

Verification is the whole game.

Generating is the easy half. The half that matters is proving the output is right before it ships, and that discipline is what I carry from a decade of evaluation work. So nothing the crew produces is trusted on sight.

Findings get handed to judges who try to refute them. Anything that touches a former employer goes through the redaction guardian, whose only job is to catch a confidential name or number before it reaches a public page. Claims that can't be defended cold get cut, even the flattering ones. The honest version is almost always the stronger one.

Here's the loop every piece of this site passed through.

The proof is the thing you're reading.

This portfolio was built the way I just described. I set the direction; agent crews drafted, judged, and hardened the case studies; an adversarial pass scrubbed every page for anything confidential and every claim for anything it couldn't support; and I approved what shipped. The footer on every page says as much.

That's where the craft is heading, and where I want to be: not the person who specs a system and hands it off, but the one who directs a fleet of them to do high-stakes work, and proves it's right.
