No. 08 · How I built this site

I built this site the way I build agentic systems.

I've spent nine years designing and building AI. I built my own portfolio the same way I build agentic systems at work, and it asked the same things of me. This site is the result, and the closest look at how I actually work, so it earns the last slot. Here's how it came together.

The resultThe site you're reading

I started on the newest model in the room.

Anthropic had just put Fable 5 in front of the public, the most capable model available, and the timing was too good to pass up. I opened with the vision, not a task list: the shape of the site, the sections, the case studies, and the working prototypes I wanted embedded in the page rather than described in it. I handed over the spec and let the model reason toward the build.

Fable 5 got a surprising amount right from very little direction. On a long, open-ended job like this, it held the whole picture and filled gaps the way I would have.

Three hours in, I lost Fable 5.

My access to Fable 5 dropped about three hours into the project, and I moved to Opus 4.8 to keep going. The two models work differently, and the switch is what taught me where each one fits. Fable is the stronger model on long, autonomous stretches; it needed less hand-holding to take a big, ambiguous build in the right direction. Opus 4.8 wanted sharper specs from me, but once it had them it was fast, steady, and relentless, the better default for iterative work. So I tightened how I directed, and the build never stalled.

Knowing which model to reach for, and how to brief each one, is its own skill now. I'd rather make that call deliberately than run everything on one setting.

Most of the work was feedback.

I set the bar up front, then sorted everything I said back into two kinds. Minor was a tweak: change this line, tighten that section, keep going. Major was a signal to stop and re-plan, and when I pushed on something big, the model stepped back, re-evaluated its own approach, and proposed a new one before writing another word. I had it grade its own output against my direction too, so it caught its own misses before I did.

A tweak and a rethink are different instructions. Knowing which one you're giving is most of the craft.

Then I gave it a team.

A portfolio isn't one job; it's a dozen crafts at once. So I had the model stand up a crew of specialist agents and run them in parallel, each with a brief of its own: content strategy, design and layout, motion and media, the interactive prototypes embedded in each case. Over several rounds I added a panel of judges to read the work cold from a skeptic's seat, and a guardian whose only job was to keep anything confidential off a public page. I set the direction; they produced and checked each other; I kept what held.

Until it sounded like me.

We went round after round, on the voice most of all. A portfolio can be accurate and still sound like no one. So I cut anything that read as performance, kept pushing until the sentences were ones I'd actually say out loud, and only stopped when the site felt like me to read, not just correct.

That last part took the longest, and it's the part that matters. You're reading the result.
