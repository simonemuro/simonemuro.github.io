Case No. 08 How I built this site This portfolio

Building this portfolio site by directing crews of AI agents.

This is the portfolio of a conversational AI manager, and the site itself is the eighth case on it. My work is directing and gating AI systems, so when I needed a portfolio, I built it the same way I build products: crews of AI agents wrote the code and drafted the pages, persona judges read every page before it shipped, and a claims census decided what every number on the site is allowed to say. The direction, the feedback, and the final say were mine. This case is the method.

01Background

The same job, pointed at a portfolio.

The seven cases before this one are about directing AI systems: deciding what an agent may do, giving it something true to say, and gating it before it ships. A portfolio site is a small product with the same risks. It makes claims about money and results, it speaks for me to strangers, and every sentence on it is either receipted or it is not. So I ran the build the way I run the day job.

The audience is specific: recruiters and hiring managers deciding in minutes whether my work is real. That audience does not extend charity. The build had to assume none.

The hands versus direction line is the point of this case, so here it is plainly. My hands: the brief, every design call, the feedback on every draft, and the final read of every page against my own bar. The agents’ hands: the code, the styles, the first drafts of the prose, one crew per page, this page included. The judges: panels I configured and pointed, whose verdicts I could overrule and mostly did not. Nothing shipped that I did not read.

02The problem

A number I could not receipt.

Before this edition wrote a page, every number on the old site and on the resumes behind it went into a census against four sources from my own archives: local files, screenshots, email, and decks I authored. Most claims came back holding a receipt. One came back holding nothing.

Memory said the number was true. Four archives said nothing.

That is the problem in one line. A portfolio that argues for evaluation discipline was carrying a claim it could not prove, and the same claim had been copied into the resume files that get sent to companies. The choice was to keep it on memory, or to treat my own site the way I treat an agent that states a number it cannot trace.

03Why it mattered

This site does not get to grade itself on a curve.

Every case on this site makes the same argument: agent behavior is only trustworthy when it is verified, and a stated number must trace to data. A single unreceipted claim falsifies that argument everywhere it appears. The readers I want, hiring managers and evaluation practitioners, are exactly the people who check.

And the blast radius was bigger than one page. The census found that claims from the site had propagated into the resume files across the project. A number fixed on the site and left wrong in a resume is not fixed.

04Exploring solutions

Strangers wrote the brief, a contest set the direction.

I did not trust the author to see the old site clearly, so I put strangers in front of it first and let options compete instead of debating them.

  1. 4.1

    The cold read.

    Eight persona judges read the old site with no context and no charity: hiring managers, a recruiter, a skeptical engineer, an evaluation practitioner, critics. Where they agreed, the gap became a requirement. The brief for this edition came out of their notes, not out of my head.

  2. 4.2

    The competition.

    Design direction was settled by contest, not debate. Three agents each built a full homepage, and a judge panel read them cold. The light editorial system won; the runtime trace and the working refusal came over from the losers. Nothing about the direction was mine to defend, only mine to pick.

05Design decisions

The rules the build ran on.

Three decisions define the method. Each one is visible in the build trace in part six.

  1. 5.1

    No receipt, no claim.

    The census became a rule, and the rule became a ledger: every number on this site traces to an artifact I own. Claims that failed the census were rewritten down to what the receipts support or removed, here and in every resume file that carried them.

  2. 5.2

    Feedback comes in two named sizes.

    Minor meant tighten this and keep going. Major meant stop, re-plan, and show me the new plan before the next line. Saying which one you are giving is most of the craft of directing agents; an agent that cannot tell a nudge from a halt wastes both.

  3. 5.3

    No live model on the site.

    A site agent that bluffs would falsify every case it hosts. So the box on the home page answers from curated replies, refuses the rest, and says it is curated in its own header. The boundary is the same one the cases argue for: say only what you can trace, and say what you are.

06The build

One agent for the system, a fleet for the pages, gates before merge.

The crews ran in a fixed order. One agent built the shared system first: the type, the tokens, the components, the budgets every page had to live inside. Then a fleet built the pages in parallel, one agent per page, this page included, each working from the brief the cold read produced.

Every page then faced three judges before merge: redaction, honesty, voice. Redaction checks that nothing internal or confidential appears. Honesty checks every claim against the ledger. Voice checks that the page sounds like me and not like a model. A page that failed a gate went back to its crew with notes. No pass, no ship.

Here is the whole build, compressed into the trace it actually followed.

07Results

The output is the site, and every number on it traces.

This case has no customer metric to report, and it does not pretend to. What it can claim, it can prove by inspection.

Every number on this site traces to the claims ledger, and the ledger traces to artifacts I own. The claim that failed the census was rewritten down to what its receipts support, and the corrections reached back into every resume file that carried it. Eight case pages shipped, each through the three gates, each built by a crew I directed and read myself before merge.

The method is the same shape as the day job: define what good looks like, build it, prove it. The seven cases before this one show the shape pointed at products. This one shows it pointed at the thing making the argument.

08Learnings

What I learned, and what I would do differently.

Strangers find what the author cannot. The eight cold-read judges surfaced gaps I had stopped being able to see, and their agreement was a better brief than my own judgment of my own work.

Naming the size of feedback is most of directing agents. Minor and major sound like process; in practice they are the difference between a crew that keeps momentum and a crew that rebuilds the wrong thing faster.

Delegating the typing does not delegate the bar. The agents produced every line of code on this site, and none of it shipped until I read it. The judgment about what is true, what is mine to claim, and what sounds like me never left my desk.

What I would do differently: run the census first, always. This is the fourth edition of this site, and the first one whose numbers all hold a receipt. The earlier editions carried claims on memory because memory said they were true. The census should have gated the first edition, not the fourth.
