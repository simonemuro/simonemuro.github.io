No. 05 · NLU Foundations · 2020 – 2022

I built the ruler before I moved the numbers.

2020. I joined a young conversation-AI team that had just moved its support assistant off a phone tree and onto NLU, so customers could finally just say what they needed instead of pressing 1. The capability was thin, and no one could see where it was breaking down. So before I changed anything, I built a way to measure where it was failing. That measurement is what turned the assistant from guesswork into a system I could tune.

RoleConversation Designer, then Senior; the team's NLU SME
When2020 – 2022
BuiltThe intent-matching measurement process and the team's NLU playbook
OutcomeTop performance rating; the discipline the rest of my work runs on

This is what a year of that work moved. Each bar is an intent I rebuilt, and the number is self-help acceptance: the share of customers who took the assistant's answer instead of asking for a person. Higher is the win. Reconstructed from my year-end review; intent names are generic.

First, I built a way to see it failing.

No one could fix the assistant because no one could see where it was breaking. It matched what a customer said to a set of intents, and when it matched wrong, or matched nothing, the customer got "Sorry, I didn't quite get that" and a handoff to a person. But no one could tell you how often that was happening, or where.

So I stood up a process to measure exactly how well we were matching real customer utterances to intents. I used it to baseline the system, find the failures, and point my work at the worst-performing places first. Over that year, matching accuracy climbed and the fallback moment, the "I didn't get that," dropped by more than half.

I built the parts that weren't there yet.

Some questions had no intent at all. A customer would ask about activating, renewing, or being billed for their subscription, and the assistant had nothing to match, so it fell straight to a human. That's not a tuning problem, it's a coverage gap.

I built roughly a dozen new intents to close the biggest gaps, including a whole cluster around subscription and payments. Adding an intent isn't a drop-in; each one has to be trained and slotted into the existing architecture without pulling accuracy off the intents around it. Expanding coverage and holding accuracy are the same job, done carefully.

I became the person the team learned NLU from.

The work made me the team's NLU expert, and the people around me started asking for the thinking behind it, not just the results. When they kept asking, I wrote it down: I authored our NLU best-practices guide and trained designers to think about intents and matching from the very start of a design, not as a cleanup step at the end.

That's the part I'm proudest of from this era. I didn't just do the work, I turned it into a discipline other people could run.

It earned the top rating. It also became the way I work.

The year's work earned the highest performance rating Intuit gives, and the promotion to Senior that came with it. But the rating isn't the point. The point is the habit it set.

Build the measurement before you trust the result. Find the failures before you celebrate the wins. Turn what you learned into something the team can repeat. That's exactly what I went on to build for LLM-era agents years later, in the quality program, when the technology changed and the discipline didn't.
