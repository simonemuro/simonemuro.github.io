BillingSkill: building the thing I'd been evaluating

For a year I defined what good agent behavior looked like: golden datasets, rubrics, quality gates. One question kept nagging me: could the person who writes the evaluation build the thing being evaluated? I gave myself five days and an internal hackathon deadline to find out.

Role
Solo builder: design, prompts, tool, eval
Context
Intuit internal builder hackathon, submitted & judged
Stack
Agent platform repo · Cursor · skill YAML · MCP
Outcome
Working skill + 108-case eval dataset in 5 days

The problem worth picking

Subscription-billing questions are one of the highest-volume reasons customers contact QuickBooks support. Ask the assistant "what's this charge?" and you got a summarized help article: generic steps, no awareness of your account, your plan, your charge. The data to answer properly existed. Nothing connected it to the conversation.

Before → after · reconstructed
Why was I charged $107 this month?
BEFORE (generic article summary): "To view your subscription charges, go to Settings → Billing & Subscription…" no account context · same answer for every customer
AFTER (account-aware): "Your June charge is $107: QuickBooks Online Plus ($99/mo) plus the Payroll add-on you added on May 14 ($8 prorated). Want me to break that down, or review your plan options?" grounded in live account data via the lookup tool

Reconstructed with invented account data. The pattern (identify the question, retrieve account context, explain plainly, offer the next action) is the real design.

The bet

The tools to build agent skills already existed inside the company: the agent platform repo, a skill definition format, a tool-registration path. They were just considered engineering territory. My thesis, stated in the hackathon submission: a conversation designer who already shapes how the agent talks, through golden datasets, rubrics, and quality criteria, can use those same tools to build a working skill end to end. If true, designers stop being quality guardians at the end of the line and become contributors at the source.

Five days, honestly logged

DAY 1Scope from evidence, not opinion

Analyzed 500+ labeled customer utterances; defined 7 intent categories (unrecognized charge, billing inquiry, pricing, duplicate charge…). Chose subscription charges: highest volume, clearest data dependency.

DAY 2Write the skill, build the tool

Wrote the skill definition in YAML: tone, when-to-use conditions, graceful handling, and an admin vs non-admin permission check. Built a billing lookup tool with three environment modes, LIVE (real APIs), EVAL (scenario injection), and DEV (mock data), and registered it in the agent repo.

DAY 3Test end to end

Ran the skill locally against the real agent through a Streamlit test harness. Validated both permission paths: admins get charge detail, non-admins get a safe redirect.

DAY 4Hit the wall, document the wall

API research for live billing history hit a permissions blocker. Documented it precisely for the platform team instead of faking around it, and built the first 33-row golden dataset so evaluation wouldn't wait on access.

DAY 5Make it real with real conversations

Pulled 55 actual production conversations, rewrote the skill instructions to handle what people actually say (cancel intent, price-increase complaints, downgrades) and grew the eval set to 108 test cases across 13 intent categories. That's six more categories than I'd scoped on Day 1, because real conversations widened the taxonomy.

How I actually built it

I forked the agent platform repo and worked in Cursor with Claude as my pair. I used it to understand how existing skills were structured, to write the code I needed, and to test everything locally on my machine. The MCP piece was the two of us vibe-coding a local prototype, with full knowledge that wiring it into the company's production infrastructure would take engineering partnership.

I'm specific about the method because the method is the point. This is what AI-native building looks like in 2026: knowing how to drive an AI pair through an unfamiliar codebase, what to ask it, what to verify yourself, and when to stop generating and read the code. That skill is what turned a year of defining quality into working software in five days.

The part most demos skip: the skill shipped with its own eval

I didn't want a demo that worked when I drove it. Every scenario in the golden dataset carries mock tool data and judge-checkable gating criteria: explicit pass/fail rules written for a judge model to apply, including the failure modes that matter most:

scenario"Customer asks why their monthly charge went up"
mock_data{ "plan": "QuickBooks Online Plus", "plan_cost_monthly": 99.00, "add_ons": [ … ] }
gating · correctnessAgent identifies the specific subscription plan and cost from the mock data
gating · no_hallucinationMust not: fabricate a charge amount not present in the mock data
gating · behaviorMust not: send the customer to support without first checking account data

Plus a cancel-intercept: when a customer's billing question is really a cancellation in disguise, the skill offers a confirmation widget, a chance to fix the actual problem, before surrendering to churn. And before any of it could conflict with production, I ran an overlap analysis against the 7 existing skills that touch billing. No critical conflicts.

5 days · 108 test cases · 0 engineers

Submitted and judged at Intuit's internal builder hackathon, with a working demo, built while learning MCP from scratch. It didn't take a prize, and I'm not going to pretend it did: the result I wanted was the proof, and the API blockers I documented went to the platform team as real feedback.

What this proves

The gap between "defines what good looks like" and "builds the thing" is now crossable by one motivated person. When the same person designs the behavior, writes the instructions, and authors the eval, the loop between intent and evidence closes in days, not quarters. The API blocker I documented is exactly where engineering partnership begins, and I documented it the way a good partner would: precisely. That's the working model I bring to a team: scope from evidence, build the smallest real thing, ship it with its own definition of good.
