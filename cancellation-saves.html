Case No. 05 Cancellation saves QuickBooks support

Hearing why QuickBooks customers cancel, and routing the savable ones to a specialist.

Before a QuickBooks subscription cancels, the customer says why. I designed the ask-why flow and the LLM classifier that reads the reason, then routes savable problems to a specialist and gives everyone else a clean goodbye. It ran end to end in 2023, before the platform could ship generative AI to production at all, and both routes are on this page as screen recordings of the working flow.

01Background

The cancel path, and who fights for it.

I was a conversation designer on the Conversation AI team, working on the support assistant that QuickBooks customers reach in chat and on the phone. When a customer comes to cancel, the business has one real chance to keep them: saves specialists, people trained to review a plan, fix a bad experience, or find a better price. Whether that chance exists at all depends on why the customer is leaving. Some reasons are winnable. Some are final.

The work spans two eras. In 2022 I designed the saves flow itself on the intent platform: ask why before offering the cancel steps, then route the winnable reasons to specialists. In early 2023, when intent training stopped improving how well the system heard those reasons, I built a generative AI classifier to read them instead.

02The problem

What the intent model couldn’t hear.

“I want to cancel” is a dozen problems wearing one sentence. Too expensive, not using it, switching products, a bad support experience, duplicate accounts, closing the business, retiring. The intent model could hear the topic, a cancellation is happening, without much trouble. Hearing the reason was where the system stalled. By early 2023 I’d spent four months tuning intents, and the curve had gone flat: about 60 percent of reasons resolved, two in five coming back unknown, and every unknown routed like every other cancellation, straight toward the cancel steps.

03Why it mattered

Every unknown reason was a lost save.

A price complaint that reaches a specialist with context is a conversation about the plan. A price complaint that reaches nobody is a lost subscription. The queue cut the other way too: a specialist spending time on a call that was never savable, a business closing, a retirement, is a specialist not saving a call that was. The bar I set for the classifier: if I send ten people to that queue, the ten should be truly savable. Not two.

  1. Ask whyThe customer names the reason in their own words.
  2. ClassifyThe model writes JSON only; the flow decides what the customer hears.
  3. RouteSavable reasons get a specialist with context attached.
  4. ExitFinal reasons get a respectful close, no pitch and no trap.

04Exploring solutions

Keep tuning, or change the tool.

My read was that the bottleneck was the tool, not the tuning, and I championed the swap at a time when the third option officially didn’t exist: in early 2023, no sanctioned path shipped generative AI to production at the company. I partnered with the platform team to onboard the API that let a flow call a language model at all, and wrote the classifier prompt. Six plus months of development per training cycle became days of prompt work.

option 01Another tuning cycle

Months more labeling, and the last cycle had barely moved the unknowns. The curve had answered this one already.

option 02Hand it to a modeling team

The same method with more hands on it. More effort against the same ceiling.

option 03Let a language model read the reason

No trained intents at all. A model built for natural language doesn’t need a training cycle to hear that “it’s gotten too expensive” is about price.

05Defining savability

Savable wasn’t my call to make. Encoding it was.

The word doing all the work in this case is savable, and I didn’t define it. The Growth and Retention team did, with the saves specialists who win these calls every day. They knew which reasons a specialist can actually turn around and which ones deserve a graceful exit instead of a pitch. I worked with them to get those guidelines out of their heads and into a form a model could follow, then structured them into the prompt instructions that power the classifier.

Recreated from the savability guidelines defined with the Growth and Retention team and the saves specialists; their rules, my structuring.

The acknowledgment itself went through the same discipline. An early version answered every reason with the same line: “Thanks for the feedback,” followed by the cancel link, even when the reason was a death. I rewrote the instructions so the acknowledgment reflects the reason with plain human empathy, while still never pitching a final one.

06The prompt

The guidelines, compiled into instructions.

The build is a hybrid of the two eras. The intent model does what it was always good at: recognizing that a cancellation is happening. The classifier does what intents couldn’t: reading why. The low-code tool between them parses the JSON, applies the savability rules, and routes the call. Here’s the classifier prompt, redacted, otherwise as shipped, with the guidelines marked where they became instructions.

their rules, encodedThe final-reasons list in “is_savable” is the retention team’s guideline, verbatim in spirit: retirement, closure, duplicates.

the safe defaultTrue for everything else. When the model is unsure, the call goes to a person, never straight to the cancel steps.

never pitch a goodbyeThe acknowledgment field is forbidden from offering help. The offer lives in its own field, present only when the reason is savable.

no improvisingJSON only. Nothing reaches the customer outside the two contract fields, and the flow composes the turn deterministically.

Verification came before trust. I tested the classifier on about 300 real customer utterances, a sample sized for 90 percent confidence, and drove it through the Responsible AI review. It cleared: free of hallucination, profanity, bias, and toxicity findings across the evaluation set.

The saves flow itself, the ask-why-and-route design from 2022, moved real results before the classifier existed: it directionally saved 12 percent of customers against a 5 percent target, on expert-reported saves. My result on the classifier is the routing precision: 96.6 percent of the cancellations it sent to the saves queue were truly savable. That number is the case-rate lever. The higher the share of truly savable cases landing on the queue, the more of the specialists’ time turns into saves instead of screening.

The classifier cleared the Responsible AI review, but the platform’s production path for generative AI didn’t exist yet in early 2023, so the experience ran end to end in the internal low-code tool: designed, validated, measured, and gated from full deployment until the platform caught up. The hybrid shape it proved, an intent model for the topic and a language model for the nuance, is the same architecture the charge-lookup redesign shipped two years later. And the savability thinking carried further still: the billing skill’s cancel-intercept widget is its agentic descendant.

08Learnings

What I learned, and what I’d do differently.

When the curve goes flat, question the tool before commissioning more effort. Four months of tuning taught me more about the intent platform’s ceiling than about customers. The moment I named the bottleneck, the answer stopped being another cycle.

The boundaries are what made an unproven model shippable here. The classifier earned its place by what it was forbidden to do: speak raw to a customer, improvise an offer, pitch a retirement. Strict output contracts and human-owned savability rules are how a new tool gets trusted with an old queue.

Two things I’d do differently. I’d start the deployment-path conversation with the platform team on day one, alongside the prompt work, so the proof and the path to production land together instead of the proof waiting. And I’d separate my result from the program’s ambition sooner: the routing quality was mine to own and measure, and I’d state it plainly rather than let the program’s downstream revenue projection stand in for it.
