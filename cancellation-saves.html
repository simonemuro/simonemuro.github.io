Case 05 · Cancellation Saves LLM era · 2022 to 2023

Four months of tuning, then a bet.

“I want to cancel” is a dozen problems wearing one sentence, and only some of them are losses you have to accept. The reason a customer gives is the thing that decides whether you can keep them. By early 2023 I had spent four months teaching an intent model to hear it, and the teaching had stopped working.

01The bet

Keep tuning, or bet the queue on an unproven model?

Four months in, precision on the reasons the model knew sat near 80 percent, and the curve had gone flat. The unknowns barely moved. She could settle for the model as it stood, commission another tuning cycle, build a bigger eval first, or hand the problem to a modeling team. Her call: act. Swap the engine and let an LLM classify the reason.

The decision cancel flow · early 2023 a methodology bet

The decision: keep tuning the intent model, or swap in an LLM classifier

Two in five cancellation reasons still came back unknown. The next tuning cycle would take months and move almost none of them.

the intent model, early 2023
The setup

Tuning meant labeling utterances against trained intents, months per cycle, and precision on known reasons had climbed to roughly 80 percent while the unknowns held. Meanwhile the saves teams kept 20 to 35 percent of customers when they knew the real reason: every unknown was money left on the table. The alternative did not officially exist. No sanctioned path shipped generative AI to production yet.

The receipt

The bottleneck was never the tuning, it was the tool. Championing the swap meant partnering with the platform team to onboard the API that let a flow call an LLM at all. In evaluation, classification coverage rose from 60 to 96 percent, and savability accuracy came in at 96.6 percent, measured on about 300 real customer utterances, a sample sized for 90 percent confidence. Months of training per cycle became hours of prompt work.

02The queue, and the economics

“If I send ten people to that queue, the ten should be truly savable. Not two.”

My bar for the classifier, and the reason it exists at all.

The first version of the flow was mine too: ask why before offering the cancel steps, then route the reasons the saves team had validated as winnable, price, bad experience, not using the product, to specialists with the context attached. It worked, and exposed the real quality problem: a queue filling with calls that should never have reached the price-rescue specialists, every wasted call costing a real save somewhere else.

The classifier tightened the whole chain: reason, sentiment, savability, route. Strict JSON that no customer ever reads raw; a low-code tool parsed it and applied savability rules from the people who knew the saves economics.

The saves math

Across 8,814 cancellation calls over four months, the flow directionally saved 12 percent of customers against a 5 percent target, on expert-reported saves. Price calls saved at roughly 43 percent, about eight times the rate of calls where the business was simply closing. That gap is the whole argument: the reason decides how rescuable the call is.

The projection, kept honest

The last row above stays a projection. The retention case, built on the earlier saves experiment, projected $2.9M in retention revenue over the following three months. No receipt upgrades that word, so this page keeps it: projected. The measured numbers are the ones the case leads with.

What I did not build

A pitch for final reasons. A customer retiring or closing the business gets a clean, respectful goodbye, not an offer.

The constraint · gated at deployment

I drove the classifier through the Responsible AI review, the gate that mattered for JSON read by machines and never shown raw. It cleared: zero hallucination findings, zero toxicity findings across the evaluation set. What it did not have, in early 2023, was a sanctioned path to ship generative AI: so the experience ran end to end in an internal low-code tool, designed, validated, and blocked from full deployment until the platform caught up. The pattern was ready first.

03The artifact

How the classifier was instructed.

The build was a hybrid: the intent model recognized that a cancellation was happening; this prompt read why; a low-code tool parsed its JSON to route the call. Redacted, otherwise as shipped.

Three decisions are visible in the excerpt. The category set is open: nine anchored labels plus room for another accurate label, so the model is never forced into a wrong bucket. Savability is a default, not a list: false only for terminal reasons, true for everything else, biasing the routing toward giving a specialist the chance. And the two message fields are separated on purpose, the acknowledgment forbidden from offering help, the offer in its own field, so the voice layer composes the turn deterministically.

04Handoff

Next: the same discipline, out loud.

The pattern is the part that outlived the wait: hear the reason, judge savability, route with context, and keep the cancel path one step away. Once a system can hear the difference between a dozen problems wearing one sentence, you stop designing flows and start designing judgment.
