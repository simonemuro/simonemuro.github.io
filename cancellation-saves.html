No. 05 · Cancellation Saves

“I want to cancel” isn’t one problem.

It’s a dozen problems wearing the same sentence, and only some of them are losses you have to accept. Teaching a support system to hear the difference took two generations of technology.

RoleLead conversation designer & prompt engineer
When2022 – 2023
StackNLU intents · LLM classifier · strict JSON · low-code build
Outcome96.6% classifier accuracy (pre-launch) · saves flow 12% vs 5% target

First, hear the reason.

Cancellation was one of the highest-volume reasons customers called, and the voice assistant treated every request identically: hand over the cancel steps, let them go. The reason, the one piece of signal that decides whether a customer is savable, was spoken out loud on every call and acted on in none of them.

The growth and retention team asked for the obvious first step: before the flow offers the steps, ask why. I analyzed what customers actually said, bucketed the answers into topics, and partnered with an NLP team on a classification model so the retention strategists could finally see the full spread of reasons they were working with.

Then came the routing experiment. Customers leaving over price, a bad experience, or because they weren’t using the product, the reasons the saves team had validated as genuinely winnable, went straight to dedicated retention specialists with the context attached, so nobody repeated themselves. Everyone else got a clean, respectful cancel path.

The queue had a pollution problem.

It worked, and it surfaced the real quality problem. The saves queue was filling with false positives: customers who weren’t actually canceling, or whose reasons were final. Specialists trained to rescue a price objection were fielding calls that should never have reached them, and every wasted call cost a real save somewhere else.

“If I send ten people to that queue, the ten should be truly savable. Not two.” my bar for the classifier

Four months of tuning, fifteen points.

I led the NLU quality project first, because that was the tool we had. The intent model returned unknown on roughly 40% of cancellation reasons: no trained intent matched the real, messy ways people explain why they’re leaving.

Four months of tuning bought about fifteen points of precision on the reasons the model could identify, up to roughly 80%. The unknowns barely moved. Meanwhile the saves teams kept 20 to 35% of customers when they knew the real reason. Every unknown was money left on the table, and every tuning cycle took months.

One prompt, thirty-six points.

When LLMs arrived in early 2023, I made the case: stop training intents and classify with a model that already understands natural language. The idea was mine to champion, and making it real meant partnering with the platform team to onboard the API that let the flow call an LLM at all.

The design was the prompt. A tightly specified classifier reads the customer’s words and returns strict JSON, intent, reason, sentiment, savability, that no human ever sees; it’s parsed downstream to make the routing call. The savability rules came from the people who knew the economics: the saves data and the analytics behind it. A customer closing the business or retiring counts as final; they get a respectful, efficient goodbye, not a pitch.

The few-shot examples came from real customer language in all its mess, and the iteration loop is the one that actually moves accuracy: find the failure, add the example that kills that class of error, re-test the whole set. Months of NLU training per cycle collapsed into hours of prompt work.

The same loop later ran at platform scale: find the failure, add the example, re-run everything. It became the backbone of the golden-dataset discipline in No. 03, The Quality Program.

Here’s the routing, reconstructed.

The evaluation came back clean.

I tested the classifier across hundreds of real customer utterances before it touched a live call. Savability accuracy: 96.6%. The share of cancellations resolved to a known reason rose from 60% to 96%, the thirty-six points the NLU ceiling never allowed.

The saves motion it fed was already proven in production: across 8,814 cancellation calls measured from December 2022 through March 2023, the routing flow saved 12% of customers against a 5% target. Price calls, the reason a specialist has the most room to fix, saved at 43%.

The analytics team’s retention model, built on that 2022 experiment, projected $2.9M in retention revenue from routing this accurately.

From prototype to shipped experiment.

Proving the classifier was half the work. I drove it through the Responsible AI review: the safety checks, guardrails, and strict testing required of anything LLM-powered. Its output was JSON read by machines, never by customers, so that review was the gate that mattered, and it cleared.

Then I built the experience end to end in an internal low-code tool and shipped it as a controlled experiment. The approval pattern from that review became the team’s standard for LLM classification use cases, and the routing pattern carried across voice and chat: hear the reason, judge savability, route with context, keep the cancel path one step away.
