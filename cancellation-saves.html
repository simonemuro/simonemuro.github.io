No. 03 · Cancellation Saves

“I want to cancel” isn’t one problem.

It’s a dozen problems wearing the same sentence, and only some of them are losses you have to accept. I spent two generations of technology teaching a support system to hear the difference, because the reason a customer is leaving is the one thing that decides whether you can keep them.

RoleDesigned the NLU saves flow · built the LLM classifier
When2022 – 2023, two generations of the tech
StackNLU intents · hybrid LLM classifier · strict JSON · low-code build
Outcome96.6% classifier accuracy (pre-launch) · saves flow 12% vs 5% target (directional)

First, hear the reason.

Cancellation was one of the highest-volume reasons customers called, and the voice assistant treated every request identically: hand over the cancel steps, let them go. The reason, the one piece of signal that decides whether a customer is savable, was spoken out loud on every call and acted on in none of them.

The growth and retention team asked for the obvious first step: before the flow offers the steps, ask why. I analyzed what customers actually said, bucketed the answers into topics, and partnered with an NLP team on a classification model so the retention strategists could finally see the full spread of reasons they were working with.

Then came the routing experiment. Customers leaving over price, a bad experience, or because they weren’t using the product, the reasons the saves team had validated as genuinely winnable, went straight to dedicated retention specialists with the context attached, so nobody repeated themselves. Everyone else got a clean, respectful cancel path.

The queue had a pollution problem.

It worked, and it surfaced the real quality problem. The saves queue was filling with false positives: customers who weren’t actually canceling, or whose reasons were final. Specialists trained to rescue a price objection were fielding calls that should never have reached them, and every wasted call cost a real save somewhere else.

“If I send ten people to that queue, the ten should be truly savable. Not two.” my bar for the classifier

Four months of tuning, fifteen points.

I took on the NLU quality project first, because that was the only tool we had. The intent model returned unknown on roughly 40% of cancellation reasons: no trained intent matched the real, messy ways people explain why they’re leaving.

Four months of tuning bought me about fifteen points of precision on the reasons the model could identify, up to roughly 80%. The unknowns barely moved. Meanwhile the saves teams kept 20 to 35% of customers when they knew the real reason, so every unknown was money left on the table, and every tuning cycle took months. I knew the ceiling, and I knew I’d hit it.

One prompt, thirty-six points.

So when LLMs arrived in early 2023, I made the call: stop training intents and classify with a model that already understands natural language, because the bottleneck was never my tuning, it was the tool. The idea was mine to champion, and making it real meant partnering with the platform team to onboard the API that let the flow call an LLM at all.

The build was a hybrid system, and the judgment was where each piece belonged. NLU still mapped the intent. The LLM read the customer’s words and returned strict JSON, reason, sentiment, savability, that no human ever sees. A low-code tool parsed that JSON and applied the routing rules. The savability rules came from the people who knew the economics: the saves data and the analytics behind it. A customer closing the business or retiring counts as final; they get a respectful, efficient goodbye, not a pitch.

The few-shot examples came from real customer language in all its mess, and the iteration loop is the one that actually moves accuracy: find the failure, add the example that kills that class of error, re-test the whole set. Months of NLU training per cycle collapsed into hours of prompt work. I costed the running model too: roughly $756 a year at the volume it handled, the build-versus-buy case made in a single line.

The same loop later ran at platform scale: find the failure, add the example, re-run everything. It became the backbone of the golden-dataset discipline in No. 03, The Quality Program.

The evaluation came back clean.

I tested the classifier on a set of real customer utterances sized for 90% confidence, before it touched a live call. Savability accuracy: 96.6%. In evaluation, the share of cancellations it could resolve to a known reason went from 60% to 96%, the thirty-six points the NLU ceiling never allowed. Accuracy, coverage, hallucination, and toxicity were each scored as separate checks.

The saves motion this fed had already been validated in the earlier routing experiment that started this whole thread. Across 8,814 cancellation calls from December 2022 through March 2023, the flow directionally saved 12% of customers against a 5% target, on expert-reported saves. Price calls, the reason a specialist has the most room to fix, saved at roughly 43%, about eight times the rate of calls where the business was simply closing. That gap is the whole argument for savability routing: the reason decides how rescuable the call is.

Built on that experiment, the retention case projected $2.9M in recovered revenue in the quarter that followed, the upside that justified getting savability right.

I proved it before the platform could ship it.

Proving the classifier was half the work. I drove it through the Responsible AI review: the safety checks, guardrails, and strict testing required of anything LLM-powered. Its output was JSON read by machines, never by customers, so that review was the gate that mattered, and it cleared.

What it didn’t have yet was a paved path to production. This was early 2023, before the company had a sanctioned way to ship generative AI, so I built the whole experience end to end in an internal low-code tool and proved it worked, ahead of the platform that would later carry it. The routing pattern is the part that outlived the wait: hear the reason, judge savability, route with context, keep the cancel path one step away. That pattern is the one I keep building on, because once you’ve taught a system to hear the difference, you stop designing flows and start designing judgment.
