Case No. 05 Cancellation saves QuickBooks support

Hearing why QuickBooks customers cancel, and routing the savable ones to a specialist.

Before a QuickBooks subscription cancels, the customer says why. I designed the ask-why flow and the LLM classifier that reads the reason, then routes savable problems to a specialist without ever blocking someone who wants to leave.

01Background

The cancel path, and who fights for it.

I was a conversation designer on the conversational AI team, working on the support assistant that QuickBooks customers reach in chat and on the phone. When a customer comes to cancel, the business has one honest chance to keep them: saves specialists, people trained to review a plan, fix a bad experience, or find a better price. Whether that chance exists at all depends on why the customer is leaving. Some reasons are winnable. Some are final.

The work spans two eras. In 2022 I designed the saves flow itself on the intent platform: ask why before offering the cancel steps, then route the winnable reasons to specialists. In early 2023, when intent training stopped improving how well the system heard those reasons, I built a generative AI classifier to read them instead, at a time when the company had no sanctioned path for shipping generative AI to production.

Conversation designer was my title, and this was hands-on work. I designed the ask-why flow myself, wrote the classifier prompt myself, built the experience end to end in an internal low-code tool, and drove the classifier through the Responsible AI review. Two things were not mine alone, and I say so: the savability rules came from the saves teams, the people who knew which reasons their specialists could actually win, and onboarding the API that let a flow call a language model at all was a partnership with the platform team.

02The problem

One sentence, a dozen problems.

“I want to cancel” is a dozen problems wearing one sentence. Too expensive, not using it, switching products, a bad support experience, duplicate accounts, closing the business, retiring. The intent model could hear the topic, a cancellation is happening, without much trouble. Hearing the reason was the hard part.

Reading reasons on the intent platform meant training intents: label utterances, tune, wait, repeat, months per cycle. By early 2023 I had spent four months in that loop and the curve had gone flat. The system resolved about 60 percent of the reasons customers gave. Two in five came back unknown, and every unknown routed like every other cancellation, straight toward the cancel steps.

03Why it mattered

Every unknown reason was a lost save.

The saves specialists could only fight for a customer when they knew what the fight was about. A price complaint that reaches a specialist with context is a conversation about the plan. A price complaint that reaches nobody is a lost subscription. Every reason the system could not hear was money left on the table.

The queue cut the other way too. A specialist spending time on a call that was never savable, a business closing, a retirement, is a specialist not saving a call that was. The bar I set for the classifier was simple: if I send ten people to that queue, the ten should be truly savable. Not two.

And the final reasons deserved better than a pitch. A customer retiring after thirty years does not want to hear an offer. They want their account closed out properly, with some grace.

  1. Ask whyThe customer names the reason in their own words.
  2. ClassifyThe model writes JSON only; no raw model prose reaches the customer.
  3. RouteSavable reasons get a specialist with context attached.
  4. ExitFinal reasons get a respectful close, no pitch and no trap.

04Exploring solutions

Keep tuning, or change the tool.

Three options were on the table. Commission another tuning cycle: months more labeling, and the last cycle had barely moved the unknowns. Hand the problem to a modeling team: the same method with more hands on it. Or let a large language model read the reason directly, no trained intents at all: a model built to read natural language does not need a training cycle to recognize that “it has gotten too expensive” is about price.

The third option did not officially exist. In early 2023 no sanctioned path shipped generative AI to production at the company. My read was that the bottleneck was the tool, not the tuning, and I championed the swap: I partnered with the platform team to onboard the API that let a flow call a language model at all, and wrote the classifier prompt. What had been six plus months of development per training cycle became days of prompt work.

The constraint shaped the design. Because the model was unproven in this channel, it would never speak to a customer directly, never decide anything a human rule had not defined, and never ship past what I could verify. The next two parts are those boundaries and how I verified them.

05Design decisions

Boundaries first, then the prompt.

Four decisions define the experience. Each one is visible in the shipped prompt in part six or in the routing trace in part seven.

  1. 5.1

    Ask why before the cancel steps, and never block the exit.

    The flow asks the reason first, because the reason decides the route. But the cancellation itself stays one step away the whole time. A saves experience that traps people is a churn experience with extra steps.

  2. 5.2

    Never pitch a final reason.

    Retirement, business closure, duplicate accounts: these are not saves, and treating them like saves is disrespectful. A final reason gets a clean, warm goodbye and a properly closed account. No offer, no queue.

  3. 5.3

    The model returns strict JSON no customer ever reads raw.

    The classifier writes a structured object, and the flow composes the turn from it deterministically. The acknowledgment field is forbidden from offering help or taking action; the offer to connect lives in its own field, present only when the reason is savable. The voice never improvises.

  4. 5.4

    Savability is a default, not a list.

    False only for terminal reasons; true for everything else. When the classifier is unsure whether a reason can be won, the customer gets the specialist, not the door. The category set stays open too, so the model is never forced into a wrong bucket.

06The build

A hybrid, and the shipped prompt.

The build is a hybrid of the two eras. The intent model does what it was always good at: recognizing that a cancellation is happening. The classifier does what intents could not: reading why. And the low-code tool between them parses the JSON, applies the saves teams’ savability rules, and routes the call. Here is the classifier prompt, redacted, otherwise as shipped. Not a reconstruction, not a rewrite for this page.

The decisions from part five are all in the excerpt. The open category set, nine anchored labels plus room for another accurate one. The savability default, false only for terminal reasons. And the two message fields separated on purpose, the acknowledgment forbidden from offering help, the offer in its own field, so the flow composes the turn the same way every time.

Verification came before trust. I tested the classifier on about 300 real customer utterances, a sample sized for 90 percent confidence, and drove it through the Responsible AI review, the gate that mattered most for output read by machines and never shown raw. It cleared: free of hallucination and toxicity findings across the evaluation set.

The saves flow itself, the ask-why-and-route design from 2022, moved real money before the classifier existed. Across 8,814 cancellation calls over three months, December 2022 to March 2023, the flow directionally saved 12 percent of customers against a 5 percent target, on expert-reported saves. Price calls saved at roughly 43 percent, about eight times the rate of calls where the business was simply closing. That gap is the whole argument: the reason decides how rescuable the call is, which is why hearing it was worth a methodology bet. The program exec summary put the value at about $500K in expert savings.

The saves and retention teams later built a retention case on this work and projected $2.9M in retention revenue. That projection is theirs, not mine, and it was never realized. My result on this work is the routing quality: cleaner cancellation reasons handed to the saves team, measured and gated before it went anywhere near a customer.

And the honest ending. The classifier cleared the Responsible AI review, but in early 2023 the platform had no sanctioned path for shipping generative AI to production. So the experience ran end to end in the internal low-code tool, designed, validated, measured, and gated from full deployment until the platform caught up. I proved the pattern before the platform could ship it. The hybrid shape it proved, an intent model for the topic and a language model for the nuance, is the same architecture the charge-lookup redesign shipped two years later.

08Learnings

What I learned, and what I would do differently.

When the curve goes flat, question the tool before commissioning more effort. Four months of tuning taught me more about the intent platform’s ceiling than about customers. The moment I named the bottleneck honestly, the answer stopped being another cycle.

Boundaries are what make an unproven model shippable. The classifier earned its place by what it was forbidden to do: speak raw to a customer, improvise an offer, pitch a retirement. Strict output contracts and human-owned savability rules are how a new tool gets trusted with an old queue.

Verify before you champion. The 300-utterance evaluation and the Responsible AI review were not compliance chores; they were the argument. A measured 96.6 percent is persuasive in a way a demo never is.

Two things I would do differently. I would start the deployment-path conversation with the platform team on day one, alongside the prompt work, so the proof and the path to production land together instead of the proof waiting. And I would separate my result from the program’s ambition sooner: the routing quality was mine to own and measure, and I would state it plainly rather than let a downstream revenue projection stand in for it.
