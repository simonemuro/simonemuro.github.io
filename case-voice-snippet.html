Voice AI Model UX Writing Response Evaluation

Writing GenAI for the ear, where every second has to earn its place.

I co-designed one of Intuit's first GenAI voice experiences for phone support: the disambiguation that clarifies why someone called, the voice-tuned summaries that answer them, the structured output that lets the system route and evaluate — and the response-quality rubric that decided whether any of it was good enough to ship.

The problem

A huge, expensive channel running on rigid scripts.

Inbound calls were roughly 30% of contact volume with a baseline containment around 36% — a $70M-plus cost-saving opportunity if the experience actually helped people. But the voice assistant ran on rigid NLU flows and scripted responses. Callers with a specific problem heard a generic answer, repeated themselves, or got transferred without any context traveling with them.

A new GenAI platform made it possible to generate personalized, voice-friendly answers. The catch: voice is the hardest surface to write for. There's no screen to scan, the customer hears it exactly once, and it's a support context — so a wrong or risky answer isn't a bad sentence, it's a bad outcome.

My role

I designed what the model said — and how we'd know it was good.

  • Designed the disambiguation logic — the clarifying questions that pin down a vague reason for calling before the system commits to an answer.
  • Wrote and tuned the system prompt behind the customer-facing experience, including a structured JSON output: the customer question, action type, disambiguation message, the response itself, task complexity, and off-topic detection.
  • Designed voice-tuned summaries — concise, speakable answers to a proposed solution, instead of reading a help article aloud.
  • Defined the handoff moments — when the system should stop and route to an expert, carrying context with it.
  • Authored the response-quality rubric for the generated answers — an early template that later seeded a broader auto-evaluation rubric used across the org.
The key decision

Design for being heard once — and keep the output structured.

The core decision was to treat the model's job as summarize, don't recite. Instead of reading a solution article aloud, the system generates a short, voice-tuned summary of the proposed fix. That meant deliberately trading completeness for comprehension: a caller can hold one clear next step in their head, not a four-paragraph article.

The second decision was to make the output structured rather than free text. By forcing the model to emit a typed object — intent, disambiguation, response, complexity, off-topic flag — the experience could reason about what to do next, detect when a request was out of scope before generating a risky answer, and be evaluated consistently. Structure is what made the difference between a demo and something safe to put on a phone line.

The outcome

A measured lift on a channel that's expensive to get wrong.

The experience reached Bayesian significance in testing with a +3.9 percentage-point containment lift on the targeted intents — a real movement on a channel where small percentages are large dollars. Beyond the number, it established the team as credible builders of GenAI voice experiences, not just flow designers, and the response rubric I wrote became a reusable template for evaluating generated answers elsewhere.

What I'd carry forward

Structure is a writing decision, not just an engineering one.

The biggest lesson was that the output schema was the content design. Deciding what fields the model must produce — and that it must flag off-topic and complexity before answering — shaped the customer experience more than any single sentence did. I came out of this convinced that for AI, the contract between the model and the product is a core part of the writer's job, and I've designed that way ever since.

Why it maps to Google Fi

Fi is a phone service. "The voice of Google Fi" is, literally, this: writing AI that's heard once, has to be concise and correct, knows when to hand to a person, and can be measured for quality on a high-volume support line.
