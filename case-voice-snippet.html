Voice AI Model UX Writing Response Evaluation

GenAI on the phone line, the one channel where a wrong answer is a wrong outcome.

In late 2024, when putting GenAI on a live support phone line still felt risky to most teams, I bet it was exactly where it could pay off. Inbound calls were a third of all contact volume and the costliest place to give a bad answer. Voice is also the hardest surface to write for: there's no screen, the caller hears it once, and a wrong answer isn't a bad sentence, it's a bad outcome. I designed the disambiguation flow, wrote the system prompt, defined the structured output the experience runs on, and authored the rubric that decided whether any of it was good enough to ship. The bet that made it work: treat structure as a writing decision.

My role
Conversation Designer & Prompt Engineer. I owned what the model said and how we judged it
Surface
Intuit phone support (Intelligent Voice Assistant)
What I did
Disambiguation flow, system prompt, structured JSON output, voice-tuned summaries, response-evaluation rubric
Context
One of Intuit's first GenAI voice experiences; I owned the conversation-AI and evaluation design
The problem

A huge, expensive channel running on rigid scripts.

Inbound calls were roughly 30% of contact volume with a baseline containment around 36%, a $70M-plus cost-saving opportunity if the experience actually helped people. But the voice assistant ran on rigid NLU flows and scripted responses. A caller with a specific problem heard a generic answer, repeated themselves, or got transferred with no context traveling with them.

When a GenAI platform landed internally, the easy move was to point it at chat, where a clumsy answer is just a line you can scroll past. I argued for the harder, higher-value bet: voice. There's no screen to scan, the caller hears it once, and in support a wrong or risky answer isn't a bad sentence, it's a bad outcome.

My role

I designed what the model said, and how we'd know it was good.

  • Designed the disambiguation flow, the clarifying questions that pin down a vague reason for calling before the system commits to an answer.
  • Wrote the system prompt behind the live experience and defined its structured JSON output: the customer's question, the action to take, a clarifying question, the spoken response, task complexity, and an off-topic flag. On a phone line, that contract is what keeps a generated answer safe to say out loud.
  • Designed the voice-tuned summaries, concise, speakable answers to a proposed fix, instead of reading a help article aloud.
  • Defined the handoff moments, when the system should stop and route to an expert, carrying context with it.
  • Authored the response-evaluation rubric that decided whether a generated answer was good enough to ship. It became one of the early templates the org's automated-evaluation rubric was built from.
The key decision

Design for being heard once, and keep the output structured.

The core decision was to treat the model's job as summarize, don't recite. Instead of reading a solution article aloud, the system generates a short, voice-tuned summary of the proposed fix. That meant deliberately trading completeness for comprehension: a caller can hold one clear next step in their head, not a four-paragraph article.

The second decision was to make the output structured rather than free text. Forcing the model to emit a typed object let the experience decide what to do next, catch an out-of-scope request before it generated a risky answer, and be judged the same way every time. Structure is what made the difference between a demo and something safe to put on a phone line. It is also what made the answers measurable, and the measurable version is the one that moved containment: the +3.9 point lift was downstream of that decision, not separate from it.

The lift landed on a channel where small percentages are large dollars, against a roughly 36% containment baseline on about a third of all contacts. It also changed how the team was seen: credible builders of GenAI voice, not only flow designers. And the rubric I wrote to judge these answers became one of the templates the org's later automated evaluation was seeded from.

What I'd carry forward

Structure is a writing decision, not just an engineering one.

The biggest lesson was that the output schema was the content design. Deciding what fields the model must produce, and that it must flag off-topic and complexity before answering, shaped the customer experience more than any single sentence did. The hard part was choosing what to leave out: comprehension over completeness, one clear next step over a full article, and the schema is where I enforced that decision. I came out of this convinced that for AI, the contract between the model and the product is a core part of the writer's job, and I've designed that way ever since.

The transferable principle

Writing for the ear is a different discipline from writing for a screen. An answer heard once has to be concise and correct, the system has to know when to hand off to a person, and the whole thing has to be measurable on a high-volume line. Get that right and voice stops being the channel teams fear and becomes one a model can be trusted with.
