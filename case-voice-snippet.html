Voice Snippet: GenAI on the phone line, 18 months before the platform was ready

Putting a generated answer on a live support call is the hardest version of the GenAI problem: no screen, no scrolling back, one chance to be heard. I originated the concept in June 2023 and co-designed the production experience that shipped in December 2024. The gap between those dates is part of the story.

June 2023: the prototype the platform couldn't carry yet

The idea started as an "answer summary" experience in early GenAI use-case work: instead of reading help-article content at a caller, generate a short, voice-tuned answer to their actual problem. I prototyped it in June 2023. It was blocked: there was no approved deployment path for GenAI on the phone line yet, and a live call is the wrong place to improvise one. When the platform caught up, the concept shipped in December 2024, and I co-designed the production experience. The concept was mine; the ship was a team's.

Why voice, why it mattered

Inbound calls were roughly 30% of contact volume: a $70M+ opportunity against a 36% baseline containment. And the channel ran on rigid scripts: a caller with a specific problem heard a generic read-out, repeated themselves, or got transferred with no context traveling with them. The easy move for GenAI was chat, where a clumsy answer is just a line you scroll past. Voice is harder and worth more. A screen can show detail. Voice has to earn every second.

The craft: every word the caller hears is a field with a job

My pieces of the production design were the behavior and the contract. I designed the disambiguation questions that pin down a vague caller intent: "payroll" or "invoices" alone isn't answerable; one well-chosen spoken question makes it answerable. I wrote and tuned the system prompt behind the experience. I defined the JSON-structured output the whole experience runs on, parsed downstream for routing, never read by a human. I designed the escalation moments: when the system should stop, hand off to an expert, and carry the context with it so the caller never starts over. And I authored the response-quality evaluation rubric that decided whether any generated answer was good enough to say out loud, a rubric that went on to seed the team's broader automated-evaluation rubric.

customer_questionWhat the caller actually asked, normalized.
action_typeDecides what the caller hears next: answer · disambiguate · handoff · off_topic
disambiguationOne spoken question to narrow a vague intent. Just one, because the caller is on the phone.
responseThe spoken answer. Short by design: the caller hears it once.
task_complexityInforms whether to answer or hand off to an expert with context.
off_topicCaught before generation, so a risky request never becomes a spoken answer.

The structure was the safety case and the content design at once. Forcing the model into a typed contract is what let the experience catch an out-of-scope request before generating, route on complexity instead of hoping, and be judged the same way on every call. The choice that shaped the writing most was what to leave out: summarize, don't recite. One clear next step a caller can hold in their head, not a four-paragraph article read aloud.

The outcome

+3.9 pp containment lift

On the call types it served, statistically significant in production A/B testing. Live since December 2024, on a channel where small percentages are large dollars against a 36% baseline.

The second outcome was harder to put in a callout: the ship established the design team as credible GenAI builders: people who could write the prompt, define the contract, and author the evaluation, not just design the flow around someone else's model.

What this proves

That the contract between the model and the product is a writer's job: the output schema shaped the customer experience more than any single sentence did. That voice, the channel teams fear most, becomes trustable when every generated word passes through structure and a rubric. And that being early only counts when it's also rigorous: the 2023 prototype earned its 2024 ship because the behavior, the contract, and the definition of good were already worked out.
