No. 04 · Voice Snippet

One chance to be heard.

I kept coming back to the one GenAI surface I least wanted to get wrong first: a generated answer spoken on a live phone call. No screen, no scrolling back, one shot at being understood, which made it the hardest version of the problem. I prototyped the concept in June 2023; the production version has been answering callers since December 2024.

A caller can't scroll past a bad answer.

Callers didn't trust being pointed at a help article. They'd reached out to a person, or thought they had, for a reason, and the channel answered by reading generic content at them: they repeated themselves, or got transferred with nothing traveling along. The phone was a large share of support contact volume and a containment opportunity scripts had barely touched, but the number that mattered was the behavior underneath it.

Chat was the easy place to put GenAI first. A clumsy answer in chat is just a line you scroll past. On the phone, every generated word spends the caller's time and patience, and there's no taking it back. That's exactly what made the channel worth it.

Highest-risk channel, so it shipped last.

The concept started in June 2023 as an answer summary: instead of reading help content at the caller, generate a short, voice-tuned answer to their actual problem. It was one of three GenAI use cases I worked up that season, so I triaged them by generative risk because the order we shipped them in was the real safety decision. Two were low-risk and moved. This one put generated words directly into a customer's ear, with no screen to soften a miss, so it was the riskiest of the three.

That risk is also why it waited, and waiting was the call. Customer-facing generation needed a platform path that didn't exist yet, so rather than sit on a working concept I pushed for an alternative route and made the case for it until we could ship safely. It went live in December 2024 as the first GenAI voice experience on the line. I prototyped the concept; the production ship was a team's.

A screen can show detail. Voice has to earn every second.Simone, the design rule for the channel

The contract is the design.

My pieces of the production design were the behavior and the contract. The experience runs on a six-field JSON output, parsed downstream for routing and never read by a human, and the hardest field to get right is the one a caller actually hears: a two-sentence spoken answer, voice-tuned, that has to land clearly the first and only time it's said. I authored and tuned that language. Every word a caller hears is a field with a job.

customer_questionWhat the caller actually asked, normalized.
action_typeDecides what the caller hears next: answer · disambiguate · handoff · off_topic
disambiguationOne spoken question to narrow a vague intent. Just one, because the caller is on the phone.
responseThe spoken answer. Two sentences by design: the caller hears it once.
task_complexityRoutes between answering and handing the call to an expert, with context.
off_topicCaught before generation, so a risky request never becomes a spoken answer.

The structure is the safety case and the content design at once. An out-of-scope request gets caught before a word is generated. Complexity routes the call to an expert with the context carried along, so the caller never starts over. And the answer stays short on purpose: one clear next step a caller can hold in their head, not an article performed at them. The contract above is reconstructed and redacted for a public version; the shape, and the job each field does, are real.

It's been on the line since December 2024.

+3.9 percentage points of containment lift, measured against a control in production. On a channel this size, a few points is a large number of calls that resolve without a human.

The other half of the work was the rubric. I authored the standard that decided whether a generated answer was good enough to say out loud, defining what "good" means for an answer a caller hears once and can't scroll back. Writing that for a live voice channel is its own discipline: every criterion has to survive being spoken, not just read. Once you've drawn the line for the riskiest channel there is, the easier surfaces stop being the scary part, and that's the bar I bring to the next one.
