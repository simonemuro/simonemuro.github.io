Prompt Engineering LLM Classification Responsible AI Retention

An LLM that spots a savable cancellation and offers real help, not a dead-end.

In early 2023, before LLMs were standard tooling for this work, I led the design of QuickBooks' GenAI Cancel Saves experience and built it end to end. The old flow only ever showed people how to cancel. The new one understands why they're leaving, and when a specialist could still help, it offers to connect them.

My role
Lead Conversation Designer & Prompt Engineer. Led the design; built the flow end to end
Surface
QuickBooks Digital Assistant and Intelligent Voice Assistant (IVA)
What I did
Flow design, classification prompt, routing logic, model evaluation, Responsible AI, build, test, ship
Context
One workstream of a larger Saves & Retention program; I owned the conversation-AI piece
The problem

The old flow treated every subscription cancellation request the same, and that was the bug.

When someone asked to cancel, the outcome was always the same: the assistant pointed them to the cancel steps. Cancellation was a top contact driver, high-traffic across both the phone line and the Digital Assistant, so handling every case the same way was a large, repeated missed opportunity, both to serve the customer better and to keep the ones who were likely to stay.

By 2022 the flow did try to read why, using an NLU model to classify the cancellation reason, and it left two gaps. About 40% of the time it returned "unknown" because no trained intent matched what the customer said, a coverage gap. And on the roughly 60% it could label, it was only about 80% accurate, so even when it produced a reason, nearly one in five was wrong, a precision gap. Any routing built on that would inherit both: customers it misread, and customers it could not read at all. Yet when the reason was captured correctly, the saves teams retained a meaningful share of those customers (the mature retention teams ran 20 to 35%). We were losing savable customers for the simple reason that we could not reliably read why they were leaving.

How I framed it

Study the landscape, then fix what "cancel" even means.

Before designing anything, I ran a competitive teardown of how leading assistants handle cancellation, from Bank of America's Erica to Amazon to Capital One's Eno. Three principles carried into the design. Confirm what the customer actually means before acting. Read the reason and respond to it, instead of pretending the cancel request never happened. And keep the exit honest: offer real help, but never hide the cancel button or trap anyone in a retention maze the way a hard "are you sure?" wall does.

The first real design problem was disambiguation. "Cancel payroll" is genuinely ambiguous: it could mean the Payroll subscription, a single scheduled payroll run, or a one-off payment to a worker. Defaulting to "cancel the subscription" would be the most expensive possible misread, so I designed the flow to confirm what the customer actually meant before doing anything irreversible.

The build

An LLM that reads the reason, judges savability, and earns the right to route.

I reframed the task from intent-matching to classification with reasoning. Each cancellation reason now goes to an LLM that classifies the reason and sentiment and returns the one judgment the routing depends on: is this customer savable, someone a trained specialist could realistically still help? I defined savability with analytics and PM partners, wrote the LLM system instructions that produced the structured output, and built the routing in Intuit's low-code platform: the classification, the scripted fallbacks, the rules that offered savable customers a specialist and sent everyone else to a clean cancel path, and the tests. A conversation designer owning the full stack, with minimal engineering support.

Against the old NLU model the gains were not incremental. Known-reason coverage rose from about 60% to 96%, and savability, the decision the routing actually turns on, reached 96.6% accuracy. Just as important for a model that speaks to customers at their most frustrated, it cleared the full Responsible AI evaluation suite I ran: 0% hallucinations, 0% toxicity, safety-approved. And because the logic lived in a prompt rather than a trained intent model, development went from six-plus months to days.

Wiring a model to a routing decision raises the bar, because the errors are not equal. A false "savable" wastes a leaving customer's time and pulls specialists onto calls they cannot save, dragging down the save rate they are measured on. A false "not savable" quietly loses someone the team could have kept. So I tuned and tested against both costs, not one headline accuracy number. The routing stayed an offer, never a forced transfer, with the clean cancel path always one step away. And the offer carried value beyond retention: customers who are leaving often have things in motion, active payroll runs, scheduled payments, connected products, and a specialist makes sure those close out correctly. Even when someone still chose to go, getting them to a person who could handle it properly was the better experience.

The outcome

It shipped, and it was accurate enough to route a revenue-critical decision.

96.6%accuracy identifying savable cancellations
0%hallucinations or toxicity, Responsible AI approved
60% → 96%cancellations resolved to a known reason
~$2.9Mprojected retention revenue, 3 months

It shipped to production across the Digital Assistant and the voice line. The model identified savable customers accurately enough that the Saves and Retention team trusted it to route on, and their analysts projected roughly $2.9M in retention revenue over the next three months. The conversions and the dollar model were theirs; the classification that decided who reached a specialist was mine, and it was the input everything downstream depended on.

What I'd carry forward

The biggest gain after launch came from how the offer was framed.

The classifier did its job, but the routing taught me the rest. The first version simply offered to connect: "I can connect you with a specialist who can help." Many customers declined. After launch I tested leading with the value, using what the model already knew to make the stakes concrete before offering: "I see you have active payroll and payments scheduled, I can connect you with a specialist who'll make sure those are handled correctly before anything changes." Far more customers accepted. That is the principle I carry forward: an accurate model earns the right to make an offer, but people only say yes when they can see what is in it for them. The classification and the framing are one design, not two.

The transferable principle

Cancellations and downgrades are where any subscription business keeps or loses a customer. Reading the real reason, responding with empathy, and routing to the right help without ever feeling like a trap is the conversation every subscription product has to get right at the edge of churn.
