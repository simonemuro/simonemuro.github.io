Prompt Engineering LLM Classification Responsible AI Retention

An LLM that turns a cancellation into a retention opportunity.

In early 2023, while most teams were still asking whether LLMs were safe for production, I saw a problem they were built for. QuickBooks was losing customers it could have kept, month after month, because the support assistant could not reliably read why anyone was leaving. I made the case for a large language model, designed the Cancel Saves experience around it, and built it end to end: a classifier that spots a savable cancellation in real time, on the phone and in chat, and turns it into a retention opportunity.

My role
Lead Conversation Designer & Prompt Engineer. I drove it from first idea to production
Surface
QuickBooks Digital Assistant and Intelligent Voice Assistant (IVA)
What I did
Classification prompt, savability criteria, JSON output schema, routing logic, model evaluation, Responsible AI, build, ship
Context
One workstream of a larger Saves & Retention program; I owned the conversation-AI piece
The problem

The old flow treated every subscription cancellation request the same, and that was the bug.

When a customer asked to cancel, the outcome never varied: the assistant handed over the cancel steps and let them go. Cancellation was one of our highest-volume contacts, on the phone line and in the Digital Assistant alike, so each of those exits was a potentially savable customer we lost without ever trying.

It was not that we never asked. By 2022 the flow ran an NLU model to classify the cancellation reason, and it failed in two ways that compounded. It returned "unknown" on roughly 40% of cancellations, because no trained intent matched what the customer actually said: a recall gap. And on the 60% it did label, it was right about 80% of the time: a precision gap that any downstream routing would inherit. So we were either blind to the reason or working from a wrong one, in the exact moment when reading it correctly was worth real money. When the saves teams did get an accurate reason, they kept 20 to 35% of those customers. We were leaving that on the table.

The reason was right there. The flow couldn't act on it, so everyone got the same cancel steps: no judgment, no help, no routing.
The prompt

The accuracy came from prompt design, not from training an NLU model, so the design was the prompt.

The shift was from intent-matching to classification with reasoning, and it put everything on the prompt. I started with what "savable" even meant, because nobody had defined it. I drove that definition with the analytics team: a reason was savable only if a trained specialist had a real play to keep the customer, like price or a missing feature, and not when the decision was already final, like a business that had closed. That definition became the bar the classifier had to hit, not my guess at it.

From there I wrote the classifier as a tightly specified persona: a clear role and task, the rules it had to hold to, and a strict JSON output, because that output was never read by a human. It was parsed downstream to make the routing call. I gave it few-shot examples drawn from the messy, real ways people explain why they are leaving, then iterated the way you actually move accuracy: not by wordsmithing the prompt, but by finding where it failed, adding the example that killed that class of error, and re-testing every version against our Responsible AI bar. That loop is what took savability classification to 96.6% accuracy with zero hallucinations or toxicity.

The build

Routing on the model's call, without ever feeling like a trap.

With a classification I could trust, the rest was the experience around it. I built it in Intuit's low-code platform: the rules that offered savable customers a specialist, the clean path that took everyone else straight to cancel, the scripted fallbacks, and the tests. One conversation designer owning the full stack, with minimal engineering support. The same classifier drove both surfaces, the phone line (IVA) and the in-product assistant (DA), so the intelligence didn't fork by channel. And because the logic lived in a prompt instead of a trained intent model, what used to take six-plus months of NLU work shipped in days.

Wiring a model to a routing decision raises the stakes, because the two errors aren't equal. A false "savable" wastes a leaving customer's time and pulls a specialist onto a call they were never going to save, which drags down the save rate they're measured on. A false "not savable" quietly loses someone the team could have kept. I tuned and tested against both costs rather than one headline accuracy number, and I kept the routing an offer, never a forced transfer, with the cancel path always one tap away. The offer earned its place beyond retention, too: people who are leaving usually have things in motion, active payroll runs, scheduled payments, connected products, and a specialist makes sure those land safely. Even when someone still chose to go, sending them to a person who could close things out cleanly was the better experience.

The outcome

It shipped, and it was accurate enough to route a revenue-critical decision.

96.6%accuracy identifying savable cancellations
0%hallucinations or toxicity, Responsible AI approved
60% → 96%cancellations resolved to a known reason
$2.9Mrealized retention revenue, first 3 months

It shipped to production on both the phone line and the in-product assistant, and delivered $2.9M in realized retention revenue in its first three months. The classification that decided who reached a specialist was mine, and the entire retention funnel depended on getting it right.

What I'd carry forward

The model opened the door. The right framing got customers to walk through it.

A correct classification earns you the right to make an offer. It doesn't make anyone accept it. The first version of the routing offered plainly: "I can connect you with a specialist who can help." Plenty of customers declined. After launch I tested leading with the value, using what the model already knew to make the stakes concrete first: "I see you have active payroll and payments scheduled, I can connect you with a specialist who'll make sure those are handled correctly before anything changes." Acceptance jumped. The lesson I carry: the model's output and the words wrapped around it are one design. A great classification, framed carelessly, still loses the customer.

First version: a generic offer
Before you go, I can connect you with a specialist who can help. Would you like that?
No thanks, just cancel.

The transferable principle

Strip away the use case and the shape repeats everywhere: a messy human signal that has to become a decision the business can act on. The strategy is seeing where a model can carry that weight early; the craft is the prompt that makes its output trustworthy, and the conversation that gets a person to act on it. Cancellation was one version of that problem. Fraud, triage, and escalation are the same shape, and they are won or lost on the same two things.
