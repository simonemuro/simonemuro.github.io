Prompt Engineering LLM Classification Retention

An LLM that spots a savable cancellation, and routes it with empathy.

QuickBooks' cancellation flow used to treat every request the same: it just showed people how to cancel. I drove a two-phase GenAI rebuild from ideation to launch, first proving customers would tell us why they were leaving, then using an LLM to read the reason and route the savable ones to a retention specialist.

My role
Lead Conversation Designer & Prompt Engineer; drove it end to end, ideation to launch
Surface
QuickBooks Digital Assistant and Intelligent Voice Assistant (the voice assistant)
What I did
Flow design, classification prompt, Responsible AI, build, testing, shipping
Where it sits
Hybrid LLM + rules · GenAI in production support
The problem

The old flow treated every subscription cancellation request the same, and that was the bug.

When someone asked to cancel, the assistant did exactly one thing: it showed them how to cancel. No attempt to understand why they were leaving, and no attempt to keep them. Cancellation was a top contact driver, a high-traffic intent across both the phone line and the Digital Assistant, so handling it as a dead-end was a large, repeated missed opportunity to serve the customer better and retain more of the customers who were likely to stay.

This started back in 2022, before LLMs were an option, and that first version leaned entirely on NLU. Training intents to capture cancellation reasons worked to a point, but it was limited and slow to build, and about 40% of reasons still came back classified as "unknown." That gap was expensive: when the reason was captured, retention specialists saved as many as 35% of those customers. We were losing savable customers for the simple reason that we could not reliably read why they were leaving.

How I framed it

Study the landscape, then fix what "cancel" even means.

Before designing anything, I ran a competitive teardown of how leading assistants handle cancellation, from Bank of America's Erica to Amazon to Capital One's Eno. Four principles carried straight into my design: disambiguate before acting, ask for the reason with empathy, never leave a dead-end, and prefer a soft, respectful retention offer over a hard stop.

The first fix was disambiguation. A request like "cancel payroll" is genuinely ambiguous: it could mean the Payroll subscription, a single payroll run, or a one-off payment to someone. I designed the flow so it never defaulted to cancelling a subscription unless the customer said so, because the most expensive mistake here is confidently cancelling the wrong thing.

The build

I shipped it in two phases, on purpose.

Phase 1: prove customers will tell us why. The assistant had never asked for a reason, and an LLM cannot classify a reason it never hears. So before building any classification, I embedded a single, natural, empathetic question into the flow ("We're sad to see you go. Can you tell me why you're cancelling today?") and shipped just that, to test whether customers would answer or bristle. About 80% offered a reason. The win came from keeping it short, human, and non-defensive, not from interrogating anyone.

Phase 2: read the reason and route on it. With reasons now flowing in, I handed each one to an LLM to classify the reason and sentiment and return a savability judgment. I worked with Analytics and PMs to define savability itself, which reasons a trained retention specialist could realistically act on, then wrote the LLM system instructions that returned that classification and the backend rules and fulfillment logic that routed savable customers to a specialist and everyone else to clean cancellation steps. Moving from the old NLU model to GenAI lifted reason classification from about 60% to 96% and cut model development from six-plus months to days.

Before: assume & instruct
I want to cancel my subscription.
To cancel your subscription, go to Settings → Billing → Cancel. Your access ends at the end of the billing period.
After: ask, read, route
We're sad to see you go, and we really value your feedback. Can you tell me why you're cancelling today?
I haven't really used the product yet.
I understand. I'm connecting you with a specialist who can help. One moment please.

The outcome

Accurate enough to route a revenue-critical decision.

~80%of customers offered a reason when the assistant asked
96.6%savability-classification accuracy
60% → 96%reason-classification rate, NLU to GenAI
6+ mo → daysmodel development time with GenAI

Hitting 96.6% accuracy on savability is what made it safe to wire the classifier to a real routing decision instead of a guess. Customers a specialist could realistically help were handed off with their reason and context attached; everyone else got a clean, respectful path to cancel. It shipped as one of the first GenAI experiences in QuickBooks' support stack.

What the data taught me

The empathy wasn't decoration. It was load-bearing.

The most useful lesson came after launch, from measurement. In a later revision, cancellation handling was shifted off the generative flow toward more rules-based responses, and the team's conversation-quality scores fell. The drop was a measured reminder that the model's empathy and nuance in this moment were doing real work, not just sounding nice.

My takeaway: in emotionally loaded moments, "technically correct" and "actually helpful" are different things, and only evaluation tells you which one you shipped. I'd argue harder, earlier, to keep the model in the loop precisely where the human read matters most.

The transferable principle

Cancellations and downgrades are where any subscription business keeps or loses a customer. Reading the real reason, responding with empathy, and routing to the right help without ever feeling like a trap is the conversation every subscription product has to get right at the edge of churn.
