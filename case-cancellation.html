Prompt Engineering LLM Classification Retention

An LLM that knows who's worth saving — and how to ask.

The first GenAI experience to ship in QuickBooks' Digital Assistant. I was the prompt engineer and tester for an LLM that reads a cancellation request — its reason, sentiment, and savability — and replaces a cold "here's how to cancel" with a disambiguating, empathetic handoff to a retention specialist.

My role
Prompt Engineer & Tester (IC)
Surface
QuickBooks Digital Assistant (also Search, Intuit Intelligence)
What I did
Classification prompt, structured output, ~300-utterance tuning, empathetic handoff copy
Where it sits
Hybrid LLM + rules · GenAI in production support
The problem

The old flow treated every "cancel" the same — and that was the bug.

When a customer typed something like "cancel my subscription," the assistant assumed the worst, showed no empathy about losing them, and dropped straight into cancellation instructions. No attempt to understand why, and no attempt to help. For a subscription business, that's leaving retention on the floor.

But the obvious fix — "always try to save them" — is its own trap. I sampled 299 real utterances mapped to the cancel intent and found the population was mixed:

  • 65% genuinely wanted to cancel
  • 14% were unsure whether they even wanted to
  • 21% had nothing to do with canceling an account at all

So the design had a real ethical and UX constraint baked in, one the team named explicitly: a concern about pushing people toward churn. Strong-arming the 65% who'd decided, or mis-routing the 21% who weren't even canceling, would be worse than the cold flow. The system had to tell these groups apart before it did anything.

My role

I built and tuned the classifier — and wrote the words around it.

  • Designed the classification prompt with a structured JSON output that read more than a single flag: the cancellation reason, the customer's sentiment and level of frustration, and a savability judgment.
  • Tuned and tested it against ~300 real utterances, measuring classification accuracy and chasing down the misses until the behavior was reliable.
  • Defined what "savable" actually means — price, switching to another product, not using it — by working with the retention team on the cancellation reasons that a specialist can realistically address.
  • Wrote the conversation: a gentle disambiguation for vague "cancel" utterances, a non-defensive probe into the reason, and — when savable — an empathetic offer to connect to a specialist by chat or callback.
  • Designed the graceful exit: if the reason isn't savable or the customer declines, the flow shows them how to cancel cleanly and follows up with data-backup advice. No dark patterns.
The key decision

Classify the moment, not just the intent.

The pivotal call was to make the model read several signals at once — reason, sentiment, savability — rather than emit a single "savable: yes/no." A boolean would have been simpler to build and evaluate. But a boolean can't tell the difference between an angry customer who's already gone and a hesitant one who just needs a better plan, and it would have steamrolled the 14% who were unsure and the 21% who weren't canceling at all.

Reading the whole moment let the flow do the humane thing: disambiguate first, save only where saving is genuinely possible, and route everyone else respectfully. The tradeoff was a more complex prompt and a wider evaluation surface — which is exactly why I held it to a measured accuracy bar before it shipped.

Before — assume & instruct

Customer: "I want to cancel my subscription."

Assistant: "To cancel your subscription, go to Settings → Billing → Cancel. Your access ends at the end of the billing period."

After — read & respond

Customer: "I want to cancel my subscription."

Assistant: "Happy to help with that. Can I ask what's prompting it — cost, a feature, something not working? If it's something a specialist could fix, I can connect you in a couple of minutes."

The outcome

Accurate enough to trust with a revenue-critical decision.

96.6%savability-classification accuracy across ~300 utterances
1stGenAI experience to ship in the QuickBooks Digital Assistant
$2.9Mprojected 90-day retention from the saves flow

The classifier hit 96.6% accuracy on savability across the test set, which is what made it safe to wire to a real routing decision: savable customers were handed to the retention team — by chat or callback, with the case context attached — instead of being shown a cancel button. It shipped as the Digital Assistant's first generative experience, with a projected $2.9M in 90-day retention.

What the data taught me

The empathy wasn't decoration — it was load-bearing.

The most useful lesson came after launch, from measurement. In a later revision, cancellation handling was shifted off the generative flow toward more rules-based responses — and our conversation-quality scores fell. The drop was a measured reminder that the model's empathy and nuance in this moment were doing real work, not just sounding nice.

My takeaway: in emotionally loaded moments, "technically correct" and "actually helpful" are different things, and only evaluation tells you which one you shipped. I'd argue harder, earlier, to keep the model in the loop precisely where the human read matters most.

Why it maps to Google Fi

Cancellations and downgrades are where a subscription business keeps or loses a customer. Reading the real reason, responding with empathy, and routing to the right help — without ever feeling like a trap — is precisely the conversation Fi has to get right at the edge of churn.
