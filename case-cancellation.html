Cancellation Saves: teaching the system to hear why customers leave

A customer who says "I want to cancel" isn't one problem. They are a dozen, and only some of them are losses you have to accept. This is the story of teaching a support system to hear the difference: first with the tools of 2022, then with a prompt that beat months of NLU work in hours, and what shipped when.

Role
Lead conversation designer & prompt engineer
Surface
The voice assistant; pattern later shipped across voice and chat
What I did
Routing design, NLU tuning, classifier prompt, JSON schema, evaluation, RAI approval, end-to-end build
Outcome
12% saves vs 5% target · 96.6% classifier accuracy

December 2022: route on the reason, not the request

Cancellation was one of the highest-volume reasons customers called, and the flow treated every request identically: hand over the cancel steps, let them go. The reason, the one piece of signal that decides whether a customer is savable, was spoken out loud on every call and acted on in none of them.

I designed the savable-cancellation routing for the voice assistant. The flow disambiguated the cancel intent, probed for the reason, and split the path: customers leaving for price, switching products, or not using the product (the three reasons the saves team validated as genuinely winnable) were routed to dedicated retention experts with the case context pre-populated, so nobody repeated themselves. Everyone else got a clean, respectful cancel path. If the business closed, we helped them leave well.

Dialogue reconstructed from the shipped flow; production copy is confidential. The design is real: hear the reason, judge savability, route with context, and keep the cancel path one step away.

It worked, and we measured it. Across 8,814 cancellation calls from December 2022 through March 2023, the flow saved 12% of customers against a 5% target. Calls where the reason was price, the reason a specialist has the most room to fix, saved at 43%.

The queue had a pollution problem

The work started further back than the routing. The growth and retention team first asked us to collect the cancellation reason at all: before the flow offered cancel steps, it would ask why. I analyzed what customers actually said, bucketed the reasons into topics, and partnered with an NLP team on a classification model so the retention strategists could finally see the reasons landscape they were working with.

Then came the routing experiment, and with it the real problem. The saves queue was being polluted with false positives: customers who weren't actually canceling, or whose reasons were final. Specialists trained to rescue a price objection were fielding calls that should never have reached them. My bar for the classifier came straight from that queue: if I send ten people to the saves specialists, ten should be genuinely savable. Not two.

Four months of tuning, fifteen points of progress

I led the NLU quality project first, because that was the tool we had. The intent model returned "unknown" on roughly 40% of cancellation reasons: no trained intent matched the real, messy ways people explain why they're leaving. Four months of intent tuning bought roughly fifteen points of precision on the reasons it could identify, to about 80%. The unknowns barely moved. Meanwhile the saves teams kept 20–35% of customers when they knew the real reason: every "unknown" was money left on the table, and every tuning cycle took months.

Early 2023: stop training intents, start prompting a model

When LLMs arrived, I saw the opening and made the case: stop training intents and classify with a model that already understands natural language. The idea was mine to champion, and making it real meant partnering with the platform team to onboard the API that let the flow call an LLM at all. The design was the prompt: a tightly specified classifier persona emitting strict JSON (intent, reason, sentiment, savability) that no human ever reads; it's parsed downstream to make the routing call.

The savability rules came from the people who knew the economics: the growth and retention team's saves data and the analytics behind it. A customer closing their business or retiring counts as final; they get a respectful, efficient cancel path, not a pitch. The few-shot examples came from real customer language, in all its mess. And the iteration loop was the one that actually moves accuracy: find the failure, add the example that kills that class of error, re-test the whole set.

roleA cancellation-reason classifier for subscription support.
taskRead the customer's stated reason. Classify intent, reason, and sentiment; decide whether a specialist could still help.
rulesClassify only what the customer actually said. Never invent a reason; if unclear, return unknown instead of guessing.
output{ intent, reason, sentiment, savability }. Strict JSON, parsed downstream to make the routing decision. No prose, ever.
few-shot"It's just too expensive right now" → reason: price · savability: true
"I sold the business" → reason: no longer needed · savability: false

I tested it across hundreds of real customer utterances. The classifier hit 96.6% savability accuracy, lifted the share of cancellations resolved to a known reason from 60% to 96%, and produced zero hallucinations and zero toxicity across the test set. Estimated run cost: about $756 a year. And the dev-time story might be the biggest number of all: what took months of NLU training per iteration collapsed into hours of prompt work.

Classifier accuracy measured across hundreds of real customer utterances, zero hallucinations or toxicity, ~$756/year to run. The analytics team's retention model, built on the 2022 experiment, projected $2.9M in retention revenue from routing this accurately.

From prototype to shipped experiment

Proving the classifier was half the work. I drove it through the Responsible AI review (the internal gauntlet every LLM-powered experience had to clear: safety checks, guardrails, strict testing), then built the experience end to end in an internal low-code tool and shipped it as a controlled experiment. The approval pattern from that review became the team's standard for LLM classification use cases, and the pattern itself carried across voice and chat.

What this proves

Three things. First, problem-first prompt craft under a real business constraint: the prompt wasn't a demo, it was a routing decision with asymmetric costs on each kind of error. Second, evaluation rigor: accuracy, classification coverage, hallucination rate, toxicity, and run cost were all measured across hundreds of real utterances. Third, the pattern generalizes: fraud triage, escalation, any problem shaped like read the messy human reason, then route the decision is won the same way: a tightly ruled classifier, structured output, and an eval strong enough to stake the routing on.
