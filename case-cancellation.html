Prompt Engineering LLM Classification Retention

An LLM that spots a savable cancellation, and routes it with empathy.

In early 2023, before LLMs were standard tooling for this work, I led the design of QuickBooks' GenAI Cancel Saves experience and built it end to end. The old flow only ever showed people how to cancel. The new one understands why they are leaving and routes the customers a specialist can actually help to a human who can act.

My role
Lead Conversation Designer & Prompt Engineer. Led the design; built the flow end to end
Surface
QuickBooks Digital Assistant and Intelligent Voice Assistant (IVA)
What I did
Flow design, intent architecture, classification prompt, routing logic, Responsible AI, build, test, ship
Context
One workstream of a larger Saves & Retention program; I owned the conversation-AI piece
The problem

The old flow treated every subscription cancellation request the same, and that was the bug.

When someone asked to cancel, the assistant did exactly one thing: it showed them how to cancel. No attempt to understand why they were leaving, and no attempt to keep them. Cancellation was a top contact driver, high-traffic across both the phone line and the Digital Assistant, so handling it as a dead-end was a large, repeated missed opportunity to serve the customer better and retain more of the customers who were likely to stay.

This started in 2022, before LLMs were an option, and that first version leaned entirely on NLU. Training intents to capture cancellation reasons worked to a point, but it was limited and slow to build, and about 40% of reasons still came back classified as "unknown." That gap was expensive: when the reason was captured, the saves teams retained a meaningful share of those customers (the mature retention teams ran 20 to 35%). We were losing savable customers for the simple reason that we could not reliably read why they were leaving.

How I framed it

Study the landscape, then fix what "cancel" even means.

Before designing anything, I ran a competitive teardown of how leading assistants handle cancellation, from Bank of America's Erica to Amazon to Capital One's Eno. Four principles carried into the design: disambiguate before acting, ask for the reason with empathy, never leave a dead-end, and prefer a soft, respectful retention offer over a hard stop.

The first real design problem was disambiguation. "Cancel payroll" is genuinely ambiguous: it could mean the Payroll subscription, a single payroll run, or a one-off payment to a worker. Defaulting to "cancel the subscription" would be the most expensive possible mistake, so I designed the flow to confirm what the customer actually meant before it did anything irreversible.

The build

I shipped it in two phases, on purpose.

Phase 1: prove customers will tell us why. The assistant had never asked for a reason, and an LLM cannot classify a reason it never hears. So before building any classification, I embedded a single, natural, empathetic question ("We're sad to see you go. Can you tell me why you're cancelling today?") and shipped just that, to learn whether customers would answer or bristle. About 80% offered a reason. The win was in keeping it short, human, and non-defensive, not in interrogating anyone.

Phase 2: read the reason and route on it. With reasons coming in, I handed each one to an LLM to classify the reason and sentiment and return a savability judgment. I worked with the analytics and PM partners to define savability itself, which reasons a trained specialist could realistically act on, then wrote the LLM system instructions and built the routing in Intuit's low-code conversation platform: the intents, the classification, the scripted fallbacks, the rules that sent savable customers to a specialist and everyone else to a clean cancel path, and the tests. A conversation designer owning the full stack, with minimal engineering. Moving off the old NLU model lifted reason classification from about 60% to 96% and cut model development from six-plus months to days.

Wiring a model to a routing decision raised the bar, because the errors are not equal. A false "savable" wastes a leaving customer's time and risks feeling like a trap; a false "not savable" quietly loses someone we could have kept. So I treated the two as different costs when I tuned and tested it, not just one headline accuracy number, and I designed it as an offer rather than a trap: the clean path to cancel was always one step away, never buried.

Before: assume & instruct
I want to cancel my subscription.
To cancel your subscription, go to Settings → Billing → Cancel. Your access ends at the end of the billing period.
After: ask, read, route
We're sad to see you go, and we really value your feedback. Can you tell me why you're cancelling today?
I haven't really used the product yet.
I understand. I'm connecting you with a specialist who can help. One moment please.

The outcome

It shipped, and it was accurate enough to route a revenue-critical decision.

~80%of customers offered a reason when the assistant asked
96.6%accuracy identifying savable cancellations
60% → 96%reason-classification rate, NLU to GenAI
~$2.9Mprojected retention revenue, 3 months

The experience shipped to production. The customers it flagged as savable were routed to the Saves and Retention team, who retained them at rates they tracked week over week, and that team's analysts projected about $2.9M in retention revenue over three months. My piece sat at the top of that funnel: turning cancellations the old system could not read into accurately classified, routable reasons. The saves conversion and the dollar model belonged to the retention team; the reason-capture and routing that fed them were mine.

What I'd carry forward

The unlock was the question, not the model.

Two lessons stuck. The first: the unlock was never the classifier. It was getting customers to answer at all, and a short, human question did more than a smarter model could have. The second came from working next to the analytics team: anchor a design's value in the metric that pays for it, retained revenue, not only the metric I could move directly, classification accuracy. The model was the easy part. Knowing what "good" was worth, and asking in a way people would answer, was the work.

The transferable principle

Cancellations and downgrades are where any subscription business keeps or loses a customer. Reading the real reason, responding with empathy, and routing to the right help without ever feeling like a trap is the conversation every subscription product has to get right at the edge of churn.
