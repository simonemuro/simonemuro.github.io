Cancellation Saves: teaching the system to hear why customers leave

A customer who says "I want to cancel" is not one problem — they're a dozen, and only some of them are losses you have to accept. This is the story of teaching a support system to hear the difference: first with the tools of 2022, then with a prompt that beat months of NLU work in hours — and an honest account of what shipped when.

Role
Lead conversation designer & prompt engineer
Surface
The voice assistant; pattern later shipped across voice and chat
What I did
Routing design, savability criteria, classifier prompt, JSON schema, evaluation
Outcome
12% saves vs 5% target · 96.6% classifier accuracy

December 2022: route on the reason, not the request

Cancellation was one of the highest-volume reasons customers called, and the flow treated every request identically: hand over the cancel steps, let them go. The reason — the one piece of signal that decides whether a customer is savable — was spoken out loud on every call and acted on in none of them.

I designed the savable-cancellation routing for the voice assistant. The flow disambiguated the cancel intent, probed for the reason, and split the path: customers leaving for price, switching products, or not using the product — the three reasons the saves team validated as genuinely winnable — were routed to dedicated retention experts with the case context pre-populated, so nobody repeated themselves. Everyone else got a clean, respectful cancel path. No dark patterns, no hostage negotiation — if the business closed, we helped them leave well.

Before → after · reconstructed
I want to cancel, it's gotten too expensive.
BEFORE — "Here's how to cancel: go to Settings → Billing → Cancel subscription…" same answer for every reason · a savable customer, gone
AFTER — "I hear you — I don't want cost to be the reason you go. I can connect you with a specialist who can review your plan and pricing before anything changes. Want me to do that?" reason heard · routed to a retention specialist who can actually fix price · context attached

Dialogue reconstructed from the shipped flow; production copy is confidential. The design is real: hear the reason, judge savability, route with context — and keep the cancel path one step away.

It worked, and we measured it. Across 8,814 cancellation calls from December 2022 through March 2023, the flow saved 12% of customers against a 5% target. Calls where the reason was price — the reason a specialist has the most room to fix — saved at 43%.

The ceiling: a model that couldn't hear

The routing was only as good as the classification feeding it, and the NLU-era intent model had a hard ceiling. It returned "unknown" on roughly 40% of cancellation reasons — no trained intent matched the real, messy ways people explain why they're leaving. And when it did produce a label, it was right about 80% of the time, so downstream routing inherited both the blindness and the errors. Meanwhile the saves teams kept 20–35% of customers when they knew the real reason. Every "unknown" was money left on the table, and training new intents took months per iteration.

May–June 2023: replace the intent model with a prompt

So I prompt-engineered a GenAI savability classifier. The design was the prompt: a tightly specified classifier persona — a clear role, a precise task, and the rules it had to hold — emitting strict JSON (intent, reason, sentiment, savability) that was never read by a human; it was parsed downstream to make the routing call. The few-shot examples came from real customer language, in all its mess. And the iteration loop was the one that actually moves accuracy: find the failure, add the example that kills that class of error, re-test the whole set.

roleA cancellation-reason classifier for subscription support.
taskRead the customer's stated reason. Classify intent, reason, and sentiment; decide whether a specialist could still help.
rulesClassify only what the customer actually said. Never invent a reason; if unclear, return unknown instead of guessing.
output{ intent, reason, sentiment, savability } — strict JSON, parsed downstream to make the routing decision. No prose, ever.
few-shot"It's just too expensive right now" → reason: price · savability: true
"I sold the business" → reason: no longer needed · savability: false

I tested it the way I'd want anyone to test a claim of mine: across ~300 real utterances, a sample sized for 90% confidence. The classifier hit 96.6% savability accuracy, lifted the share of cancellations resolved to a known reason from 60% to 96%, and produced zero hallucinations and zero toxicity across the test set. Estimated run cost: about $756 a year. And the dev-time story might be the biggest number of all: what took months of NLU training per iteration collapsed into hours of prompt work.

The part I didn't get to ship — and what happened instead

Here's the honest twist: the classifier was blocked from production. The deployment path it needed wasn't approved platform infrastructure yet, and I wasn't going to route a revenue-critical decision through an unapproved one. I didn't fake my way around it. I documented the proof — the accuracy, the test methodology, the cost — and made the case.

Blocked in 2023. Shipped in FY25.

When the platform caught up, the GenAI classification pattern shipped through approved channels, across voice and chat — and the Responsible AI approval pattern I helped establish became the team standard for LLM classification use cases.

I won't claim the 2023 prototype shipped as-is, because it didn't. What I'll claim is what the evidence supports: I proved the pattern before the platform could carry it, kept the proof rigorous enough to survive scrutiny, and the production version that followed stood on it.

What this proves

Three things. First, problem-first prompt craft under a real business constraint — the prompt wasn't a demo, it was a routing decision with asymmetric costs on each kind of error. Second, evaluation rigor: every number on this page was measured, and the one famous number that wasn't defensible isn't here. Third, the pattern generalizes — fraud triage, escalation, any problem shaped like read the messy human reason, then route the decision is won the same way: a tightly ruled classifier, structured output, and an eval you'd stake the routing on.
